## Overview
This journal article (published in *Wear*, 2019) systematically audits the quality of experimental design in tribology research. A team of 26 reviewers evaluated 379 papers from the top five tribology journals (2017 output) against criteria drawn from medical and social-science research-integrity literature: randomisation of samples and tests, blinding, control tests, repeated measurements, statistical analysis, and data/code sharing. The study finds that most tribology papers fall far short of basic scientific rigour, and it closes with concrete recommendations for researchers and group leaders.

## Unique contribution
First systematic, field-wide audit of experimental-design quality specifically in tribology. Unlike prior commentary, it provides hard prevalence numbers from a large sample (379 papers, 26 reviewers) and introduces the Normalised Mean Difference (NMD) as a proxy effect-size measure when full statistics are absent. It also proposes a step-by-step statistical design workflow tailored to tribological experiments.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- **Sample:** 379 unique papers from *Wear*, *Tribology International*, *Tribology Transactions*, *Tribology Letters*, *Journal of Tribology* (2017); 290 classified as experimental.
- **Key prevalence findings:** blinding 2.2%, sample randomisation 3.2%, test randomisation 1.2%, ≥3 repeats 30%, statistical tests 4.4%, full data given ~low single digits.
- **Control tests** had the highest prevalence (~64%) but also the worst inter-rater agreement, reflecting definitional ambiguity.
- Median study: NMD ≈ 0.54, sample size n = 1 — making false-positive rates unquantifiable but likely high.
- Ball-on-flat COV between labs is ~49%; even within a single lab ~23% — meaning 21 tests would be needed to estimate a mean with 5% standard error.
- Recommended workflow: (1) pre-specify the outcome variable, (2) choose minimum effect size of interest and study power, (3) compute required n via power analysis, (4) randomise and blind where possible, (5) run pre-specified statistics only after data collection, (6) publish regardless of outcome and share all data and code.
- Lowering the significance threshold from p < 0.05 to p < 0.005 is advocated, consistent with broader replication-crisis literature.
