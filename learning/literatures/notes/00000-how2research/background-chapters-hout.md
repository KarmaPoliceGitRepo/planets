## Overview
A concise pedagogical handout (8 pages) from a thesis-writing workshop by Brian Paltridge. It synthesises guidance on how to structure and write the background/literature-review chapters of a research thesis, covering what to include, how it differs by degree level (BA through PhD), how to locate and critically read literature, how to report on previous research, and relevant linguistic strategies (tense choices, hedging, paraphrasing). Supplementary reading and online resource lists are included.

## Unique contribution
Provides a practitioner-friendly one-stop reference for thesis writers, integrating frameworks from multiple established sources (Phillips & Pugh, Boote & Beile, Cone & Foster, Hyland) into a single structured handout. The Boote & Beile doctoral-literature-review standards table and the linguistic-strategy taxonomy (hedges, boosters, attitude markers, engagement markers, self-mentions) are particularly useful compact references.

## Key concepts
- Background theory / focal theory / previous research (three-layer model)

- Paraphrasing and summarising

## Methods / results / takeaways
- At PhD level, the literature review must achieve analytical synthesis across all known literature, including sources in other languages, with high-level conceptual linking and critical evaluation of philosophical traditions.
- Five core elements every review must address: key issues underpinning the project; major findings (by whom and when); main viewpoints and controversies; critical evaluation of strengths and weaknesses; and state-of-the-art conclusions plus the remaining gap.
- Boote & Beile (2005) five-category rubric: Coverage, Synthesis, Methodology, Significance, Rhetoric — each with specific criteria that distinguish adequate from outstanding doctoral reviews.
- Locate literature strategy: identify key authors and journals, use state-of-the-art review articles, search Google Scholar, scan Tables of Contents, follow reference chains, read primary sources only (avoid popular press).
- Reporting tense conventions: simple present for generalisations and accepted facts; simple past for references to a specific single study; present perfect for general areas of ongoing investigation.
- Linguistic strategies (Hyland 2004): hedges (might, perhaps, possible) reduce commitment; boosters (in fact, clearly) assert certainty; attitude markers (unfortunately, surprisingly) signal author stance.
- Paraphrasing techniques: change words (synonyms), change word class, change word order — must be combined to avoid plagiarism.
