## Overview

A first-year undergraduate mechanical engineering textbook covering the core engineering science disciplines in six self-contained units: solid mechanics, materials and processing, fluid dynamics, thermodynamics, electrical and electronic systems, and machine dynamics. Written by eight University of Nottingham academics, the book is deliberately concise and focused on fundamentals, accompanied by a companion website and a parallel mathematics text. The opening units (solid mechanics and materials) are substantively present in the extracted text; later units are summarised from the table of contents.

## Unique contribution

Provides a balanced multi-disciplinary introduction in a single volume, covering mechanical, materials, fluid, thermal, electrical, and dynamics topics at the same introductory level. The multi-author format ensures discipline-specific depth within a unified pedagogical style. The explicit unit structure makes it suitable for modular taught courses, and the worked-example approach (forces, moments, couples, beam bending, Mohr's circle) mirrors standard UK undergraduate assessment style.

## Key concepts

**Unit 1 — Solid Mechanics (Richard Brooks)**
- Forces, moments, and couples; Newton's laws; static equilibrium
- Free-body diagrams; force resolution into components
- Stress, strain, and Hooke's law (elasticity)
- Young's modulus, Poisson's ratio
- Shear stress and shear strain
- Beam bending: bending moment diagram, shear force diagram
- Second moment of area; bending stress (σ = My/I)
- Multi-axial stress and strain; principal stresses; Mohr's circle
- Torsion: shear stress in circular shafts (τ = Tr/J), polar second moment of area

**Unit 2 — Materials and Processing (Andrew Kennedy & Philip Shipway)**
- Crystal structure, atomic bonding; microstructure
- Mechanical properties: strength, hardness, toughness, ductility
- Material selection for engineering design
- Materials processing: casting, forming, machining, joining
- Failure modes: fatigue, fracture, wear, corrosion

**Unit 3 — Fluid Dynamics (Stephen Pickering)**
- Hydrostatics: pressure, buoyancy
- Bernoulli's equation; continuity equation
- Viscous flow; Reynolds number; laminar vs. turbulent flow
- Linear momentum equation for fluids

**Unit 4 — Thermodynamics (Paul Shayler)**
- First law of thermodynamics: work, heat, internal energy
- Second law: heat engines, Clausius inequality, entropy, irreversibility
- Perfect gas properties; steam and water properties
- Thermodynamic processes; heat transfer (conduction, convection, radiation)
- Power cycles: Carnot, Rankine, Brayton

**Unit 5 — Electrical and Electronic Systems (Alan Howe)**
- DC circuits: Ohm's law, Kirchhoff's laws
- Electromagnetic systems: inductance, transformers, motors
- Capacitance and RC circuits
- AC circuits: phasors, impedance, power factor
- Three-phase circuits
- Semiconductor rectifiers; amplifiers; digital electronics
- AC induction motors

**Unit 6 — Machine Dynamics (Stewart McWilliam)**
- Kinematics of particles and rigid bodies in a plane
- Linkage mechanisms
- Mass properties; moments of inertia
- Dynamics of rigid bodies; Newton-Euler equations
- Balancing of rotating masses
- Geared systems; gear ratios; efficiency
- Work-energy methods; impulse-momentum

## Methods / results / takeaways

- The book opens with Newton's third law and vector force resolution as the foundation for all subsequent static and dynamic analysis.
- Moment of a force = F × d (perpendicular distance); a couple creates pure rotation without net translation — a concept that recurs in torsion and machine dynamics.
- Bending stress in a beam is linearly proportional to distance from the neutral axis: σ = My/I, where M is the bending moment, y the distance, and I the second moment of area.
- The second law of thermodynamics defines the upper bound on heat engine efficiency (Carnot efficiency); real cycles (Rankine, Brayton) fall below this due to irreversibilities.
- Volumetric flow rate Q = Av (continuity); Bernoulli shows the trade-off between pressure and velocity in ideal incompressible flow — the basis for many flow measurement devices.
- AC induction motors are the dominant industrial motor type; speed is determined by supply frequency and number of pole pairs, with slip under load.
- The book consistently emphasises SI units and dimensional consistency as non-negotiable engineering practice.
