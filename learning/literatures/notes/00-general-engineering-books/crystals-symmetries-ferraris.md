## Overview

A self-described review (not original research) of how crystal symmetry constrains the physical properties of anisotropic crystalline materials. The slides were presented at a theoretical crystallography and materials science workshop. The content progresses from anisotropy vs. isotropy, through tensor representations of physical properties, to worked examples of specific properties (pyroelectricity, piezoelectricity, elasticity) and how crystal point-group symmetry reduces the number of independent tensor components.

## Unique contribution

Provides a concise, unified tensor framework linking crystal symmetry (point groups) to the number of independent components in physical property tensors of any rank. The Neumann principle is used as the organising rule. Worked reductions for second-, third- and fourth-rank tensors across all crystal systems (triclinic through cubic) are tabulated. The treatment of site symmetry vs. crystal symmetry for microscopic properties is a useful clarification.

## Key concepts

- Anisotropy vs. isotropy in crystalline materials
- Long-range order and crystal structure
- Neumann principle (symmetry of a physical property cannot be lower than the crystal point group)
- Tensor rank: scalar (rank 0), vector (rank 1), matrix/second-rank tensor, third-rank tensor, fourth-rank tensor
- Symmetrical vs. non-symmetrical tensors
- Ellipsoid representation of second-rank tensor properties
- Crystal point groups and the 7 crystal systems (triclinic, monoclinic, orthorhombic, tetragonal, trigonal, hexagonal, cubic)
- Reduction of tensor components by: (1) inherent property symmetry, (2) crystal symmetry constraints
- Polar vector vs. axial (pseudo) vector
- Pyroelectricity and spontaneous polarisation
- Pyroelectric point groups (10 acentric groups): 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, m, mm2, 3m, 4mm, 6mm
- Piezoelectricity: third-rank piezoelectric tensor d_ihk; 20 piezoelectric point groups (all acentric except 432)
- Stress tensor (σ_hk) and strain tensor (ε_ij) — both symmetric second-rank tensors
- Elasticity: compliance tensor s_ijhk (fourth-rank); 21 independent components in triclinic, 3 in cubic
- Site symmetry for microscopic properties (thermal displacement ellipsoids, electronic deformation)
- Wyckoff position and site symmetry

## Methods / results / takeaways

- A general second-rank tensor has 9 components; symmetry of the property reduces it to 6 (symmetric tensor); crystal symmetry further reduces it to 4 (monoclinic), 3 (orthorhombic), 2 (tetragonal/trigonal/hexagonal), 1 (cubic).
- The piezoelectric tensor d_ihk starts with 27 components → reduced to 18 (σ_hk symmetric) → further reduced by crystal point group (e.g. down to 1 independent component in some hexagonal classes).
- The elastic compliance tensor s_ijhk starts at 81 → 36 (stress and strain symmetry) → 21 (additional symmetry s_ijhk = s_hkij) → crystal-system-specific (13 monoclinic, 9 orthorhombic, 3 cubic).
- Pyroelectricity requires a polar axis; only 10 point groups allow it; the number of nonzero polarisation components depends on specific group (e.g., only p3 nonzero for groups 3, 4, 6, mm2, etc.).
- Axial vectors have symmetry ∞/m (compatible with 13 point groups including centrosymmetric ones), unlike polar vectors (∞m, 10 groups).
- The key practical takeaway: the higher the crystal symmetry, the fewer the independent parameters needed to fully characterise a physical property.
