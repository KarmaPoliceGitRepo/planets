## Overview

A comprehensive graduate-level reference textbook on the thermodynamics, combustion, fluid mechanics, heat transfer, friction, and emissions of spark-ignition (SI) and compression-ignition (CI) internal combustion engines. Written by MIT Professor John Heywood, the book covers both fundamental theory and practical engine design, tracing the history of ICE development from Otto (1876) and Diesel (1892) through modern turbocharged designs. It is structured in four major sections: engine fundamentals, combustion and working fluids, gas exchange and power cycles, and emissions.

## Unique contribution

This is widely regarded as the definitive reference text for ICE thermodynamics and combustion engineering. Its unique value lies in the rigorous integration of thermodynamic cycle analysis, detailed combustion chemistry (including chemical equilibrium and reaction kinetics), in-cylinder fluid motion, heat transfer, friction, and pollutant formation — all tied to real measured engine data. The extensive treatment of fuel-air mixture thermodynamics (including enthalpy charts for burned and unburned mixtures) and the availability analysis of engine processes are particularly thorough.

## Methods / results / takeaways

- Heywood organises engine performance using dimensionless parameters (mean effective pressure, volumetric efficiency, etc.) that scale across engine families.
- The ideal Otto cycle gives thermal efficiency η = 1 − r_c^(1−γ), where r_c is compression ratio and γ is the ratio of specific heats; real engines fall well below this due to heat transfer, non-ideal combustion timing, and gas-exchange losses.
- Volumetric efficiency is the key metric for breathing performance; it depends on valve timing, port geometry, and gas dynamics (wave tuning).
- Flame propagation in SI engines is turbulent premixed combustion; burn duration strongly affects cycle-to-cycle variability and knock margin.
- NOx formation follows the Zeldovich mechanism and is highly temperature-sensitive — reducing peak temperature (EGR, retarded ignition timing) is the primary lever.
- Diesel combustion is mixing-controlled; injection pressure, spray penetration, and air swirl govern burn rate and soot/NOx trade-off.
- Turbocharging improves specific power with modest efficiency gain; intercooling reduces charge temperature and improves knock margin in SI or air density in CI.
- Historical note: tetraethyl lead (antiknock, 1923) allowed steady compression-ratio increases; its elimination drove renewed interest in combustion chemistry and engine management.
