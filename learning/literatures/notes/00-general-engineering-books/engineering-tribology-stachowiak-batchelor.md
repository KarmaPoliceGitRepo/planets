## Overview
A comprehensive textbook on engineering tribology covering the physical, chemical, and mechanical aspects of friction, wear, and lubrication for engineering applications. The book is structured around lubrication regimes — hydrodynamic, hydrostatic, elastohydrodynamic, boundary, and extreme-pressure — with additional chapters on lubricant properties and composition, wear mechanisms, surface engineering, and computational methods. It is widely used as a graduate-level reference and teaching text.

## Unique contribution
This text distinguishes itself by combining rigorous analytical treatment (Reynolds equation derivations, Hertz contact mechanics, EHL film thickness formulae) with practical engineering guidance (bearing design charts, additive selection, viscosity classification). The second edition adds computational hydrodynamics (finite-difference solutions, Vogelpohl parameter method) and detailed treatment of thermohydrodynamic and elastohydrodynamic effects, making it a bridge between theory and design practice. Coverage of non-Newtonian lubricant rheology and EHL traction is particularly thorough.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Reynolds equation is derived from first principles (equilibrium of a fluid element plus continuity of flow) and then simplified for pad, journal, squeeze-film, and porous bearing configurations.
- Load capacity, friction force, coefficient of friction, and flow rate are derived analytically for standard geometries; results are presented both as closed-form expressions and design charts.
- EHL film thickness is characterized by dimensionless speed, materials, load, and ellipticity parameters; four lubrication regimes are identified depending on whether surfaces are rigid/elastic and lubricant is isoviscous/piezoviscous.
- Flash temperature at the conjunction (asperity level) is computed for circular, square, and line contacts and shown to affect EHL film stability and lubricant degradation.
- Boundary lubrication is explained through an adsorption model: at low loads/temperatures physisorption of fatty acids dominates; at higher loads chemisorption and sacrificial EP films (sulfur, phosphorus compounds) are required.
- The Vogelpohl parameter method enables efficient finite-difference solution of the Reynolds equation, avoiding numerical singularities; applied to grooved 360° journal bearings and vibration stability analysis.
- Grease lubrication mechanism is described as bleed of base oil from the thickener matrix; consistency (penetration) and drop point are the key classification properties.
