## Overview
This book bridges tribology — the science of interacting surfaces in relative motion — with the field of abrasive machining processes (grinding, honing, lapping, polishing, superfinishing). The authors argue that abrasive machining has historically been treated empirically ("more art than science") and use tribological principles to provide a rigorous, predictive framework. Major chapters address kinematics of abrasive contacts, contact mechanics, friction/force/energy, heat transfer and thermal damage, process fluids, wheel topography, dressing, and workpiece materials, closing with molecular dynamics simulation of abrasive events.

## Unique contribution
Unlike conventional machining or tribology texts, this work treats abrasive wear as simultaneously useful (material removal from workpiece) and detrimental (tool wear). It applies the full tribosystem concept — bonded abrasive, loose abrasive, and dressing sub-systems — to quantify grinding process outputs. The contact mechanics chapter challenges the traditional assumption of uniform energy distribution over the nominal contact area, showing that actual power density can be less than half the conventional estimate, with direct implications for thermal damage predictions.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- The book uses an analogy between tribological parameters and technological parameters for grinding (originated by Marinescu in a 1982 CIRP paper) as its unifying framework.
- Kinematic analysis shows that grain distribution is non-uniform, which affects chip thickness, forces, and surface finish in ways that uniform-distribution models miss.
- Contact mechanics reveals the actual contact area in precision grinding differs substantially from the nominal (geometric) contact, reducing effective power density — a critical correction for thermal analysis.
- Thermal analysis defines "permissible domains" of operation: creep grinding (deep cuts, low workspeeds), conventional grinding (shallow cuts, moderate speeds), and high-efficiency deep grinding (deep cuts, very high removal rates).
- The book covers external and internal cylindrical grinding, surface grinding, and loose abrasive processes (lapping), showing how G-ratio affects both removal rate and depth of cut.
- Practical industrial problems addressed: poor accuracy, poor surface quality, rapid wheel wear, vibrations, workpiece burn, and high costs.
