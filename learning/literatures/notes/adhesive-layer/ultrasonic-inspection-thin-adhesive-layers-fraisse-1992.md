## Overview
This Journal of Applied Physics paper develops a theoretical framework for characterizing adhesive layers whose thickness is negligible compared to the ultrasonic wavelength. It introduces an interfacial transfer function (ITF) formalism that bridges two existing modeling traditions — treating the interface as a thin homogeneous layer versus allowing stress/displacement jumps at the boundary — and shows they are equivalent in the long-wavelength limit. Experiments on a curing epoxy layer (~5 µm) between glass plates confirm the model by extracting evolving viscoelastic properties from reflection coefficient measurements.

## Unique contribution
The paper proves that the spring-mass "imperfect interface" model (Baik & Thompson) and the exact multilayer transfer-matrix calculation converge for layers much thinner than the wavelength, and derives explicit conditions for this equivalence. It then uses this unified model to extract time-resolved complex stiffness (K* in both shear and longitudinal modes) from a 5 µm epoxy layer during polymerization, achieving results in excellent quantitative agreement with bulk measurements of the same adhesive.

- Interfacial transfer function (ITF)
- Imperfect boundary conditions / spring-mass interface model
- Reflection coefficient (shear and longitudinal modes)
- Acoustic impedance mismatch
- Viscoelastic adhesive cure monitoring
- Long-wavelength (thin-interface) approximation
- Specific stiffness (K*)
- Zener rheological model
- Gel point detection
- Lame coefficients in complex (lossy) media

## Methods / results / takeaways
- **Setup:** shear and longitudinal piezoelectric transducers on one glass plate; 200 MHz digitizer; 10-point averaging; reference signal stored before bonding.
- **Model:** the ITF S is a 2×2 matrix linking displacement and stress states across the interface. For a homogeneous thin layer K* = A*/d and m = ρd; combining these with Brekhovskikh's multilayer result shows identical R formulas when k*d < 1.
- **Gradient interfaces:** the model extends to continuously varying properties by integrating dm and dK* across the layer thickness.
- **Experimental findings:**
  - Shear-mode reflection is far more sensitive to cure evolution than longitudinal mode because the relative change in shear velocity is much larger.
  - Real part of K* converges across frequencies (as expected for an elastic contribution), while imaginary part scales with frequency — consistent with a Zener viscoelastic solid.
  - Loss tangent (K"/K') reaches unity at ~53 min, marking the gel point, consistent with bulk observations at 35°C.
  - Layer thickness (measured by SEM) ≈ 5.9 µm ± 0.2 µm; derived elastic constants agree with bulk values to within measurement uncertainty dominated by thickness error.
- **Practical implication:** the technique is directly applicable to fast-curing adhesives (cyanoacrylates) and thin-film systems; attenuation inversion is not required.
