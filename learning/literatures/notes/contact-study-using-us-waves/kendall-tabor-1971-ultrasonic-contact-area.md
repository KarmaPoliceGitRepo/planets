## Overview
A foundational Proceedings of the Royal Society paper that introduced the ultrasonic technique for measuring interface stiffness between contacting surfaces. The authors establish that the transmitted ultrasonic pulse amplitude is proportional to the *elastic stiffness* of the interface (not the true area of contact directly), derive the relationship between stiffness and contact geometry, and validate the technique on metals, glass, rubber, and polymers — both stationary and sliding.

## Unique contribution
First systematic demonstration that longitudinal ultrasonic wave transmission can quantitatively measure interface stiffness, and that this stiffness depends on the *linear dimension* (radius) of individual contacts, not their area — analogous to electrical spreading resistance. This insight immediately signals the fundamental ambiguity: the same area of contact arranged differently produces different stiffnesses. The paper also documents junction growth in soft metals during sliding as detected ultrasonically.

## Key concepts
- Surface stiffness S = dW/dw (load per displacement increment)
- Acoustic impedance and wave transmission coefficient T
- Quasi-static spring model of incomplete interface
- Multiple contact interactions (stiffness vs. contact distribution)
- Plastic vs. elastic contact deduction from stiffness-load slope
- Junction growth in sliding ductile metals
- Hertzian contact (elastic spheres/cylinders)
- Transmission across air gaps (Fabry-Perot analysis)
- Oxide films and their negligible effect on acoustic transmission

## Methods / results / takeaways
- **Theory:** For a cylindrical punch of radius a into an elastic half-space, S ≈ k·E·a (k between ~1.9–2.4 for reasonable Poisson ratios). Stiffness is proportional to the contact *diameter*, not area — identical to the spreading-resistance result for electrical contacts.
- **Multiple contacts:** When N contacts are well separated, total S = N·S₀. When closely packed, S ≈ √N·S₀ (determined by the embracing circle). Only when true area is less than ~1/10 of apparent area do empty spaces markedly reduce S. This severely limits using S alone to recover true contact area.
- **Transmission formula:** For a constriction of stiffness S in a bar of cross-section A₀ at low transmission (T² << 1), T ≈ S/(ρc·A₀)·(ω/2π) — amplitude proportional to stiffness.
- **Air gap transmission:** Air gaps > ~10 nm thick transmit negligible acoustic energy at 200 kHz. Oil fills gaps and restores transmission. Oxide films of 50 nm have negligible effect.
- **Experimental results:** Crossed steel cylinders at Hertz loading — dry surfaces gave S = 3.1×10⁶ W^(1/2), oil-lubricated surfaces matched Hertzian prediction S = 5.8×10⁶ W^(1/3). The difference implies dry contact area is only ~1/10 of nominal. Smooth glass/glass follows Hertz; scratched glass shows plastic deformation (S ∝ W^(1/2)). PTFE shows plastic; rubber shows elastic contact.
- **Sliding contacts:** For glass-glass sliding, stiffness holds near static value then drops at the onset of surface scratching. For lead/indium on steel, stiffness *increases* during sliding — attributed to junction growth, with contact area A/A₀ scaling as (A/A₀)² − 1 ≈ a²·(friction coefficient)², consistent with McFarlane & Tabor (1950).
- **Limitation explicitly stated:** The method cannot distinguish true from apparent contact area without knowing the spatial distribution of contacts. Shorter wavelength (higher frequency) could improve discrimination but introduces diffraction complications.
