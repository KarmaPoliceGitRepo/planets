## Overview
A PhD thesis from the University of Sheffield (supervisor: Roger Lewis; co-supervisor: Rob Dwyer-Joyce) that develops ultrasonic reflectometry methods for real-time, in-situ measurement of both wear and contact pressure in dry machine element contacts. The work spans ultrasonic theory, apparatus design, and four application domains: pin-on-disk wear, metal-to-metal seals, wheel/rail contact patches, and industrial monitoring.

## Unique contribution
Extends the ultrasonic contact-measurement toolkit to *dry* sliding conditions and demonstrates *real-time* measurement capability. Two novel wear measurement methods are developed — time-of-flight (TOF) and resonant-dip frequency feature — and validated on a pin-on-disk rig with temperature compensation. An embedded ultrasonic array is applied to map 2D contact pressure distributions in rolling wheel/rail contacts at representative speeds.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- **Spring model:** At long wavelengths, a partially contacting interface behaves as a distributed spring with stiffness K. Reflection coefficient R (Eq. 2-11/2-12) depends on ω, z, and K. K is frequency-independent and serves as a proxy for contact pressure after calibration.
- **Wear measurement (TOF):** Pin length measured via zero-crossing time-of-flight between consecutive echoes. Temperature compensation using an internal reference notch eliminates thermal drift. Demonstrated on aluminium pins sliding on steel disks.
- **Wear measurement (frequency feature):** When echoes overlap (thin specimens), resonant dips in the FFT spectrum locate material thickness. Applied to monitor gradual wear in standard pin-on-disk tests.
- **Seal contact:** Ultrasonic arrays mapped contact pressure of end-fitting metal-to-metal seals under compression and under internal fluid pressure. Real-time pressure maps showed contact redistribution during pressurization.
- **Wheel/rail:** Static maps obtained with scanning immersion transducer agreed with Hertz theory. Dynamic array measurements embedded in the rail tracked the contact patch at speed; contact area and peak pressure matched theoretical predictions to within engineering accuracy.
- **Dynamic/dry sliding limitation:** During fast sliding, asperity contacts change too rapidly for the spring model to hold, making contact pressure extraction unreliable. At moderate conditions (PTFE, Cu on steel), stiffness drops ~80 % on onset of sliding.
- **Key hardware insight:** Pulse repetition frequency must exceed inverse contact duration for real-time tracking; array multiplexing enables 2D mapping without sacrificing temporal resolution.
