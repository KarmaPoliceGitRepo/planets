# Ultrasonic Scattering from Imperfect Interfaces: A Quasi-Static Model

- **Author/Year:** Jai-Man Baik and R. Bruce Thompson / 1984
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
A Journal of Nondestructive Evaluation paper that derives and validates the quasi-static spring (and spring-mass) model for the reflection and transmission of ultrasonic waves at an interface containing distributed pores, inclusions, or cracks. The paper establishes the theoretical underpinning — that in the long-wavelength limit the interface acts as a distributed spring with stiffness proportional to the area density of contacts — and provides the closed-form expressions widely cited in subsequent contact-measurement literature.

## Unique contribution
Provides the rigorous theoretical justification for the quasi-static spring model of imperfect interfaces: derives the reflection and transmission coefficients in terms of interfacial stiffness K (and mass if needed), validates against exact scattering solutions, and shows that the simpler spring approximation is accurate when the wavelength is large compared to scatterer size. This paper is the theoretical foundation referenced by Drinkwater et al. (1996), Krolikowski (1991), and the Brunskill thesis for justifying the spring model of contact measurement.

## Key concepts
- Quasi-static spring model (long-wavelength limit)
- Spring-mass interface model
- Reflection coefficient R and transmission coefficient T
- Interfacial stiffness K (spring stiffness per unit area)
- Interfacial mass (surface mass density)
- Pore/crack scatterer density and size
- Low-frequency (quasi-static) approximation validity criterion
- Exact scattering theory comparison
- Normal and tangential stiffness

## Methods / results / takeaways
- **Model derivation:** Interface with distributed pores/inclusions modelled as a planar spring layer. Boundary conditions replace continuity of displacement with a jump proportional to traction/stiffness. Reflection coefficient R = (iωZ/2K)/(1 + iωZ/2K) for identical materials (Z = acoustic impedance); identical to the expression used by Krolikowski, Drinkwater, and Brunskill.
- **Spring-mass extension:** Adding interfacial mass m per unit area introduces a −ω²m term, but is shown to be negligible for air-filled gaps (low density) or when gap thickness is small compared to wavelength — which covers most contact situations.
- **Validity criterion:** Quasi-static approximation is valid when ka << 1, where k = wavenumber and a = scatterer radius. Authors show excellent agreement with exact scattering solutions in this regime and divergence only when ka approaches 1.
- **Stiffness-geometry link:** For a dilute array of circular cracks of radius a at density n per unit area, K = E_eff·n·a (effective modulus times crack density times crack radius) — confirming the linear-dimension dependence identified by Kendall & Tabor (1971).
- **Practical significance:** Justifies using a single scalar K to characterize a complex rough-surface contact and explains why measuring K (via R) provides information about the overall interface compliance, not uniquely about true contact area. The paper is the key reference for validating the simplifying assumptions inherent in contact ultrasound reflectometry.
