# Propagation of Acoustic Waves in a 1D Array of Noncohesive Cylinders

- **Source:** Propagation of acoustic waves in a one-dimensional array of noncohesive cylinders - G Huillard (2011).pdf

- **Author/Year:** Guillaume Huillard, Xavier Noblin, Jean Rajchenbach / 2011
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
A Physical Review E paper studying how acoustic pulses propagate through a 1D chain of 36 polycarbonate cylinders. The authors use a photoelastic visualization technique combined with high-speed imaging to track stress in individual grains in real time. The central finding is that wave velocity follows a power law V ∝ F₀^β with two distinct regimes separated at a crossover force of ~20 N, and that surface imperfections at both micro- and macro-scales explain the non-ideal behaviour at low forces.

## Unique contribution
Direct spatiotemporal visualization of stress in each grain during pulse propagation using photoelastic photography — not just end-to-end timing. This enables simultaneous measurement of contact area evolution with confining force and unambiguous attribution of two velocity regimes to surface roughness/shape defects rather than packing disorder (since 1D geometry eliminates contact-recruitment effects).

## Key concepts
- Photoelastic visualization of granular contact
- Hertz contact law (spheres); logarithmic contact law (cylinders)
- Nonlinear wave propagation / Nesterenko solitary waves
- Spring-mass chain analogy for phonon propagation
- Contact area vs. confining force (power law)
- Solid friction as primary damping mechanism
- Coulomb friction coefficient from acoustic attenuation
- Surface roughness exponent β ≈ 1/4 (low force) vs 1/9–1/11 (high force)

## Methods / results / takeaways
- **Setup:** 36 polycarbonate cylinders (d = 13 mm) in a duraluminum frame; photoelastic imaging at up to 89,000 fps; tweeter-generated mechanical pulse.
- **Wave velocity regimes:** β ≈ 1/4 for F₀ < Fc ≈ 20 N; β ≈ 1/9 for rough surfaces and 1/11 for polished surfaces above Fc. The smoother case (1/11) matches the theoretical cylinder-contact prediction closely.
- **Contact area visualization:** Direct optical measurement shows contact area grows more steeply than the F₀^(1/2) ideal-cylinder law at low forces, converging to the ideal law above Fc.
- **Force-displacement relation:** Two-regime power law F₀ ∝ δ^α (α ≈ 1.78 at small δ; α ≈ 1.37 at larger δ), consistent with velocity transitions.
- **Damping:** Pulse amplitude decays linearly with distance, independently of lateral confining force F₀ but proportional to vertical load F⊥. This matches a Coulomb dry-friction model (μ ≈ 0.28), identifying wall friction as the dominant dissipation source. Polymer viscoelasticity is a secondary contribution.
- **Practical insight:** Deviations from the ideal (β = 1/6 Hertz or 1/11 cylinder) are caused by macro-scale shape imperfections and micro-roughness of grains — not by 3D packing disorder. Polishing reduces but does not fully eliminate the anomalous β.
