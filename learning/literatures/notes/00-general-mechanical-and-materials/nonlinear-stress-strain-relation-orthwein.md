## Overview
A theoretical mechanics paper proposing a new nonlinear constitutive equation for isotropic, homogeneous elastic materials that bridges the gap between classical linear elasticity and empirical observations. The relation accounts for the apparent Poisson's ratio variation observed experimentally, the Poynting effect in torsion, the effect of hydrostatic stress on shear strain, and the reduced stiffness under combined tension + hydrostatic pressure. It satisfies Truesdell's seven admissibility conditions for constitutive equations and reduces to classical linear elasticity for small stresses.

## Unique contribution
Derives a three-dimensional nonlinear stress-strain relation expressed in general curvilinear tensor form (strain as function of stress tensor and its invariants) that unifies several experimentally observed nonlinear phenomena under a single framework requiring only three additional constants (α, γ, η) beyond E and ν. Connects Ludwik's and MacGregor's empirical uniaxial formulas to Truesdell's mathematical requirements. Also demonstrates that classical torsion stress fields break down in the finite deformation regime and provides the correct stress fields including the Poynting effect.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Classical linear theory reviewed and shown to violate mass conservation for finite deformations (rod cross-section goes to zero before fracture when ν = 0.5 and σ = E/ν).
- Green–St. Venant and Alamansi–Hamel nonlinear strain measures examined; both fail to improve agreement with experiment when combined with linear stress-strain relations.
- Logarithmic strain: agrees with experiment for 1D but neglects Poisson's ratio and has no satisfactory 3D extension (prior criticism acknowledged in literature).
- Proposed relation: e_ij = (1+ν)/E × σ_ij − ν/E × J₁δ_ij + (γ−η)J₁σ_ij + αJ₁²δ_ij + ησ_ij²·σ_ij (symbolic; see paper for full tensor expression).
- Uniaxial tension: ratio of lateral to longitudinal strain (effective Poisson's ratio V′) varies with stress level in agreement with experimental data for aluminium and titanium alloys (Goodman 1953).
- Hydrostatic + shear: additional shear strain = 2η(r·τ)² where η ≈ 10⁻¹⁶ in⁴/lb² — only significant under extreme hydrostatic loads (Bridgman effect confirmed).
- Combined tension + hydrostatic: longitudinal strain decreases as hydrostatic pressure increases, consistent with Chalupnik & Ripperger static tests.
- Torsion: correct stress fields include σ_rr, σ_θθ, σ_zz, σ_rz components; linearised result shows Poynting effect (rod elongates under torque) and high compressive stress along twist axis (Davis effect).
- Constants α, γ, η determined from uniaxial tension + shear tests only — five phenomena explained with only three extra parameters.
