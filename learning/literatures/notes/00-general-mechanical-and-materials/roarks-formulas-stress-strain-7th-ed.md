## Overview
The definitive engineering reference for stress and strain formulas across virtually all structural geometries and loading conditions. First published by Raymond Roark in 1938 and now in its 7th edition, the book compiles closed-form analytical solutions, tabular formulas, and design charts for beams, frames, plates, shells, pressure vessels, elastic foundations, and more. It is used as a practitioner's lookup table as much as a textbook.

## Unique contribution
Unmatched breadth and depth of tabulated formulas — tables for shear, moment, slope, and deflection for dozens of beam configurations; reaction and deflection for frames under in-plane loading; formulas for beams on elastic foundations; plate bending under various edge conditions and loads; and extensive fatigue, fracture, and stress concentration data. Includes numerical methods overview (FEM, BEM, FDM) and experimental methods (strain gauge rosette equations). The 7th edition added material properties table, tensor transformation matrices, and finite element library.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Part 1 (Introduction): defines notation, units (Table 1.1: SI and USCS); multipliers for unit conversion (Table 1.3).
- Part 2 (Facts/Principles): Chapter 2 covers stress-strain relations in full tensor form; transformation equations and Mohr's circle; Chapter 3 covers material behaviour — plasticity, creep, fatigue, brittle fracture, stress concentration, elastic stability; Chapter 4 presents analytical method principles.
- Part 3 (Formulas): Chapter 7 tension/compression/shear; Chapter 8 beams (Table 8.1 covers standard support/load combinations with symbolic formulas for V, M, y, θ); Table 8.2 for in-plane frame reactions.
- Strain gauge rosette equations (Table 6.1) allow principal stress calculation from 3-gauge readings on any linear isotropic material.
- Practical gotcha: formulas assume linearly elastic behaviour unless stated otherwise; stress concentration factors apply at the point of concentration and are applied to nominal stress, not vice versa.
