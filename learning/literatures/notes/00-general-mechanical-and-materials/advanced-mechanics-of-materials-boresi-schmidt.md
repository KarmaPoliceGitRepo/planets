## Overview
A graduate-level textbook bridging fundamental elasticity theory and practical engineering analysis of complex structural members. Targeted at advanced undergraduates and graduate students in aerospace, civil, and mechanical engineering. Two-semester depth of material. The 6th edition condensed the 5th edition, removed the finite element chapter (available separately on the publisher's website), and updated examples and problems.

## Unique contribution
Stronger theoretical foundation than introductory texts — derives results from first principles in three-dimensional elasticity (tensor notation), covering topics rarely found in undergraduate texts: curved beams, unsymmetric bending, shear centre, torsion of non-circular sections, plates and shells, contact stresses, and inelastic behaviour. Emphasises understanding limitations and applicability of methods.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Develops constitutive equations for isotropic elastic materials from symmetry arguments; generalises to orthotropic and anisotropic materials.
- Curved beam bending: radial stress σ_r is non-zero (unlike straight beams), and neutral axis shifts towards the centre of curvature — Winkler-Bach formula applies.
- Torsion of non-circular members: warping of cross-section requires Prandtl stress function solution; thin-walled closed sections solved by Bredt's q = T/(2A_enc) where A_enc is enclosed area.
- Failure criteria: von Mises (distortion energy) generally preferred for ductile metals; Mohr-Coulomb for brittle/granular materials.
- Inelastic analysis section covers elastic-perfectly-plastic and strain-hardening models; residual stresses after unloading.
- Practical note: the book's philosophy is "develop from basic principles so limitations are clear" — users are expected to understand when formulas break down, not just apply them.
