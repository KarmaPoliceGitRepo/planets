## Overview
A master's thesis investigating true stress–strain behaviour in the plastic range for fifteen engineering materials (six steels, six aluminium alloys, two nickel alloys, one brass), using the photo-grid technique with a 1/4-inch gauge length and instantaneous cross-sectional area measurement. The goal is to establish empirical formulas that cover the full plastic range, including the region from maximum load to fracture — which most theoretical formulas at the time failed to describe.

## Unique contribution
Applies the photo-grid technique (developed by Stastny) to obtain true stress–strain data without an extensometer in the plastic range. Introduces a two-segment approximation: Hollomon's power-law formula (σ = Kε^n) up to maximum load, and a new least-squares straight-line or exponential fit from maximum load to fracture. Uses an IBM 650 digital computer for least-squares regression — an early computational approach in materials testing. Covers a broad material matrix including 1012, 1020, 1042, 1095, 8620 steels, Crucible 1-B, 2024-O, 3003-O, 3004-O, 5052-O, 6061-O, 7075-O aluminium, yellow brass, Monel-R, and Inconel.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Photo-grid method: a fine grid is printed on the specimen surface and photographed under load; grid spacing changes reveal local area reduction, giving true instantaneous area from which true stress and strain are computed.
- Gauge length: 1/4 inch (vs. standard 2-inch gauge), enabling measurement in the highly localised necking region.
- Three specimens per material tested; data scatter documented.
- Post-maximum-load segment: empirical straight lines in most cases (two exceptions better fitted by exponential curves); the straight-line fits were regarded as evidence of data reliability since they are consistent with literature.
- Hollomon's formula gives a good fit in the pre-necking region; but no single theoretical formula adequately covers the post-maximum load behaviour — the thesis fills this gap empirically.
- Results tabulated for 15 materials: Hollomon K and n constants, plus post-necking slope parameters.
- Practical value: data usable for cold-forming simulations, metal forming tool design, and plasticity analysis where elastic range approximations are inadequate.
