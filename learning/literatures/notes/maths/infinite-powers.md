## Overview
A popular-science narrative of calculus history and ideas by Cornell mathematician Steven Strogatz. The book traces calculus from Archimedes' method of exhaustion through Newton and Leibniz to modern applications in medicine, physics, and technology. It is aimed at a general audience and emphasises intuition and storytelling over formalism.

## Unique contribution
Strogatz's "Infinity Principle" — slicing continuous problems into infinitely many infinitesimally small pieces, solving each, and reassembling — is presented as the unifying idea behind all of calculus, from areas under curves to differential equations governing disease spread and GPS.

## Key concepts
- Limit and infinity principle
- Integration as sum of infinitesimals (Riemann)
- Differentiation and rates of change
- Fundamental theorem of calculus
- Differential equations (Newton's laws, epidemiology)
- Fourier series (briefly)
- Historical development: Archimedes, Kepler, Newton, Leibniz, Euler

## Methods / results / takeaways
- The infinity principle is positioned as the philosophical core: every calculus problem involves taming infinity in a systematic way.
- Accessible historical vignettes explain why mathematicians needed calculus and how it transformed science.
- Covers applications: GPS (relies on relativistic corrections via ODE solutions), HIV drug timing, medical imaging.
- The book avoids most symbolic manipulation, relying on diagrams and narrative.
