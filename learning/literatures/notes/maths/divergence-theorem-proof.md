## Overview
A concise, pedagogically-motivated proof of the Divergence (Gauss's) Theorem, starting from first principles via the concept of fluid flux. The approach derives the surface integral definition of flux, explains the construction of surface integrals over curved surfaces, then proves the theorem by splitting a closed surface into top and bottom halves and reducing both sides to double integrals over the projected region.

## Unique contribution
Motivates the abstract statement of the Divergence Theorem through a physical flux argument before presenting the formal proof. The split-surface approach (top/bottom = two graphs z = f+(x,y) and z = f-(x,y)) is clearly explained, including the sign convention for the outward normal on each piece.

## Key concepts
- Flux of a vector field
- Surface integral
- Unit outward normal n
- Divergence theorem: integral of F.n dσ over closed surface = integral of div F dV
- Vertically simple surface
- Gauss's theorem

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Flux across a planar surface: Flux = A(Ω)(v · k) generalises to a surface integral for curved/non-horizontal surfaces.
- For a closed surface, top half has outward normal = upward normal; bottom half has outward normal = downward, introducing a sign change.
- The theorem reduces to three separate identities (one per component of F) each provable by the same technique.
- Extension to non-simple domains: subdivide and use cancellation of interior faces.
