## Overview
Summer school lecture slides on tensor representation of anisotropic physical properties of single crystals, aimed at geophysicists. Covers 2nd rank tensors (thermal conductivity, thermal expansion) and 4th rank tensors (elastic constants), transformation laws, Voigt notation, crystal symmetry effects on tensor form, and the Christoffel tensor for computing seismic velocities. Includes applications to quartz, olivine, calcite, and post-perovskite.

## Unique contribution
Combines tensor theory with real crystal physics data and crystal symmetry constraints. The table of physical properties by tensor rank (0 through 4) is a compact reference. The post-perovskite section covers a 2004 discovery of major geophysical significance.

## Key concepts
- 2nd rank tensor (thermal conductivity, thermal expansion, electrical conductivity)
- 4th rank tensor (elastic stiffness/compliance)
- Transformation law: T'_{ij} = A_{ik} A_{jl} T_{kl}
- Neumann's principle (crystal symmetry constrains tensor form)
- Voigt notation (6×6 matrix for 4th rank elastic tensor)
- Christoffel tensor T_{ik} = C_{ijkl} n_j n_l
- Seismic phase velocity from Christoffel eigenvalues
- Quasi-P, quasi-S1, quasi-S2 waves
- Post-perovskite MgSiO3 phase at D" (>120 GPa)
- Crystal Preferred Orientation (CPO) and seismic anisotropy

## Methods / results / takeaways
- A 2nd rank symmetric tensor has a geometric representation (quadric ellipsoid or hyperboloid) whose principal axes align with crystal symmetry directions.
- Neumann's principle: physical property symmetry must include all crystal point-group symmetry elements (can be higher, never lower).
- Voigt notation reduces 81-component 4th rank tensor to 36 (and 21 for triclinic) by exploiting stress/strain tensor symmetry; factors of 2 and 4 must be inserted for compliance.
- Christoffel tensor: three eigenvalues give qP, qS1, qS2 phase velocities; eigenvectors give particle motion directions. P and S are only quasi because particle motion is not exactly parallel/perpendicular to propagation in anisotropic media.
- Post-perovskite (MgSiO3 Cmcm phase) has strong seismic anisotropy; it is likely responsible for the observed anisotropy of the D" layer at the base of the mantle.
