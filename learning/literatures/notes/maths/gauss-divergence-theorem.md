## Overview
Concise lecture notes presenting Gauss's Divergence Theorem as a statement about flux and divergence, with four worked examples. The examples range from computing flux of x^3i+y^3j+z^3k over a hemisphere, to applying the theorem with a cylindrical shell domain, to a tetrahedron, and to the electric field of a point charge (requiring care at the singularity).

## Unique contribution
The electric field example is noteworthy: because the vector field is not continuous at the origin, the theorem cannot be applied to a ball around the origin. Instead the argument uses an annular region between two spheres plus the fact that div E = 0 in that region to conclude the outward flux is the same through both spheres.

## Key concepts
- Gauss's Divergence Theorem
- Flux (integral of F · n dS)
- Divergence of a vector field
- Closed surface, outward normal
- Point charge electric field singularity
- Annular region argument

## Methods / results / takeaways
- The theorem equates surface flux integral with volume integral of divergence: ∬∂S F·n dS = ∭S div F dV.
- When the field has a point singularity, apply the theorem to a region that excludes the singularity.
- For a point charge, div E = 0 away from the origin; flux is equal through any enclosing sphere.
- The four examples systematically develop skill in choosing the right domain and applying the formula.
