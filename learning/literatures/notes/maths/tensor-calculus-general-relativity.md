## Overview
MIT graduate course notes introducing tensors in the context of general relativity. Covers the geometric foundations: manifolds, vectors and one-forms (covectors), tensors as multilinear maps, covariant differentiation, and the metric tensor. The treatment is coordinate-free where possible, with indices introduced as a practical computational tool.

## Unique contribution
Explicitly motivated by GR's three key ideas: curved spacetime as a pseudo-Riemannian manifold, the strong equivalence principle (locally flat coordinates), and the Einstein field equations. This physical motivation is absent from purely mathematical treatments and makes the abstraction meaningful.

## Key concepts
- Pseudo-Riemannian manifold
- Coordinate-free tensor definition
- Contravariant (vector) and covariant (one-form) components
- Metric tensor g_{μν}
- Covariant derivative and Christoffel symbols
- Parallel transport
- Geodesics
- Riemann curvature tensor
- Einstein field equations (context)
- Strong equivalence principle

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Tensors are defined as multilinear maps on combinations of vectors and one-forms; components change predictably under coordinate transformations.
- The metric g_{μν} raises and lowers indices and defines inner products, lengths, and angles in curved spacetime.
- Covariant derivative ∇_μ V^ν = ∂_μ V^ν + Γ^ν_{μλ} V^λ accounts for the change of basis vectors in curved coordinates.
- Geodesics generalise straight lines: d^2x^μ/dτ^2 + Γ^μ_{νλ}(dx^ν/dτ)(dx^λ/dτ) = 0.
- The Riemann tensor measures non-commutativity of covariant differentiation (∇∇ - ∇∇ ≠ 0 in curved space).
