## Overview
A Physics Education journal paper by NPL's John Nunn describing how to measure Young's modulus using the impulse excitation technique: a rod is tapped axially, its longitudinal resonant frequency is captured with a microphone and computer sound card, and together with simple dimensional/mass measurements, the elasticity modulus is calculated from the relation E = 4ρL²f². The paper is aimed at school and university students and includes a systematic worked example of propagating measurement uncertainties to show which variable dominates error, guiding iterative instrument upgrades.

## Unique contribution
Offers a school-accessible, low-cost treatment of impulse excitation that explicitly walks through uncertainty budgeting step by step — showing, for example, that thickness measurement (not frequency) is the dominant error source for a rectangular bar. Provides free custom spectrum-analyser software. Also briefly describes a high-temperature adaptation (up to ~750 °C) using transverse modes, a ruby ball impactor, and ceramic waveguides.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Core equation: E = 4ρL²f² (from v = √(E/ρ) and λ = 2L for the fundamental longitudinal mode).
- For a rectangular bar: E = 4mLf²/(WT); for a circular rod: E = 16mLf²/(πd²).
- Thickness measurement contributes the largest uncertainty (±0.5 mm ruler gives ΔE_T ≈ 12 GPa on a 3 mm bar); switching to Vernier calipers (±0.1 mm) then a micrometer (±0.01 mm) progressively tightened results from 74 ± 13 GPa → 70 ± 3 GPa → 69.5 ± 0.3 GPa.
- Frequency uncertainty (sound card) typically <0.1% for most computers against a calibrated signal generator — not the limiting factor.
- Aluminium rectangular bar (~300 × 16 × 3 mm): resonant peak at 8537 Hz; final E ≈ 69.5 ± 0.25 GPa (micrometer + precise balance + better spectrometer).
- For high-temperature use, transverse modes (lower frequency for small specimens) replace longitudinal ones; excitation via ruby ball through a funnel and ceramic waveguides.
- Technique is sensitive enough to detect Young's modulus changes with temperature changes of a few degrees.
