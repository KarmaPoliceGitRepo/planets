## Overview
A theoretical investigation of how the normal-incidence longitudinal reflection coefficient from a thin layer embedded between two thicker media varies with frequency, layer thickness, and the acoustic impedances of all three materials. The paper establishes closed-form expressions for R at the layer resonance frequency and at the halfway frequency, uses these to categorise seven behavioural cases, and analyses the practical detectability of liquid and solid thin layers in adhesive-joint and diffusion-bond inspection contexts.

## Unique contribution
Provides exact analytical expressions (Equations 25 and 39) for the reflection coefficient at the resonance frequency and at the half-resonance frequency, which together fully define the shape of R(f). Shows conclusively that the sensitivity is greatest when the two half-space impedances are equal, and provides a systematic 7-case classification of all possible impedance relationships. Demonstrates the frequency–thickness scaling theorem and applies it to quantify when a CAA-process oxide layer (3.5 µm) becomes undetectable.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- At the resonance frequency of the layer R_res = (z₁ − z₃)/(z₁ + z₃), independent of the layer's own impedance — the layer is acoustically transparent.
- At half the resonance frequency R_half = (z₁z₃ − z₂²)/(z₁z₃ + z₂²).
- These two values fix the shape of the entire R(f) curve; the curve oscillates between R_res (at fres) and R_half (at fhalf) as frequency increases.
- Seven impedance cases are classified: the sign and magnitude of R_half − R_res determines whether R peaks or dips at fres.
- The reflection coefficient is a function of the frequency–thickness product, so thinner layers can be characterised by extrapolating data measured at lower frequencies.
- For a 1 µm silicone-fluid layer between glass (z₁ = z₃), R rises steeply from zero; 0.2 µm layer detectable at 50 MHz.
- For a glass/silicone/epoxy system the sensitivity is much lower because R at zero frequency is already 0.651 (glass–epoxy baseline).
- For a 5 µm porous aluminium oxide layer between aluminium and epoxy (a typical CAA adhesive joint), a 50% change in oxide longitudinal velocity causes only an 8% change in R at 100 MHz — at the margin of reliable detectability.
- Key practical limitation: thin layers in high-impedance-mismatch systems are hard to characterise with normal-incidence methods; oblique incidence or guided waves may be needed.
