## Overview
A key experimental and theoretical paper demonstrating that ultrasonic reflection can measure lubricant-film thickness across a wide range (nm to mm) in real bearing components. The paper presents two complementary models — a spring interface model for thin films (stiffness-dominated regime) and a continuum resonance model for thicker films — and validates them on static wedge films, EHD ball-on-flat contacts, and a rolling-element bearing.

## Unique contribution
First published demonstration of in-situ ultrasonic film-thickness measurement inside a running rolling-element bearing (6410 deep-groove ball bearing) and in an EHD ball-on-flat contact, with good agreement to Dowson-Higginson regression predictions. Provides a clear framework for choosing between the ToF method (> 40 µm), the resonance method (10–40 µm), and the spring model (< ~25 µm down to ~2 nm at 60 MHz), and plots operating limits explicitly in a single figure.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Reflection coefficient R from a fluid layer (same material either side): R = 1 / sqrt(1 + (K/πfz₀)²) where K = ρc²/h is the layer stiffness per unit area.
- Spring model valid for f < ½ f₁ (first resonance); continuum model valid for all thicknesses.
- EHD lubricant acoustic properties change dramatically with pressure: speed of sound rises from ~1450 m/s at ambient to ~3550 m/s at 0.8 GPa (2-4× increase), requiring correction via Jacobson-Vinet model.
- Liquid-wedge validation: spring-model and resonance-model results agree with shim-geometry predictions; slight overestimation attributed to residual water or shim deformation.
- Annular oil-film apparatus (eccentric cylinders): film-thickness profile matches theoretical geometric solution.
- EHD ball-on-flat (stationary disc, sliding ball, 50 MHz transducer): measured films 50–500 nm; ±20% repeatability; agreement with Dowson-Higginson theory is good.
- Rolling bearing (6410, 25 MHz transducer in water bath on outer raceway): measured film vs. EHD theory shows good correlation across loads 45–75 kN and speeds 48–375 RPM.
- Practical limits: ToF method requires h > 40 µm; resonance method 10 < h < 40 µm; spring model 2 nm < h < ~25 µm (at 60 MHz, R < 0.9 for upper bound, R > 0.1 for lower bound).
