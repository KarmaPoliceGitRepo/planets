## Overview
PhD thesis investigating ultrasonic reflection techniques for measuring oil film thickness and contact in tribological systems involving dissimilar materials — in particular contacts where one body is a low-modulus polymer (Perspex, PTFE, nitrile rubber) against a stiffer material (steel). The main novelty is extending ultrasonic film-thickness measurement to acoustically mismatched contacts where the conventional spring-model amplitude method fails, and also using Rayleigh (surface) waves to characterise soft contacts (o-rings, lip seals) remotely.

## Unique contribution
- Demonstrates that the **phase-shift method** (rather than the amplitude-only spring model) is necessary and valid for thin films (h < 40 µm) between acoustically dissimilar materials such as Perspex and steel.
- Maps oil film thickness profiles in the **iso-viscous elastohydrodynamic (I-EHL)** regime for a nitrile ball on Perspex disc, observing wedge-shaped constrictions and cavitation in the exit region; measured central film 1–6 µm, consistent with optical data.
- Uses **Rayleigh wave reflection** at a contact to characterise compliance of soft contacts (rubber O-rings) remotely — reflection coefficient decreases with increasing load; proposes this as an in-situ sensor approach.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- **Chapters 3–4** cover the full ultrasonic background: longitudinal, shear, and surface waves; acoustic impedance; spring model derivation; amplitude and phase-shift methods; calibration (liquid wedge, journal geometry, glass plate, digital piezoelectric translator).
- **Chapter 5** compares the amplitude spring model vs. the phase-shift model for three material pairs (Perspex/Perspex, Perspex/PTFE, Perspex/steel); the phase-shift method is shown valid for h < 40 µm where the amplitude model fails.
- **Chapter 6** measures I-EHL film thickness for a nitrile sphere on Perspex disc under various loads and speeds; profiles show the characteristic film constriction at the contact exit; film thickness 1–6 µm; somewhat lower than Hamrock-Dowson theory.
- **Chapter 7** uses Rayleigh wave wedge transducers to measure reflection at point and line contacts of rubber on hard surfaces; load increases cause reflection coefficient to decrease; approach applicable as a remote in-situ soft-contact sensor.
- Key experimental finding: water path (focus position) critically affects measured film thickness; measurement must be performed within the focal zone.
- Acoustic impedance of contacting materials measured in-situ using ToF and solid-solid and solid-liquid reflection techniques.
