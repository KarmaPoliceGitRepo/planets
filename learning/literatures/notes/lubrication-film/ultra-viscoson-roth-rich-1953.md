## Overview
A foundational 1953 paper from the Journal of Applied Physics introducing the Ultra-Viscoson: a continuous ultrasonic viscometer that uses a pulsed, resonantly-excited magnetostrictive strip to measure the viscosity × density product of fluids. The paper derives the full theory connecting the probe's exponential decay (attenuation coefficient α) and oscillation frequency (β) to the driving-point impedance of the surrounding viscoelastic medium.

## Unique contribution
First detailed analytical treatment of a magnetostrictive-strip ultrasonic viscometer. Derives the driving-point impedance for viscous, rigid, and general viscoelastic materials including thin-film bounded volumes, introduces the skin-depth concept for shear waves in liquids, and provides closed-form expressions for both the infinite-medium and bounded-film cases. Shows the instrument directly reads η × ρ in cP × g/cc from a simple analog electrical computation, and demonstrates real-time tracking of blood coagulation, polymerization, and film drying.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- The probe is a narrow magnetostrictive strip excited impulsively; particle displacement decays as e^(−αt) sin(βt) along the strip.
- For an unbounded viscous liquid: α ∝ (η·ρ·ω)^(1/2), so the instrument reads η × ρ directly after squaring.
- Skin depth δ = (2η/ωρ)^(1/2); at 28 kHz in water-viscosity fluids, δ is tens of microns, so a few cc of fluid suffice.
- Bounded thin films cause standing-wave resonances: α shows peaks and troughs as film thickness sweeps through multiples of λ/4.
- For films with high rigidity the probe resonant frequency β can exceed its undamped value ω₀ near λ/4 thickness — caused by a rigid sheath effect.
- Operating range: −190°C to +650°F, vacuum to 1000 psi, 0–50 000 cP×g/cc, accuracy ±2% below 1000, ±5% above.
- Response time < 1 s; peak displacement < 1 µm so material is not disturbed.
- Colloidal or surface-active materials show an exponential viscosity increase after probe immersion due to particle migration to the metal interface — a useful secondary measurement.
- Experimental data shown for rubber latex drying, blood coagulation, starch conversion, thermosetting resin cure, and ceramic/glaze thinner.
