## Overview
A short research paper proposing a picosecond ultrasonics method to measure longitudinal sound velocity in individual film layers as thin as 2–5 nm. By stacking bilayers into periodic Si/Mo superlattices of known thickness ratios and measuring the effective superlattice sound velocity via laser pulse-echo, the authors extract individual layer velocities through the Rytov superlattice phonon dispersion relation.

## Unique contribution
Extends picosecond pulse-echo capability below the previous ~20 nm minimum by using superlattice stacking (40 bilayer periods, d ≈ 7 nm) so the aggregate is thick enough for accurate time-of-flight measurement, while Bloch-wave propagation ensures no interface scattering. The technique is demonstrated on ion-beam sputtered Si/Mo superlattices relevant to EUV lithography.

- Ion-beam sputtering (IBS)

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Five Si/Mo samples with Mo thickness ratio Γ = 0.3–0.7, period d ≈ 7 nm, 40 bilayers each; deposited by IBS on Si(100) substrates.
- Ti:sapphire laser (800 nm, 130 fs, 100 MHz repetition rate) used as pump; reflectivity change ΔR(t) measured by time-delayed probe.
- Echo times measured with ±0.5 ps precision; acoustic spectrum peaks at ~90 GHz, extending to ~200 GHz — far beyond conventional ultrasonics.
- Least-square fit of effective velocities vs. Γ yields: c(a-Si) = 8.16 km/s (98% of bulk) and c(Mo) = 6.25 km/s (94% of bulk). Estimated errors ~±4%.
- The anomalously high a-Si velocity relative to sputtered films in the literature is attributed to compressive stress / material densification during multilayer growth (period contraction ~0.7 nm, stress −350 to −450 MPa).
- Density uncertainty of 10% in constituent materials introduces only ~2.8% error in c_eff; Γ uncertainty of 10% gives only ~1% error.
