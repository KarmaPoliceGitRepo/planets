## Overview
A theoretical paper examining how the directivity patterns of laser-generated elastic waves depend on the optical, thermal, and geometrical parameters of the source. The author compares the widely used but simplified dipole-source model against a rigorous theory that accounts for light absorption, thermal wave propagation, and the actual acoustic reflection coefficient at the free surface, identifying conditions where the dipole approximation fails and proposing a surface-characterisation application based on the sensitivity of normal-direction longitudinal radiation to surface modifications.

## Unique contribution
Establishes a practical criterion (Eq. 19) for when the dipole-source model is quantitatively valid for laser-generated longitudinal waves, and shows that for wide laser beams or high frequencies (where kT approaches kl,t), the normal-direction longitudinal amplitude is strongly sensitive to surface properties — enabling non-contact characterisation of thin films, damaged layers, and surface roughness from changes in radiation pattern.

## Key concepts
- Laser-generated sound (thermo-optical regime)
- Dipole-source model (pair of horizontal forces)
- Rigorous thermoelastic theory (coupled elastic/thermal equations)
- Directivity pattern (longitudinal, shear, Rayleigh waves)
- Thermal wavenumber kT vs. acoustic wavenumbers kl, kt
- Rayleigh determinant / Rayleigh wave generation
- Acoustic reflection coefficients Vll(k), Vlt(k)
- Surface modification effects (thin films, roughness, damaged layers)
- Dipole validity criterion

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Governing equations: linearised elastic motion coupled to thermal balance (Eq. 1–3); boundary conditions: stress-free surface (Eq. 4) + thermal flow continuity (Eq. 5).
- Solution via 2D Green's function in wavenumber space; saddle-point contributions give bulk wave directivity (Eqs. 17–18); pole contributions give Rayleigh wave amplitudes (Eqs. 15–16).
- For metals at NDT frequencies (<1 GHz): kT >> kl,t always holds (demonstrated for aluminium). Shear waves: dipole model adequate. Rayleigh waves: dipole model adequate. Longitudinal waves: dipole model fails at θ = 0 because directly radiated and surface-reflected contributions nearly cancel — but do not fully cancel, leaving a kT-dependent residual.
- Criterion for dipole validity: kl·a·(kT/kl)^0.43·sin(0.08·kl·a) < 0.1 (Eq. 19 approximately). Violated for wide beams (a > λl) at any frequency, or for narrow beams at high frequencies (>250 MHz in Al).
- Wider beams increase normal-direction longitudinal amplitude monotonically; in the limit of an infinitely wide beam only longitudinal waves are generated.
- Surface modifications (thin films, roughness, subsurface damage) alter reflection coefficients and hence the normal-direction longitudinal amplitude; estimated to cause noticeable changes, enabling surface property inversion.
- Surface roughness (mean-square height σ, correlation length a) shown to amplify normal-direction longitudinal radiation — potentially a basis for non-contact roughness measurement.
