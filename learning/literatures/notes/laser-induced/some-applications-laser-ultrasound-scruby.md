## Overview
A comprehensive review article from the UK National NDT Centre at Harwell that surveys the physical principles, distinguishing features, and practical application potential of laser-based ultrasonic techniques. The paper covers both laser generation (thermoelastic and ablation regimes) and laser interferometric detection, then presents laboratory demonstrations across calibration, defect detection, and materials characterisation tasks.

## Unique contribution
Provides one of the earliest systematic assessments of laser ultrasonics readiness for industrial application, including quantitative comparisons to piezoelectric and EMAT alternatives (sensitivity, bandwidth, size, cost, safety), and illustrates concrete results: probe beam calibration, subsurface defect detection, grain-size measurement, and plasma-spray coating thickness monitoring — all from a single research group's laboratory data.

## Key concepts
- Laser ultrasound generation (thermoelastic regime vs. ablation regime)
- Source radiation pattern / directivity (dipole vs. normal-force model)
- Michelson interferometer / phase-locked detection
- Rayleigh surface wave
- Bulk longitudinal and shear wave
- Acoustic emission calibration
- Time-of-flight diffraction (TOFD)
- Ultrasonic attenuation and grain-size characterisation
- Plasma-spray coating inspection
- Subsurface defect detection
- Non-contact / remote transduction
- Electromagnetic acoustic transducers (EMATs)

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Thermoelastic source: principal stresses parallel to free surface; compression energy directed in a lobe at ~50–60° to the normal; shear and surface waves also generated. Ablation source: normal-stress dominated, broad compressional lobe perpendicular to surface.
- Michelson interferometer provides absolute displacement calibration referenced to optical wavelength; minimum detectable displacement ~100 pm over 10 MHz bandwidth under ideal conditions — 15 dB less sensitive than piezoelectric but 8 dB better signal/noise than EMAT in one comparison.
- Demonstrated transducer calibration (reciprocity confirmed: transmitter and receiver beam profiles match); acoustic emission sensor characterisation on a 1/5-scale PWR vessel.
- Defect detection: laser source + piezoelectric receiver showed crack tip TOFD signal in welded joint; subsurface flat-bottomed holes (1.2 mm deep, 6.35 mm dia.) identified by 2.6 MHz high-frequency oscillation and 150 kHz low-frequency flexural mode.
- Grain-size measurement: frequency-dependent attenuation and forward scattering between P and S arrivals distinguishes fine- vs. coarse-grained steel; coarse grain shows higher spectral amplitude at 4–20 MHz.
- Plasma-spray coating: Rayleigh wave delay and guided-wave reverberation frequency scale linearly with coating thickness (1–5 layers, ~0.13 mm each); technique viable for on-line thickness monitoring.
- Key limitations: interferometer sensitivity degrades on rough/unpolished surfaces; system cost ~10× piezoelectric; bulk and complexity remain barriers to industrial adoption as of 1988.
