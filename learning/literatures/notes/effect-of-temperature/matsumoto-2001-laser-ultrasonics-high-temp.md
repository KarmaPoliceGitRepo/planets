# Laser Ultrasonics at High Temperature: SiC and Steel to 1600°C

## Overview
Applies laser ultrasonics (Nd:YAG generation, interferometric detection) to measure longitudinal wave velocity in silicon carbide (SiC) up to 1600°C and in steel to high temperatures. Derives a 5th-order polynomial V(T) for steel and demonstrates that thermal expansion correction is necessary to achieve <3% measurement error.

## Unique contribution
Demonstrates laser-ultrasonic velocity measurements in SiC to 1600°C and identifies that thermal expansion of the sample (changing path length) must be explicitly corrected to achieve accurate V(T) inversion.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- SiC measurements successful to 1600°C (limited by laser fluence and detection sensitivity).
- Steel V(T) fitted to 5th-order polynomial to capture non-linear behaviour including phase transformation features.
- Thermal expansion of sample changes acoustic path length: neglecting this introduces >3% error in velocity; correction brings error to <1%.
- Laser spot generates thermoelastic pulse; Fabry-Pérot or Michelson interferometer detects displacement.
- No physical contact required; suitable for furnace environments with optical access.
