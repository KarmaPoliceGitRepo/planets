# Ultrasonic Detection of Creep Damage in ½Cr½Mo¼V Steel at 600°C

## Overview
Uses ultrasonic attenuation and velocity measurements to monitor creep damage evolution in ½Cr½Mo¼V steel subjected to 600°C / 70 MPa conditions. Proposes a two-component attenuation model α = Mf⁴ + Nf² (Rayleigh + diffraction terms) to separate damage-induced scattering from geometric losses.

## Unique contribution
Provides direct experimental evidence that ultrasonic attenuation (specifically the Rayleigh-scattering coefficient M) is a sensitive and quantitative indicator of creep cavity nucleation and growth in power-plant steel before macroscopic failure.

## Key concepts
- Creep damage
- ½Cr½Mo¼V steel
- Ultrasonic attenuation model (α = Mf⁴ + Nf²)
- Rayleigh scattering
- Cavity nucleation

- 600°C / 70 MPa

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Attenuation model: α = M·f⁴ + N·f² where M (Rayleigh scattering) increases with creep cavity density; N (diffraction/geometric) remains approximately constant.
- M increases monotonically with creep life fraction; precedes and predicts failure.
- Velocity also decreases with creep damage accumulation but is less sensitive than attenuation.
- Applied to ½Cr½Mo¼V (a standard power-plant steam pipe steel) at 600°C / 70 MPa.
- Results demonstrate feasibility of ultrasonic remaining-life assessment for power-plant components.
