## Overview
First use of the "shoulder technique" (shouldered cylindrical sample with water-cooled transducer face) to measure ultrasonic attenuation at elevated temperatures (20–800°C) at 5 MHz. Applied to EN-8 and EN-45 carbon steels and 18/8 austenitic stainless steel. Interprets results in terms of grain scattering theory (Bhatia–Moore) and magnetic contributions near the Curie temperature.

## Unique contribution
First report of ultrasonic attenuation measurements up to 800°C using the shoulder technique, which keeps the transducer at room temperature while the sample section is at high temperature. Identifies the 200°C Curie-temperature peak of cementite as a magnetic (magnetostrictive) attenuation contribution.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Peak in attenuation at ~200°C in EN-8 and EN-45 carbon steels due to Curie temperature of ferromagnetic cementite (Fe₃C).
- Attenuation in EN-8: rises with temperature, peaks at ~200°C, falls to a minimum at ~400°C, then rises again above 400°C.
- Non-magnetic 18/8 stainless steel: after heating at 800°C for 1 h (sigma-phase formation), attenuation remains essentially constant with temperature.
- Sigma phase (FeCr) formed above 800°C in 18% Cr steels causes permanently elevated, temperature-independent attenuation.
- Chemical compositions: EN-8 (0.35–0.45% C, 0.6–1.0% Mn), EN-45 (0.5–0.6% C, 1.5–2.0% Si).
- Grain diameters: EN-8 = 0.0094 cm, EN-45 = 0.0075 cm.
- Calculated vs. observed attenuation ratio (obs − magnetic)/calc ≈ 2.2–2.7; theoretical model underestimates, likely due to incomplete magnetic subtraction.
