# Ultrasonic Attenuation by Momentary Contact to 1200°C in SAE 52100 Steel

## Overview
Uses a "momentary contact" technique (briefly touching a cooled transducer assembly to the hot steel surface) to measure ultrasonic attenuation in SAE 52100 bearing steel from 25 to 1200°C. Identifies an attenuation peak at 720–760°C associated with the Curie/phase-transformation region.

## Unique contribution
Extends attenuation measurements to 1200°C using the momentary-contact approach, the widest temperature range for a contact technique, and clearly documents the 720–760°C attenuation peak in a bearing steel.

## Key concepts
- Momentary contact technique
- Ultrasonic attenuation
- SAE 52100 steel (bearing steel)
- Curie temperature attenuation peak
- Grain growth
- High-temperature measurement to 1200°C

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Attenuation peak at 720–760°C: attributed to combined effect of Curie transition and onset of ferrite→austenite transformation, increasing domain wall mobility and microstructural changes.
- Above 760°C: attenuation initially drops as ferrite completes transformation to austenite, then rises again with grain growth.
- At 1200°C: very high attenuation from coarse austenite grain scattering limits pulse-echo sensitivity.
- Momentary contact: transducer assembly at room temperature, pressed briefly against hot sample; TOF and amplitude measured in < 1 second before transducer heats.
- Results complementary to laser-ultrasonic (Scruby 1993) data from similar temperature range.
