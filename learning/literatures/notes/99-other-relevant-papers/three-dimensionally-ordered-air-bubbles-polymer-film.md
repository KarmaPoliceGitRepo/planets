## Overview
Published in Science (Vol. 292, April 2001), this paper reports a simple method for creating three-dimensionally ordered, hexagonally packed arrays of air bubbles in polymer films. Dilute polystyrene solutions in volatile solvents are cast on glass slides under moist airflow. Evaporative cooling condenses water droplets into ordered breath-figure arrays; the droplets sink into the solution layerby-layer and, after full solvent evaporation, leave behind multilayer macroporous scaffolds with pore diameters tunable from 0.2 to 20 µm by changing airflow velocity.

## Unique contribution
This work is the first to achieve robust 3D ordered macroporous polymer films from simple coil-like polymers (no special rod-coil architecture required) using thermocapillary convection and breath-figure templating. The scalable, dynamically controllable pore size (adjustable in real time via airflow velocity) distinguishes this method from colloidal crystal templating where length scale is fixed by the template.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Polystyrene (M_w = 50,000, monochelate with carboxylic acid end group) dissolved in benzene or toluene at 0.1–5 wt%; cast on glass in moist airflow.
- Evaporative cooling lowers surface temperature by up to 25°C (near 0°C), nucleating and ordering water droplets into hexagonally packed breath figures.
- Droplets are stabilised against coalescence by thermocapillary convection (temperature gradient between cool solution surface and warmer condensing droplets) plus a separating vapour layer of escaping solvent.
- After droplet sinking and solvent/water evaporation, a 3D polymer scaffold with open, hexagonally packed spherical pores is left.
- Pore diameter: 6 µm at 30 m/min airflow → 0.5 µm at 300 m/min airflow (controllable over ~0.2–20 µm range).
- 3D order confirmed by confocal optical sectioning (multiple reflective layers at 2 µm intervals corresponding to hole diameter) and electron microscopy.
- Solvent density matters: solvents less dense than water (benzene, toluene) yield 3D arrays; denser solvents (CS₂) yield only a single-layer 2D film.
- The method generalises to other polymers (cellulose acetate, PMMA) dissolved in volatile solvents.
- Applications discussed: microlens arrays, beam-steering devices, picoliter reactors/analytical containers, metal scaffolds via chemical reduction, photonic band-gap materials.
- Relevance to surface texture: provides a self-assembly route to well-defined, tunable microstructured porous surfaces without lithography.
