## Overview
Part 3 of EN 50324, covering large-signal (high-power) characterisation of piezoceramics. It addresses the non-linear behaviour of piezoceramic materials under high electric field and high mechanical strain, defining measurement methods and material selection criteria for power transducer applications.

## Unique contribution
Introduces the Figure of Merit Mij = (dij)²/sij^E for electromechanical conversion efficiency at high power, and M = d·g for mechanoelectrical conversion (e.g., igniters). Provides quantitative non-linearity characterisation via two coefficients: α (increase in mechanical loss tangent per unit mean square strain) and β (compliance change per unit mean square strain), measured from frequency-sweep impedance data.

## Key concepts
- Large signal / nonlinear behaviour
- Figure of Merit Mij (electromechanical), M = d·g (mechanoelectrical)
- Dielectric loss tangent tan δd at high electric field
- Mechanical loss tangent tan δm
- Amplitude-frequency (A-F) effect (resonance frequency decrease with drive level)
- Frequency hysteresis in resonance curves
- Non-linearity coefficients α and β
- Mean strain ⟨S⟩ (RMS strain from current at resonance)
- Type 100 vs Type 300 comparison at high signal
- Depolarisation / thermal runaway risk

## Methods / results / takeaways
Large-signal dielectric properties are measured with a Schering Bridge at 1 kHz using disc specimens (10–20 mm diameter, 1 mm thick). Type 100 material allows up to 18% increase in ε33^T and tan δd ≤ 0.04 at 400 kV/m; Type 300 allows only 4% increase and tan δd ≤ 0.01. Mechanical non-linearity is measured with a network analyser and power amplifier via frequency sweep (f1→f2 in <2 s to prevent heating). Specimens show Type 300 materials have significantly lower α and β than Type 100, confirming their advantage for high-power continuous applications. For pulsed transducers (very low duty cycle) Type 100 or 200 may be preferred due to higher d coefficients.
