## Overview
ASTM E9 specifies the apparatus, specimen geometry, and procedure for axial-load compression testing of metallic materials at room temperature. It covers determination of compressive yield strength, yield point, modulus of elasticity, and compressive strength (for brittle materials). An annex extends the methods to cemented carbides.

## Unique contribution
Specifies three standardised specimen form categories — short (L/D ≈ 0.8–2.0), medium-length (L/D ≈ 3.0), and long (L/D ≈ 8–10) — and provides dimensional tables for cylindrical specimens. Defines the critical buckling stress using both Euler and modified Euler (tangent modulus) equations and specifies end-fixity coefficients (C = 1 for pinned, 4 for fully fixed, 3.75 for flat-end). Gives representative compression jig designs and specimen dimensions for thin sheet testing to prevent buckling.

## Key concepts
- Axial compression; compressive yield strength; compressive strength
- Buckling (elastic instability, inelastic instability, local, torsional)
- Critical stress and end-fixity coefficient C
- Radius of gyration r = (I/A)^0.5
- Euler buckling equation: Scr = Cπ²E/(L/r)²
- Barreling (end-friction-induced non-uniform transverse deformation)
- Offset yield strength (0.2% offset method)
- Strain rate: 0.005 in./in./min in elastic range
- Alignment device / subpress; bearing block parallelism

## Methods / results / takeaways
Bearing block faces must be flat and parallel to within 0.0002 in./in. Specimens require lateral surfaces coaxial within 0.01 in. and ends flat/parallel to 0.0005 in./in., perpendicular within 3' of arc. For thin sheet specimens, compression jigs (Montgomery-Templin, NACA, Miller types) are used with controlled lateral support to prevent buckling. Testing speed is set to achieve ≈ 0.005 in./in./min strain rate in the elastic region. The 0.2% offset method is applied from the stress-strain diagram to determine yield strength. For ductile materials, compressive strength is reported at a specified total strain. The method explicitly notes that no reference standards exist for bias determination in compression.
