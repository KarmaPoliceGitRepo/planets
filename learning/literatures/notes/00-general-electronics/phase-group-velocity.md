## Overview
A derivation-oriented note explaining how phase velocity and group velocity arise from Maxwell's equations in free space and in dispersive media. Shows that energy velocity equals phase velocity in a non-dispersive medium and derives the group velocity for a two-wave superposition in a dispersive medium.

## Unique contribution
Derives energy velocity = phase velocity (both equal 1/√με) via a cylinder energy-conservation argument in free space. Then derives group velocity vg = dω/dk from the beat-envelope of two waves with nearby frequencies, providing clear physical intuition for when the two velocities differ.

## Key concepts
- Phase velocity: vp = ω/k = 1/√με
- Group velocity: vg = dω/dk
- Dispersive vs. non-dispersive medium (ε or μ frequency-dependent)
- Maxwell equations in sourceless phasor form
- Helmholtz vector wave equations for E and H
- Energy velocity: v = s/w (Poynting flux / energy density)
- Beat envelope from two plane waves at nearby frequencies

## Methods / results / takeaways
- From Maxwell's equations in a sourceless region, the vector Helmholtz equation (∇² + ω²με)E = 0 yields k = ω√με and vp = ω/k = 1/√με.
- Energy velocity equals phase velocity in a non-dispersive medium: v = (E0²/η) / (εE0²) = 1/√με = vp.
- For two waves at ω1 ≈ ω2, the total field is the product of a rapid carrier at (ωc, kc) and a slow envelope at (Δω, Δk); the envelope moves at vg = Δω/Δk → dω/dk.
- In a dispersive medium vg ≠ vp; vg can be greater or less than vp depending on the sign of dvp/dk.
- For a single-frequency wave, even in a dispersive medium, vg = vp because there is only one frequency component.
