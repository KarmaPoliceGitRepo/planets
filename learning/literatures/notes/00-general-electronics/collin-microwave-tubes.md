## Overview
Chapter 9 covers high-power microwave vacuum tubes: klystrons, magnetrons, traveling-wave tubes (TWTs), and backward-wave oscillators. These are still the dominant high-power sources at microwave frequencies for radar, satellite uplinks, and scientific instruments. The chapter derives the electron-wave interaction physics underlying each device.

## Unique contribution
Provides the physics of velocity modulation and bunching in klystrons, the crossed-field interaction in magnetrons, and the slow-wave/beam interaction in TWTs from first principles. This is the theoretical foundation for understanding why solid-state devices cannot (yet) match tube power at the highest microwave frequencies.

## Key concepts
- Velocity modulation and bunching of electron beams
- Klystron: two-cavity (amplifier), reflex (oscillator)
- Magnetron: crossed-field device, resonant cavities, high-power oscillator
- Traveling-wave tube (TWT): wideband amplifier using slow-wave structure
- Backward-wave oscillator (BWO): electronically tunable
- Beam-wave interaction, gain mechanism
- Space-charge waves
- Power limitations of solid-state vs. tubes

## Methods / results / takeaways
- In a klystron, an electron beam passes through a buncher cavity (velocity modulation), drifts to form current bunches (density modulation), then gives up energy to a catcher cavity.
- The reflex klystron uses a single cavity and a repeller electrode to reflect the beam back for self-oscillation; tunable by adjusting repeller voltage.
- Magnetrons operate with electrons in crossed E and B fields, spiraling in the interaction space and coupling to the resonant cavities; efficiencies of 50–70% are achievable.
- TWTs use a helix (or coupled-cavity) slow-wave structure to make the EM wave travel at nearly the same velocity as the electron beam; interaction over a long distance gives high gain (20–60 dB) with wide bandwidth.
- Tubes are irreplaceable for power levels >100 W above a few GHz; solid-state power amplifiers dominate below ~10 W or at lower frequencies.
