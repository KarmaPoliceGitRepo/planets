## Overview
Chapter 6 covers passive devices used in microwave circuits: directional couplers, hybrids, power dividers, isolators, and circulators. Includes the theory and design of waveguide and microstrip directional couplers, magic-T junctions, Wilkinson dividers, and ferrite-based nonreciprocal devices.

## Unique contribution
Provides the rigorous derivation of directional coupler properties from S-matrix theory, showing the constraints imposed by losslessness and reciprocity. Covers ferrite-based isolators and circulators — nonreciprocal devices that are unique to microwave frequencies and require gyromagnetic material theory.

## Key concepts
- Directional coupler: coupling factor, directivity, isolation, through-port loss
- 3-dB hybrid couplers: branch-line (90°) and rat-race (180°)
- Magic-T junction (waveguide 180° hybrid)
- Wilkinson power divider (equal and unequal split, with isolation resistor)
- Ferrite materials: gyromagnetic effect, resonance, permeability tensor
- Isolator (nonreciprocal, absorbs reflected power), circulator (3-port)
- Faraday rotation in ferrite-loaded waveguides

## Methods / results / takeaways
- A lossless, reciprocal 4-port network is a directional coupler; the S-matrix constraints give coupling factor C = –20 log|S21| dB and isolation I = –20 log|S31| dB.
- Wilkinson divider: equal-split uses λ/4 arms of Z0√2; isolation resistor 2Z0 between output ports prevents coupling when both outputs are driven.
- Ferrite circulators exploit gyromagnetic resonance: at the operating frequency, clockwise circulation is supported (wave passes from port 1→2→3→1); reversed direction is highly attenuated.
- Isolators are one-port terminated circulators, protecting sources from reflections (e.g., microwave amplifier to antenna).
