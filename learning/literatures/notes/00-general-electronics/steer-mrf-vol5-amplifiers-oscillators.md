## Overview
Volume 5, the final book in the Steer series, focuses on the design of active microwave circuits: transistor amplifiers and oscillators. Covers transistor (FET and BJT) S-parameter characterization, stability analysis, gain circles (available gain, power gain, maximum transducer gain), noise figure, low-noise amplifier (LNA) design, power amplifier (PA) design classes, and oscillator design using negative resistance and feedback techniques.

## Unique contribution
Integrates stability analysis, gain-noise tradeoffs, and matching network design in a unified S-parameter framework oriented toward practical microwave amplifier and oscillator design. Provides the "design loop" perspective where stability is checked first, then gain and noise circles guide the simultaneous input/output match.

## Key concepts
- Transistor S-parameters (small-signal model)
- Stability analysis: unconditionally stable (K > 1, |Δ| < 1), potentially unstable
- Stability circles, gain circles on the Smith chart
- Transducer power gain GT, available gain GA, power gain GP
- Maximum stable gain (MSG), maximum available gain (MAG)
- Noise figure (NF), minimum noise figure (NFmin), noise circles
- Low-noise amplifier (LNA) design: simultaneous noise and gain match
- Power amplifier (PA) classes: A, AB, B, C, D, E, F
- Oscillator design: Barkhausen criterion, negative resistance, feedback
- Phase noise in oscillators

## Methods / results / takeaways
- An amplifier is unconditionally stable when K > 1 and |Δ| < 1 (Rollett stability factor); otherwise stability must be checked for the specific source/load reflection coefficients.
- LNA design: input-matching for minimum noise figure is generally not the same as conjugate match for maximum gain; the noise and gain circles on the Smith chart show the available tradeoff.
- PA design class A (linear, always on) offers best linearity but lowest efficiency (~50%); class B (half-cycle operation) achieves ~78% efficiency; class E/F switch-mode PAs can theoretically reach 100% efficiency.
- Oscillator: feedback loop gain = 1 at the oscillation frequency (Barkhausen); negative-resistance view treats the active device as presenting –R to the resonator.
- Phase noise is proportional to 1/f² offset from carrier (Leeson model) and is minimized by high-Q resonators and low-noise oscillator transistors.
