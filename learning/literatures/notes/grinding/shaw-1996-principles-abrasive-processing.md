## Overview
A comprehensive 1996 Oxford University Press monograph on abrasive technology by Milton C. Shaw, covering the full range of abrasive processes from fundamental mechanics to industrial practice. The book emphasizes understanding why phenomena occur rather than providing procedural recipes. Scope extends from abrasive particle production and characterization, through surface grinding mechanics (treated as the foundation case), to extensions covering other grinding configurations and comminution theory. Shaw highlights that grinding differs fundamentally from cutting — speeds are ~10× higher, chip thicknesses ~100× smaller, and specific energy ~10× greater, all with major thermal consequences.

## Unique contribution
A single authoritative reference that bridges abrasive particle science, grinding mechanics, and thermal analysis of abrasive processes in one volume. The preface notes the evolution of grinding from a finishing operation to a competitor of cutting ("abrasive machining"), which distinguishes this book's perspective from older references focused only on surface finish.

## Key concepts
- Abrasive processing / abrasive machining
- Surface grinding (kinematics, chip formation)
