## Overview
A seminal 1963 paper in the journal Wear (Vol. 6, pp. 483–494) that surveys the history and theory of flash temperatures — the transient surface-temperature rises generated at moving contacts between rubbing surfaces. Blok presents derivations of flash temperature formulas for band-shaped heat sources (representative of meshing gear teeth, cams, and tappets), discusses the partitioning of heat between the two rubbing bodies, and introduces the scuffing-temperature postulate (constant critical temperature at onset of scuffing for a given oil/material pair) as a practical design criterion.

## Unique contribution
Foundational theoretical formulation of flash temperature for a moving band-shaped heat source, including the heat-partition principle between two rubbing bodies (heat split proportional to √(b·v) for each surface, where b = thermal contact coefficient and v = surface velocity). The paper also consolidates years of experimental data on scuffing of steel gears with mineral oils, showing scuffing temperatures of roughly 130–300 °C depending on oil viscosity grade — providing a quantitative basis for lubrication design.

## Key concepts
- Flash temperature (transient surface temperature rise at a sliding contact)
- Moving heat source theory (Boussinesq, Mache, Rosenthal foundations)
- Band-shaped heat source (line contact geometry)
- Thermal contact coefficient b = √(k·ρ·c)
- Heat partition / partitioning principle between two rubbing bodies
- Maximum flash temperature Tmax formula
- Scuffing / seizure of gear teeth, cams, tappets
- Extreme pressure (EP) additives and lubricants
- Scuffing temperature postulate (material+oil-specific constant)
- Foil/workpiece thermocouple technique (historical mention)
- Elasto-hydrodynamic lubrication (EHL) — mentioned as extension
- Hertz elastic contact theory (contact width w)

## Methods / results / takeaways
- For a band-shaped heat source at high speed the flash temperature concentrates in a very thin surface layer; heat flow is essentially 1D (perpendicular to surface) within the source footprint.
- Maximum flash temperature formula: Tmax = A · qav · √(w/v) / b, where A ≈ 1.13 (uniform flux distribution) or 1.11 (semi-elliptical); w = source width; v = velocity; b = thermal contact coefficient.
- For two rubbing bodies sharing the heat source, heat is partitioned in proportion b₁√v₁ : b₂√v₂; for equal b, the formula for gear teeth becomes: Tmax ∝ f·p·(√v₁ + √v₂) / b (Eq. 3), where f is friction coefficient, p is Hertz contact pressure.
- Scuffing temperature postulate: for a given steel/oil combination, the sum of bulk + flash temperature at onset of scuffing is approximately constant, independent of speed or load. Validated for a range of non-additive mineral oils on the IAE Spur Gear Rig.
- Scuffing temperatures for non-additive mineral oils: ~130–160 °C for 7 cSt oil (at 60 °C) up to ~220–300 °C for 150 cSt oil.
- Systematic error in applying Eq. 3 in practice: actual tooth load and friction coefficient are hard to predict at design stage; dynamic loading effects compound this.
- Applications mentioned beyond gears: cutting tools, brakes, firearms (gun barrel bore), rotating X-ray tube anodes, journal bearings.
