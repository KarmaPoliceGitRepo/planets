## Overview
This Physical Review paper (vol. 129, no. 6, March 1963) reports systematic experimental measurements of how a static magnetic field alters the speed of sound in single-crystal metals (Cu, Ag, Au, Al, Ta, V). Using a 10 MHz ultrasonic "sing-around" pulse technique sensitive to parts-per-million velocity changes, Alers and Fleury test the macroscopic theory of Alpher, Rubin, and Rodriguez, which predicts that sound velocity increases as the square of the applied field. They also probe the angular dependence of the effect and the regime where the electron mean free path becomes comparable to the acoustic wavelength.

## Unique contribution
This is the first comprehensive single-crystal study across multiple metals and temperatures testing the magnetic-field–sound-velocity coupling quantitatively. Key additions over prior polycrystalline work:
- Verified H² dependence and the angular (sin²θ / cos²θ) dependence for longitudinal and shear modes respectively.
- Showed the macroscopic theory holds quantitatively when the skin depth δ ≪ acoustic wavelength λ, with only a slight discrepancy in the finite-conductivity correction for lower-purity specimens.
- Discovered that in certain crystallographic directions of high-purity copper at 4.2 K — specifically directions corresponding to "open orbit" electron trajectories identified in magnetoresistance studies — the velocity varies linearly with field rather than as H², signalling a breakdown of the macroscopic approximation.
- Demonstrated temperature independence of the effect coefficient γ in noble metals at 300 K, 77 K, and 4.2 K, confirming the conductivity correction is negligible in high-conductivity metals.

## Methods / results / takeaways
**Technique:** A 10 MHz sing-around oscillator measures transit-time changes directly as frequency shifts; velocity resolution ~1 ppm. Temperature drift was managed by passive thermal isolation and post-hoc correction rather than active control. Magnetic fields up to 21 kOe from an ADL electromagnet; single-crystal specimens smaller than 1 inch cube.

**Theoretical framework:** From Maxwell's equations combined with the equation of motion for a conducting elastic medium, the predicted velocity corrections are:
- Longitudinal: ΔC/C ∝ H² sin²θ / (8πρC₀²) (for δ ≪ λ)
- Shear: ΔC/C ∝ H² cos²θ / (8πρC₀²)

The coefficient γ = 1/(8πρC₀²) serves as the key measurable.

**Key results:**
- H² dependence confirmed in Au, Ag, Cu, Al, Ta, V; angular (sin²θ / cos²θ) dependence confirmed.
- Measured γ values agree with theory to within ~5% experimental error for high-conductivity metals.
- For metals with lower conductivity or shorter acoustic wavelength, a finite-conductivity correction is needed; the correction formula is approximately right but slightly underestimates the true effect.
- In high-purity Cu at 4.2 K, strong ultrasonic attenuation peaks appear at orientations corresponding to open-orbit electron directions (electron mean free path ~0.08 × acoustic wavelength). In at least two such directions the velocity grows linearly with H (6–21 kOe range) rather than as H², demonstrating macroscopic theory failure.
- Temperature independence of γ in noble metals (300 K, 77 K, 4.2 K) confirms theory prediction that conductivity correction is negligible for these metals.

**Practical takeaway:** The macroscopic theory is a reliable quantitative tool for acoustoelastic magnetic-field effects in high-conductivity, high-purity metals as long as electrons are tightly coupled to the sound wave (mean free path and skin depth both ≪ wavelength). Breakdown occurs precisely where Fermi-surface topology (open orbits) matters, opening an acoustic probe of electronic band structure.
