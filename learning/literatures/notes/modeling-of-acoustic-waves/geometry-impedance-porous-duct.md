# Effect of Geometry and Impedance Variation on the Acoustic Performance of a Porous Material Lined Duct

- **Source:** 2017 - EFFECT OF GEOMETRY AND IMPEDANCE VARIATION ON THE ACOUSTIC PERFORMANCE OF A POROUS MATERIAL LINED DUCT - Abdelmonem Masmoudi.pdf

## Overview
A journal paper (JTAM, Warsaw) that numerically investigates how changes in duct cross-section geometry and acoustic liner impedance affect the acoustic performance of cylindrical ducts lined with porous materials. Two duct configurations are studied: one with a central section of reduced diameter (constriction) and one with a central section of expanded diameter. For each, the length and radius of the geometrically altered zone are varied across four configurations while computing the multimodal scattering matrix, reflection/transmission coefficients, and acoustic power attenuation.

## Unique contribution
Provides a direct numerical comparison between duct-diameter-decrease and duct-diameter-increase configurations, demonstrating that constricted sections are more acoustically absorptive than expanded ones. The Delany-Bazley porous material model and the Elnady-Boden perforated plate impedance model are combined into a single liner impedance and fed into a FEM-based multimodal scattering matrix to quantify the combined effect of geometry and liner.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- A FEM weak-variational formulation of the Helmholtz equation with modal-pressure degrees of freedom at inlet/outlet yields the full 2N×2N scattering matrix S, from which acoustic power attenuation W_att is derived via eigenvalue analysis.
- Liner = perforated plate (t=1 mm, d_p=1 mm, σ_p=2.5 %) + porous layer (d_m=20 mm, flow resistivity σ=26 000 Ns/m³) + rigid backing.
- **Diameter-decrease duct:** attenuation peaks near ka≈2.5, reaching up to ~4.8 dB for the longest constriction (80 % of duct length). Longer and narrower constrictions increase both absorption (larger liner surface) and reflection.
- **Diameter-increase duct:** maximum attenuation ~2 dB at ka≈2.5 for the largest expansion; significantly less effective than the constriction case.
- Key takeaway: constricting sections concentrate the wave-lining interaction and produce higher absorption; expanding sections increase reflection with only modest absorption gain.
