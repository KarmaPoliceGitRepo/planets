## Overview
An Ultrasonics journal paper that derives a new closed-form formulation for the global ultrasonic transmission and reflection coefficients of a two-phase (fluid-saturated) random porous medium under normal incidence. Starting from the nine local transmission/reflection coefficients at the fluid–porous interfaces (determined by Biot-theory boundary conditions), the authors use a matrix recurrence approach to sum all internal multiple-reflection contributions — fast-to-fast, fast-to-slow, slow-to-fast, slow-to-slow wave conversions — to any desired order. The formulation converges beyond the 16th order. Experimental validation is performed on alumina porous samples immersed in water.

## Unique contribution
Prior approaches computed global coefficients numerically or from propagator-matrix methods without an explicit summation formula accounting for all fast/slow wave conversion modes. This paper provides an analytic recurrence in matrix form that makes the physical contribution of each internal reflection order transparent, enables rapid computation, and directly supports inverse problem solution (fitting medium parameters to measured transmission data).

## Key concepts
- Biot theory (slow wave, fast wave)
- Local transmission / reflection coefficients at porous interface
- Global transmission coefficient (Debye-series / recurrence approach)
- Fast and slow compressional waves in porous media
- Wave conversion modes at porous/fluid interface
- Propagator matrix / transfer matrix in porous media
- Inverse problem method for porous material characterization
- Porosity, tortuosity, permeability (porous material parameters)
- Ultrasonic measurement cell (normal incidence, water tank)
- PMMA-ball sintered alumina porous samples

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Nine local coefficients (r_ep, t^Sl_ep, t^Fa_ep, r^SlSl_pe, r^SlFa_pe, t^Sl_pe, r^FaFa_pe, r^FaSl_pe, t^Fa_pe) are resolved from three 3×3 boundary-condition matrices derived from Biot theory.
- Global transmission S₁₂ and reflection S₁₁ are expressed as sums over all internal reflection orders using 2×2 matrix recurrences; convergence beyond order 14–16.
- Experimental cell: alumina porous sample (thickness ~8 mm, pore radius ~550 µm) standing in 19 °C water between a 50–600 kHz network-analyzer-driven emitter and receiver.
- Inverse problem fit to two samples (porosity β=0.68–0.70, tortuosity α=1.83, permeability P=16.8×10⁻¹² m²) showed good agreement with measured S₁₂ curves.
- Slow wave was too attenuated to detect directly; only the fast wave component was experimentally resolved above the noise floor.
- Formulation is generalizable to oblique incidence and multi-layer porous stacks.
