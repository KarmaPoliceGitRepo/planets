## Overview
A classic two-part technical drawing textbook, now in its third edition (SI units). Part 1 covers plane and solid geometry through construction techniques: scales, geometric figures, isometric and oblique projection, conic sections, loci, orthographic projection, intersections of solids, and surface developments. Part 2 covers engineering drawing conventions (BS 308), including sectioning, screw threads, dimensioning, assembly drawings, and fastening standards. The book bridges hand-drafting fundamentals with the conceptual grounding needed for CAD use.

## Unique contribution
Provides rigorous step-by-step geometric construction procedures (compass-and-straightedge level) for every topic — rather than relying on formulae — making the spatial and logical reasoning behind technical drawing explicit. The detailed treatment of surface developments (prisms, pyramids, cylinders, cones, intersecting solids) and equivalent-area constructions is especially thorough. The Pythagoras-based method for combining pipe cross-sections by area is a notable practical technique.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- **Scales:** plain scales read one dimension; diagonal scales add a second subdivided dimension via diagonal lines; proportional scales use radial lines from a pole to rescale figures.
- **Geometric constructions:** All constructions are compass-and-straightedge. Inscribed/circumscribed circles, tangent arcs, arc blending, and polygon construction are each derived from first principles.
- **Isometric drawing:** Conventional (not true) isometric uses full measurements along 30° axes; ellipses replace circles in isometric using four-centre approximations.
- **Blending:** The key rule is to locate the arc centre correctly — a tangent arc touching two curves must have its centre equidistant from both; finding that centre geometrically guarantees tangency.
- **Orthographic projection:** First angle (European) places views in the direction the object is projected; third angle (American/BS preferred) places views on the same side as the observer. Auxiliary views reveal true shapes of inclined faces.
- **Intersection of solids:** Cutting-plane method — pass a series of horizontal (or other) section planes, find their intersection outlines on both solids, join the points to plot the line of interpenetration.
- **Developments:** Prisms and cylinders unfold by projecting true heights; pyramids and cones use radial development (arc length = slant edge length); oblique/truncated solids require triangulation.
- **Equivalent areas:** A rectangle equal in area to a triangle is constructed by halving the triangle's height; a square equal in area to a rectangle uses a semicircle construction. Pythagoras lets you combine or split circle cross-sections by area without calculation.
