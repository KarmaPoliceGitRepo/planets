# The Use of Ultrasonics for Gauging and Proximity Sensing in Air

## Overview
Foundational JASA paper investigating the use of 215 kHz ultrasonics for gauging and proximity sensing in air. Achieves ±0.1 mm accuracy over 100–250 mm range with temperature compensation. Covers absorption limits, beam forming theory for flat and focused transducers, and experimental results under still air, turbulent airflow, and hot-surface conditions.

## Unique contribution
Provides a rigorous first-principles treatment of the limits of ultrasonic gauging in air: frequency upper bound (~1 MHz) from absorption, beam forming equations for plane piston and spherical cap, and systematic experimental characterisation of error vs temperature, airflow, surface temperature, and angle. Introduces extinction distance formula: `extinction distance = 5×10³/f² mm` where f in Hz.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- 215 kHz two-transducer system (separate TX and RX, 6° angle); granite table reflector.
- Accuracy after temperature compensation: ±0.1 mm over 100–250 mm in still air.
- Turbulent air stream (7.5 m/s): accuracy degrades to ±0.1 mm at ≤220 mm, then worse at longer distances.
- Hot reflecting surface: errors grow rapidly in still air above 50 °C; with forced airflow, accuracy ±0.2 mm up to 260 °C.
- Beam spot diameter ~30 mm at 100 mm (two-transducer geometry) — large spot limits accuracy on curved surfaces.
- Key frequency limit: at 1 MHz, extinction distance ~50 mm (1/e attenuation length) — sets maximum practical range.
- Temperature correction to better than 0.5 °C required for ±0.1 mm over 100 mm.
- Plate thickness measurements agree within 0.05 mm of micrometer; cylindrical rod errors larger (spot too big).
