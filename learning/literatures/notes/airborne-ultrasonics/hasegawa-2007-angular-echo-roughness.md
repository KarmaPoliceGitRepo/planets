# Angular Dependence of Ultrasonic Echo from Surface with Minute Roughness

- **Source:** Hasegawa 2007 angular echo roughness
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1TZuXC3OOQvptKNGRKuIt38FlKNkncnlH/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Hasegawa, Kudo & Yamamoto, 2007 (IEEE Ultrasonics Symposium 2007)
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Investigates the angular dependence of ultrasonic echo amplitude from metal surfaces with minute roughness (Rq = 0.2–4.1 μm) using a phased-array probe with electronic beam steering. A 10 MHz clinical ultrasound probe (Aloka SSD-6500) steers the beam from 0° to ±32.6° in water immersion. The ratio P(θ)/P(0°) at θ ≥ 20° increases with Rq, providing a sensitive roughness indicator in the micron range. Application target: early detection of atherosclerotic lesions (intima roughness changes) in arterial walls.

## Unique contribution
Uses electronic beam steering (phased array) rather than mechanical rotation, enabling fast angular scan without repositioning. Demonstrates sensitivity to sub-5 μm roughness at 10 MHz — a regime where normal-incidence reflection coefficient alone is insensitive. The P(θ)/P(0°) ratio at oblique angles acts as a differential roughness metric that rejects common-mode amplitude variations (coupling, distance). Clinical motivation distinguishes this from other roughness papers.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Probe: Aloka SSD-6500 10 MHz linear phased array; beam steered electronically to θ = 0°, ±10°, ±20°, ±32.6°.
- Specimens: mirror-polished and mechanically ground flat metal plates, Rq = 0.2–4.1 μm (measured by stylus).
- Medium: water immersion; standoff ~30 mm.
- Metric: P(θ)/P(0°) — amplitude at angle θ normalised by specular (0°) amplitude.
- Result: P(θ)/P(0°) at θ ≥ 20° increases monotonically with Rq for Rq = 0.2–4.1 μm; separation between samples is clearest at θ = 32.6°.
- At θ = 0°: specular reflection is high and relatively insensitive to small roughness differences.
- At θ ≥ 20°: diffuse scattering contribution dominates, which scales with Rq; provides roughness discrimination.
- Theoretical basis: Kirchhoff scattering — coherent component falls with angle while diffuse component sustains energy at oblique angles as roughness increases.
- Application: arterial wall roughness (intima texture change in early atherosclerosis) detectable with clinical ultrasound probe configuration.
- Limitation: laboratory setup; beam-steering range limited by probe aperture; model validation against Kirchhoff theory not completed in this conference paper.
