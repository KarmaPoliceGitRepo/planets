# Absorption of Sound in Air versus Humidity and Temperature

- **Source:** Harris 1966 absorption humidity temperature
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1Y7gAsQmZHDe5wW_J5nKEy-TYChIpJRxt/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Harris, C. M., 1966 (Journal of the Acoustical Society of America, 40(1), 148–159)
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Comprehensive experimental measurements of sound absorption in air at 1/3-octave intervals from 2000 to 12,500 Hz as a function of relative humidity at six temperatures from −0.5°C to 25.1°C, at normal atmospheric pressure. The foundational experimental dataset for atmospheric absorption in the audio frequency range. Results extend and improve earlier measurements; comparisons with existing theory and previous data are provided.

## Unique contribution
The definitive pre-standard experimental reference for atmospheric sound absorption as a function of humidity and temperature in the 2–12.5 kHz range. Widely cited as the experimental baseline against which all subsequent theoretical absorption models (Evans 1972, Bass 1990, Bass 1995, ISO 9613-1) are validated. Introduces the experimental data that revealed the non-monotone humidity dependence of velocity (also explored separately in Harris 1971).

## Key concepts
- Atmospheric sound absorption coefficient α (dB/100 m or dB/1000 ft)
- Molecular vibrational relaxation of O₂ and N₂ as dominant mechanism at audio frequencies
- Humidity (relative humidity, %) as primary variable controlling relaxation frequency f_r
- Temperature effect: shifts f_r and maximum absorption frequency
- Frequency range: 2–12.5 kHz (1/3-octave steps)
- Classical + rotational absorption as baseline

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Apparatus: reverberation room method or free-field absorption measurements; carefully controlled temperature (±0.1°C) and humidity.
- Six temperature levels: approximately −0.5, 5, 10, 15, 20, 25°C; normal atmospheric pressure.
- Key finding: absorption at fixed frequency vs. humidity shows a clear maximum (at the humidity where f_r equals the measurement frequency); this maximum shifts with temperature.
- At 2 kHz: maximum absorption around 10–15% RH at 20°C; at 8 kHz: maximum around 50–60% RH.
- Comparison with earlier data (Knudsen, Evans and Sutherland): generally good agreement but Harris provides finer humidity resolution and more temperature levels.
- Results became the experimental foundation for ANSI S1.26-1978 and eventually ISO 9613-1.
- Limitation: limited to audio range (2–12.5 kHz); does not cover ultrasonic frequencies above ~15 kHz.
