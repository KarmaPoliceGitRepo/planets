# Effects of Humidity on the Velocity of Sound in Air

- **Source:** Harris 1971 humidity velocity sound
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1aAS-QG1k6bqpIJ0VVyv1So4rfCTBbj4N/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Harris, C. M., 1971 (Journal of the Acoustical Society of America, 49(3B), 890–893)
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Precision measurements of the speed of sound in air versus relative humidity at 20.00°C and normal atmospheric pressure, at two low audio frequencies (293 and 503 Hz). Method: natural frequency decay of radial modes in a high-Q spherical steel resonance chamber (0.837 m diameter, reverberation time ~50 s for dry air). Key result: contrary to the common assumption that moisture increases c, the velocity first decreases when humidity is added to dry air, reaching a minimum at ~14% RH, then rises. Above ~30% RH, velocity increases linearly with RH.

## Unique contribution
Provides the benchmark experimental data at 20°C that was used to validate all subsequent theoretical sound speed models (Morfey 1980, Wong & Embleton 1985, Cramer 1993). Demonstrates and explains — via vibrational relaxation theory — why the velocity is non-monotone in humidity: the O₂ relaxation mechanism dominates at low RH and reduces c, while the direct density effect (lighter H₂O molecules) takes over above ~30% RH.

## Key concepts
- Velocity of sound vs relative humidity: non-monotone behaviour
- Minimum c at ~14% RH at 20°C and audio frequencies
- Linear increase of c with RH above ~30% RH
- High-Q spherical resonance chamber method: c from normal mode frequency decay
- O₂ vibrational relaxation: increasing humidity shifts f_r to higher frequency → c decreases at fixed f
- Direct humidity effect: lower molecular weight of H₂O → higher c (linear increase at high RH)
- Kneser single-relaxation model: qualitative agreement but overpredicts initial dip

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Apparatus: 0.837-m steel spherical chamber; temperature stabilised to ±0.01°C; Li-Cl hygrometers (±1% RH accuracy).
- Frequencies: (0,1) mode at ~292 Hz and (0,2) mode at ~503 Hz; frequency measured to 0.01 Hz over 10-s decay intervals.
- Temperature: 20.00°C controlled by drift-rate technique.
- Results (Fig. 2): c/c_dry minimum at ~14% RH; ratio decrease from dry ≈ −0.5×10⁻³; rises linearly above 30% RH with slope consistent with Miller zero-frequency theory.
- Both frequencies give identical results within ±0.00015 uncertainty — confirms these are equilibrium/zero-relaxation effects.
- Theoretical comparison: Kneser single-relaxation theory predicts qualitative behaviour but cannot fit both low- and high-RH regions simultaneously; molecular relaxation shift is larger than Kneser predicts.
- Limitation: only 20°C and 1 atm; audio frequencies only (below O₂ relaxation peak at these conditions); uncertainty ~150 ppm.
