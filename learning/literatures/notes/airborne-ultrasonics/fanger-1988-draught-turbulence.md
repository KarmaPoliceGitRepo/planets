# Air Turbulence and Sensation of Draught

- **Source:** Fanger 1988 draught turbulence

- **Type:** paper

## Overview
Investigates the impact of turbulence intensity (Tu) on draught sensation in 50 human subjects exposed to airflow in a climate chamber. Three experiments at low (Tu < 12%), medium (20% < Tu < 35%), and high (Tu > 55%) turbulence intensities; six mean air velocities from 0.05 to 0.40 m/s; air temperature 23°C. Derives a draught risk model: Percentage of Dissatisfied (PD) as a function of air temperature T_a, mean velocity V_mean, and turbulence intensity Tu. Turbulence significantly increases draught sensation at the same mean velocity.

## Unique contribution
Establishes that turbulence intensity, not just mean velocity, is a critical predictor of draught discomfort — overturning earlier standards based on mean velocity alone. Provides a quantitative PD model usable in HVAC design. Motivates the need to limit turbulence (not just velocity) in air distribution systems.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Subjects: 50 adults; neutral thermal sensation condition.
- Three Tu levels: low (<12%), medium (20–35%), high (>55%); 6 mean velocities per level.
- Air temperature: 23°C constant.
- Method: subjects reported draught presence and discomfort after exposure.
- Key finding: turbulence intensity has significant effect on draught sensation — high Tu substantially increases PD at the same mean velocity.
- Model: PD = f(T_a, V_mean, Tu); higher Tu → larger PD; temperature increase reduces draught risk.
- Application: HVAC diffuser design; spaces with high Tu require lower velocity limits.
