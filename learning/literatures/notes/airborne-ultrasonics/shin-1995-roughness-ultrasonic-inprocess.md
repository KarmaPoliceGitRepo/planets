# Surface Roughness Measurement by Ultrasonic Sensing for In-Process Monitoring

- **Source:** Shin 1995 roughness ultrasonic in-process
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1MVo1wiPBVAjiYPZofjOLR7bFbKgfrup0/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Shin, Oh & Coker, 1995 (Journal of Engineering for Industry, 117, 439–447; ASME)
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Presents a water-immersion focused ultrasonic surface roughness measurement technique for in-process monitoring of machined surfaces. Uses focused 5 and 10 MHz transducers at non-zero incidence angle (~10°). The dynamic (varying) component of the echoed intensity as the surface is scanned reveals surface undulation and periodic feed marks. Statistical parameters Ra and Rq are extracted and compared against a profilometer. In-process demo performed on a CNC machining centre with a stream-coupling nozzle using coolant.

## Unique contribution
One of the earliest demonstrations of in-process, on-machine ultrasonic surface roughness monitoring during active machining (not just in water tank). Shows that non-zero incidence angle (10°) is essential — zero incidence gives poor roughness sensitivity for typical machined surface Ra. Extracts not only Ra/Rq but also the periodic feed-mark signature from the ultrasonic scan, providing surface profiling capability without contact. Demonstrates that machining coolant can serve as the coupling medium via a nozzle.

## Key concepts
- Focused ultrasonic transducer (5 and 10 MHz)
- Non-zero incidence angle (~10°) for roughness sensitivity
- In-process surface roughness monitoring
- Water-immersion and stream-coupling (nozzle) configurations
- Periodic feed mark extraction
- Ra and Rq extraction from ultrasonic scan
- Convolution of beam with surface profile
- CNC machining centre integration

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Transducers: 5 MHz and 10 MHz focused probes; focal spot diameter ≈ 0.308 mm at 10 MHz (matches few feed-mark periods); water tank or nozzle coupling.
- Specimens: aluminium, brass, stainless steel, alloy steel milled blocks; 5 different feeds (0.003–0.011 in./tooth); Ra = 0.2–4 μm.
- Key principle: non-zero incidence (~10°) → smooth surface reflects beam away; rough undulations redirect beam back → intensity proportional to local slope/height → amplitude variation encodes surface profile.
- Comparison (10 MHz): measured Ra vs profilometer Ra; excellent linear correlation for all 4 materials; two-segment linear relationship intersecting near Ra = 0.8 μm.
- Rq correlation also good (Fig. 16).
- Feed mark extraction: ultrasonic scan shows periodic intensity variations matching feed spacing — visible at 5 MHz for Ra > ~2 μm, at 10 MHz for Ra > ~0.8 μm.
- In-process (nozzle) test: results agree with immersion tank results (minor offset easily corrected); confirmed during actual cutting.
- Normal incidence comparison: Fig. 12 shows zero-incidence gives no periodic features; 10° incidence gives clear feed-mark pattern.
- Limitation: water/coolant coupling needed; not truly airborne; scanning required for profile; no correlation length extraction.
