# Speed of Sound in Air as a Function of Frequency and Humidity

- **Source:** Morfey 1980 speed sound frequency humidity
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1GH2pZWtor5OT3rVl-vYhPOo2eiot8fzO/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Morfey & Howell, 1980 (Journal of the Acoustical Society of America, 68(5), 1525–1527)
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Short letter deriving the frequency and humidity dependence of sound speed in air from the ANSI S1.26-1978 atmospheric absorption model via Kramers-Kronig relations. Extends equilibrium (zero-frequency) sound speed formula for ideal moist air mixtures. Compares predictions with Harris 1971 measurements at 292 and 503 Hz at 20°C and 1 atm. Agreement is good except at 10–20% RH where an unexplained discrepancy of ~150 ppm persists. Concludes that the initial sound speed dip at low RH (Harris 1971 result) is due to vibrational relaxation shifting the O₂ relaxation peak.

## Unique contribution
First paper to systematically derive sound speed dispersion (frequency dependence of c) from the same absorption model used for α, using Kramers-Kronig. Provides compact equations (eqs. 2–5) relating c_f, c_0, x_w through ideal-mixture γ and M formulas plus relaxation contribution. Explains the non-monotone c vs RH curve (velocity minimum at ~14% RH at 20°C) via relaxation: adding moisture shifts O₂ f_r upward, causing c to decrease at fixed audio frequency.

## Key concepts
- Speed of sound dispersion: c_f vs c_0 via Kramers-Kronig from attenuation
- Equilibrium speed c_0 in moist air: (c_0/c_a)² = (γ_h/M_h)/(γ_a/M_a); water vapour increases c_0
- Vibrational relaxation of O₂ and N₂: causes frequency-dependent dispersion in audio range
- ANSI S1.26-1978 model as basis for O₂ and N₂ relaxation parameters
- Humidity paradox: adding H₂O first decreases then increases c at fixed audio frequency
- O₂ relaxation frequency f_r increases with humidity → dispersion dip at low RH
- Comparison: Harris 1971 measurements at 292 and 503 Hz, 20°C

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Method: for each relaxation process, c_f − c_0 derived from Kramers-Kronig with single-relaxation α(f); contributions summed.
- Equilibrium speed (zero frequency): c_0/c_ref = √[(γ_h M_a)/(γ_a M_h)]; γ_w = 1.3288, γ_a = 1.4007, M_w/M_a = 0.6220.
- Results Fig. 1: predictions agree with Harris 1971 data within ±0.00015 (i.e., ±150 ppm) for h ≥ 30% RH; discrepancy at 10–20% RH.
- Possible discrepancy causes investigated (Fig. 2): modifying O₂ relaxation frequency or vibrational heat capacity does not resolve the low-RH discrepancy.
- Conclusion: method of calculating c from absorption via Kramers-Kronig is reliable; low-RH discrepancy (~150 ppm) may reflect inaccuracy in ANSI S1.26 O₂ relaxation parameters or experimental error.
- Limitation: 3-page letter; only 20°C and 1 atm; no temperature extrapolation; later superseded by Cramer 1993 for equilibrium co.
