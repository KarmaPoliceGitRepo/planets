# Applications of Airborne Ultrasound in Human–Computer Interaction

- **Source:** Dahl 2014 airborne ultrasound human computer interaction
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1Gj0AK730SdrYPh0K9wk4jNqjrBiaYXtE/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Dahl, 2014 (Ultrasonics 54, 2541–2548)
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Reviews and demonstrates the use of airborne ultrasound for gesture control and tactile feedback in human-computer interaction. Covers Doppler-based gesture detection, TOF-based hand tracking, and focused ultrasound for mid-air tactile sensation (acoustic radiation pressure on skin). Reviews commercial and research systems from 2010–2014.

## Unique contribution
Synthesises HCI-specific use cases for airborne ultrasound (gestures, touchless interaction, mid-air haptics) with emphasis on practical transducer arrays and signal processing. Connects acoustic radiation pressure (levitation / tactile) with gesture sensing in one overview.

## Key concepts
- Airborne ultrasound HCI
- Gesture recognition (Doppler)
- Hand tracking (TOF)
- Mid-air haptics / tactile feedback
- Acoustic radiation pressure
- Ultrasound phased arrays
- Focused ultrasound (levitation)
- Touchless interaction

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Doppler approach: CW or pseudo-CW transmitter; received signal frequency-shifted by moving hand; provides gesture velocity.
- TOF hand tracking: pulsed system, multiple receivers for 3D localization; similar to sonar-based navigation.
- Mid-air haptics: focused array (e.g., UltraHaptics system) produces radiation pressure ~1 Pa at hand; perceivable as tactile sensation at ~200 Hz modulation.
- Acoustic radiation pressure F = 2αI/c (travelling wave) or E (standing wave); focused array optimizes pressure at focal point.
- Application examples: gesture control of media players, touchless surgical theatre interfaces, 3D tactile shape rendering.
