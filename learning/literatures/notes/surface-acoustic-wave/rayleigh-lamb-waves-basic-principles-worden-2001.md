## Overview
A tutorial article aimed at NDE practitioners that reviews the physics of Rayleigh and Lamb wave propagation, starting from bulk waves in infinite media and building up to the guided wave solutions for semi-infinite half-spaces and plates. Practical transducer methods (comb, interdigital, wedge) and their design principles are explained. The article concludes with a summary of how these guided wave types are exploited for non-destructive evaluation (NDE).

## Unique contribution
Provides a concise, accessible derivation of both Rayleigh and Lamb wave characteristic equations together with practical NDE context — making it an ideal introductory reference. Dispersion curves for aluminium are computed using a Newton-Raphson root finder and presented for symmetric (S) and antisymmetric (A) Lamb modes. The discussion of Snell's law for wedge transducers and the role of the frequency-thickness product is especially practical.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- In an unbounded solid, P (dilatational) and S (shear) waves do not interact; adding a free surface couples them into Rayleigh waves.
- Rayleigh wave velocity cR is the sole real root of the cubic characteristic equation and is ~0.92cT for metals; it is non-dispersive.
- Amplitude decays to ~10% of surface value at one wavelength depth; geometric spreading goes as 1/√R.
- Lamb waves in a plate of half-thickness d are characterised by symmetric and antisymmetric families; their phase velocity depends on ωd (frequency-thickness product), not frequency alone — hence they are dispersive.
- At low ωd only S₀ and A₀ exist; new modes appear as ωd increases.
- For wedge excitation: sin θ = cL_wedge/cR_structure, providing a practical angle calculation.
- Mode conversion at faults (e.g., A₀ reflecting as S₀) complicates time-of-flight interpretation but is manageable with careful mode selection.
