# Enhancement of Ultrasonic Surface Waves at Wedge Tips and Angled Defects

## Overview
A short letter in Applied Physics Letters investigating the near-field behaviour of Rayleigh waves incident on wedge-shaped defects and angled surface-breaking cracks. The authors use laser ultrasonics and finite element modelling (FEM with PZFlex) on aluminium samples to show that signal enhancement in the near-field — previously noted for cracks normal to a surface — is greatly amplified for shallow-angle defects, with out-of-plane enhancements exceeding 40× the incident signal in simulations.

## Unique contribution
Provides a quantitative mechanistic explanation for the very large signal enhancement at shallow-angle defects by relating it to mode conversion: as the Rayleigh wave propagates into the thinning wedge region, the frequency–thickness product decreases and the wave transitions from Rayleigh-like to antisymmetric (A0) and symmetric (S0) Lamb-like modes. The characteristic dispersive spreading of arrival times, computed via group-velocity integration along wedge sections, matches both modelled and experimental B-scans in excellent detail, and a short-time Fourier transform confirms the frequency-dependent arrival structure.

## Key concepts
- Rayleigh wave near-field enhancement
- Wedge tip mode conversion
- Frequency–thickness product (f·d)
- Lamb wave A0 and S0 modes
- Surface-breaking defect detection
- Rolling contact fatigue geometry
- Laser ultrasound (thermoelastic Nd:YAG source, two-wave mixer interferometer)
- B-scan imaging of wave patterns
- Non-destructive testing (NDT)

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Aluminium samples with machined slots at various angles (to ~25°) and a 10° wedge were studied.
- Group velocity cg = cp²/[cp – (f·d)(dcp/d(f·d))] was used to compute dispersive arrival times; agreement with FEM and experiment is excellent.
- For a normal-to-surface slot, in-plane enhancement dominates due to constructive interference with reflected and mode-converted SSLW components. For shallow angled defects, the A0 mode dominates the out-of-plane enhancement because of its larger out-of-plane displacement at low f·d.
- Enhancement magnitude depends strongly on measurement component (in-plane vs. out-of-plane); out-of-plane detection of A0 gives the largest value for shallow angles.
- Practical implication: a scanned detection system is preferred over a scanned source system for identifying and characterising angled defects in NDT.
