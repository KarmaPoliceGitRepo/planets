## Overview
A journal paper (IEEE Trans. Instrumentation and Measurement, vol. 58, no. 5, 2009) presenting a non-invasive method to measure internal pressure in vessels without drilling holes. The technique exploits the acoustoelastic effect: pressure-induced stresses in a vessel wall alter the transit times of both Rayleigh waves and critically-refracted longitudinal (LCR) waves travelling along the vessel surface. By combining both wave types in a "reference method," temperature effects are cancelled without needing a thermometer, and pressure can be calculated from the differential transit-time delay.

## Unique contribution
The key innovation is a combined dual-element transducer (one element for Rayleigh at 65° incidence, one for LCR at 27°) that samples almost the same material volume, minimising errors from uneven temperature distribution. The derived "reference time delay" Δt_ref = ΔtR − K·ΔtL is shown to be proportional to pressure alone, with temperature influence eliminated through calibration of the constant K.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Thin-shell theory gives circumferential stress σθ = pR/δ and axial stress σφ = pR/2δ; both affect wave velocity via acoustoelastic coefficients.
- Temperature changes wave velocity and propagation distance; at 1°C ≈ 1 MPa worth of time-delay shift, so temperature compensation is non-negotiable.
- Calibration on a Q235B steel air vessel (diameter ~1.4 m, wall 4 mm, max 0.4 MPa) gave K = 1.34 and M = −17.8 (units: ns and MPa), leading to the working equation: ΔtR − 1.34·ΔtL = −17.8·p.
- Maximum absolute pressure error was 0.07 MPa in validation tests.
- Combined transducers showed smaller coefficient-of-variation for K than four separate transducers.
- Method is limited to thin-walled vessels (thickness/radius < 0.1); requires recalibration for different materials or transducer spacings.
