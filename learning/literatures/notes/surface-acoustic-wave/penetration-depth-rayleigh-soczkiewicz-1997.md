# The Penetration Depth of the Rayleigh Surface Waves

- **Source:** THE PENETRATION DEPTH OF THE RAYLEIGH SURFACE WAVES - (2007) E Soczkiewicz.pdf

- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** E. Soczkiewicz (Silesian Technical University) / 1997 (published in Nondestructive Testing and Evaluation 13:2, 113–119)
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
A short analytical paper that numerically tabulates the penetration depths of sagittally polarized Rayleigh waves in isotropic half-spaces, parameterised by Poisson's ratio σ and the velocity ratios cR/cL and cR/ct. Two penetration depth scales are computed: (kβL)⁻¹ associated with the longitudinal decay factor, and (kβt)⁻¹ associated with the transverse decay factor. Displacement amplitude profiles (U₁ and U₃ components) are plotted versus depth for a range of Poisson ratios.

## Unique contribution
Provides an explicit numerical look-up table for Rayleigh wave penetration depths across the full range of physically realizable Poisson ratios (−1 ≤ σ ≤ 0.5), including materials with negative Poisson's ratio. Previously the dispersion relation solution was only given graphically in the literature.

## Key concepts
- Rayleigh wave penetration depth
- Sagittally polarized surface wave
- Poisson's ratio dependence
- Dispersion relation for Rayleigh waves
- Longitudinal and transverse decay factors (βL, βt)
- Displacement amplitude vs. depth profile
- Semi-infinite isotropic elastic half-space

## Methods / results / takeaways
- The two penetration depths are (kβL)⁻¹ and (kβt)⁻¹; they are in units of inverse wavenumber (i.e., fractions of wavelength).
- (kβt)⁻¹ always increases with σ; (kβL)⁻¹ increases with σ for σ < 0, decreases for 0 < σ < 0.5.
- For common metals: penetration depths range from about 0.176–0.191 wavelengths for the longitudinal component and 0.376–0.487 for the transverse component.
- Displacement amplitudes U₁ and U₃ practically vanish by x₃ ≈ 3λ below the surface — confirming the surface-localised nature of Rayleigh waves.
- The paper's table enables direct computation of penetration depth from material properties, avoiding graphical estimation.
