## Overview
A graduate-level seismology lecture covering the theory of surface waves (Rayleigh and Love waves) and dispersive wave propagation from first principles. Starting from the wave equation in elastic half-spaces, it derives the Rayleigh wave characteristic equation and the retrograde elliptical particle motion, then develops the Love wave dispersion relation for a layer over a half-space. Group velocity is derived from the concept of two superimposed harmonic waves and related to the slope of the dispersion curve.

## Unique contribution
Provides a self-contained, mathematically rigorous derivation of both Rayleigh and Love wave solutions using stress-free boundary conditions, including explicit treatment of modal dispersion (fundamental mode and overtones). The discussion of how energy decays as 1/√r for surface waves vs. 1/r² for body waves directly explains why surface waves dominate earthquake seismograms.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Rayleigh waves in a homogeneous half-space satisfy a cubic secular equation; for a Poisson solid (λ = μ) cR ≈ 0.9194β (where β is shear velocity).
- Rayleigh waves are non-dispersive in a homogeneous half-space; dispersion arises in layered media.
- Love waves require a velocity increase with depth (β₁ < cL < β₂); they are inherently dispersive and can exist in multiple modes for a given frequency.
- Group velocity U = dω/dk = c + k(dc/dk); when velocity increases with frequency (normal dispersion) U < c, and vice versa.
- Surface waves decay geometrically as 1/√r (cylindrical spreading), making them dominant at teleseismic distances.
- Retrograde elliptical particle motion at the surface reverses to prograde at depth due to the P-component decaying faster than SV.
