## Overview
A 5-page technical note in Applied Acoustics that models the nonlinear acoustic wave fields generated by a focused transducer operating in pulse-echo mode. Using the multi-Gaussian beam (MGB) model applied to the KZK (Khokhlov–Zabolotskaya–Kuznetsov) equation under the quasilinear approximation, the authors compute the magnitudes and phases of the received average fundamental and second harmonic waves after reflection from rigid and stress-free boundaries, then verify the results experimentally.

## Unique contribution
Previous nonlinear focused-transducer studies mostly used pitch-catch setups; this work specifically addresses pulse-echo mode and a key finding distinguishes it: the phase difference between the newly generated second harmonic and the reflected second harmonic is always approximately π/2, regardless of boundary type (rigid or stress-free). This means the total reflected second harmonic is neither strongly enhanced nor cancelled — behavior fundamentally different from plane transducers — enabling stable nonlinear measurements in pulse-echo mode.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Transducer modeled: radius a = 4.75 mm, focal length fc = 25.4 mm in water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³, c = 1482 m/s, β = 3.5); attenuation α₁ = 0.31 Np/m at 3.5 MHz, α₂ = 1.24 Np/m at 7 MHz; excitation p₀ = 10 MPa.
- Reflector placed at z₀ = fc (focal plane); two boundary types: stress-free (Rf = −1) and rigid (Rf = +1).
- Key simulation result: phase differences between newly generated and reflected second harmonics are ≈ π/2 for both boundary types, yielding stable intermediate total second harmonic amplitudes (neither fully enhanced nor cancelled).
- In contrast, for a planar (unfocused) transducer: rigid boundary strongly enhances, stress-free boundary almost cancels the second harmonic — the focused geometry fundamentally changes this behavior.
- Experiments used a 9.5 mm diameter, 25.4 mm focal length, 5 MHz Panametrics V326-SU transducer, a 3.5 MHz tone burst, and three reflectors (steel-304, aluminum-6061, air); normalized relative nonlinearity β′ measured from second harmonic amplitude vs. fundamental squared.
- Good agreement between simulation and experiment: normalized β′ for water-air = 1.32 (sim) / 1.22 (meas), water-aluminum = 1.08 / 1.07, water-steel = 1 / 1 (reference).
- Practical implication: pulse-echo nonlinear NDE with focused beams is feasible; absolute pressure/nonlinearity calibration remains future work.
