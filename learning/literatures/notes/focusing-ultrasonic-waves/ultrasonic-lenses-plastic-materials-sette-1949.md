## Overview
A brief conference abstract (one page) from the 36th Meeting of the Acoustical Society of America, published in JASA 21(1), 1949. Sette reports measurements of acoustic propagation velocity and attenuation in synthetic plastic materials to identify the best material for ultrasonic lenses, and presents early results on focusing performance of plane-cylindrical and plane-spherical lens geometries.

## Unique contribution
The earliest document in this folder, establishing that polymethylmethacrylate (Plexiglas/PMMA) is favorable for ultrasonic lenses due to its relatively low acoustic impedance mismatch with common coupling liquids and its manageable attenuation at megahertz frequencies. Quantifies the energy concentration achievable: a plane-cylindrical Plexiglas lens reduces the required sound power for a given focal energy density by 10×, a plane-spherical lens by 100×. Also introduces the use of a protective liquid-filled envelope with a mica window to protect the plastic lens from organic solvents.

## Key concepts
- Ultrasonic lens
- Polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA / Plexiglas)
- Longitudinal wave velocity in rods vs. bulk
- Specific acoustic resistance (acoustic impedance)
- Plane-cylindrical lens geometry
- Plane-spherical lens geometry
- Energy focusing / sound power concentration
- Acoustic attenuation at high frequency
- Coupling liquid (water, carbon disulfide)
- Mica window (acoustic transmission window)

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Propagation velocity and attenuation measured in multiple synthetic materials to find the best lens candidate.
- Plexiglas (PMMA): longitudinal velocity in rods = 2100 m/s; bulk wave velocity = 2820 m/s; specific acoustic resistance = 3.3 × 10⁵ g·cm⁻²·s⁻¹; notable attenuation at 8 MHz (must be accounted for in lens design).
- Preferred geometries: plane-cylindrical and plane-spherical, with the plane face on the incident wave side.
- Energy focusing factor: 10× (cylindrical), 100× (spherical) reduction in required source power for a given focal intensity.
- Solubility of plastics in organic liquids addressed by housing the lens in a sealed envelope with a mica transmission window filled with a compatible coupling liquid (water or carbon disulfide recommended).
- Analytical treatment of lens behavior was found to agree reasonably well with measured results.
- Historical note: at the time (1949), this represents one of the earliest systematic studies of solid acoustic lenses for focusing ultrasound in liquid media.
