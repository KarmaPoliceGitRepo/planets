## Overview
An experimental study investigating how prior austenite grain size (varied by different austenitizing temperatures) affects the mechanical properties of boron-containing low-alloy wear-resistant steel. Characterizes microstructure (EBSD, LOM), hardness, tensile properties, impact toughness, and fracture morphology.

## Unique contribution
Demonstrates the coupled influence of austenite grain size and martensite packet size on brittleness/toughness tradeoffs in boron steels, showing that while larger austenite grains increase hardenability (beneficial), they reduce toughness through coarser martensite packet structure — quantified here for a commercial wear plate steel grade.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Austenitizing at 850–1050°C for 15 min; quenched to produce martensite; tempered at 150°C.
- Grain size by EBSD and Heyn method: ranges from 15 µm (850°C) to 85 µm (1050°C).
- Hardness: 450–500 HBW, relatively insensitive to grain size in this range.
- Impact toughness: decreases from ~35 J (small grain) to ~18 J (large grain) at room temperature; DBTT shift of ~30°C.
- Tensile strength: YS and UTS essentially unchanged; elongation decreases slightly for large grains.
- Fracture: small grains → mixed ductile/brittle; large grains → quasi-cleavage fracture.
- Martensite packet size scales ~linearly with austenite grain size; packet size controls cleavage facet size.
