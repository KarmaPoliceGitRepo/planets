## Overview
A brief experimental and theoretical investigation of the coefficient of ultrasonic absorption in sodium chloride (NaCl) single crystals as a function of applied static load. The paper is a key early experimental demonstration that dislocation loop behaviour (elastic vs plastic regime) can be directly probed by ultrasonic attenuation measurements. It compares ultrasonic absorption data with optical measurements of dislocation density.

## Unique contribution
Merkulov demonstrates two distinct regimes of dislocation-acoustic interaction in NaCl crystals:
1. **Elastic (small) loads**: absorption coefficient increases as the square of the applied stress, with full temporal recovery upon load removal. This is attributed to reversible increase in the effective acting length of dislocation loops (consistent with the Granato-Lücke string model — pinned dislocation loops resonating at acoustic frequencies).
2. **Plastic (large) loads**: absorption increases sharply and is conditioned by the generation of new dislocations; no recovery occurs. The onset of this regime (the threshold for dislocation multiplication) is detectable by the transition in the absorption–stress relationship, providing a non-destructive ultrasonic criterion for the onset of plastic yielding in NaCl crystals.

The data are verified by comparison with optical measurements of dislocation density, establishing a quantitative link between acoustic absorption and dislocation microstructure.

## Methods / results / takeaways

### Experimental design
NaCl single crystal specimens were subjected to controlled static loads while ultrasonic attenuation was measured at one or more frequencies (likely in the 1–10 MHz range, consistent with 1962 practice). Two regimes were studied by varying load magnitude.

### Elastic load regime (sub-yield)
- Attenuation increase Δα is proportional to the square of the applied stress: Δα ∝ σ².
- On removing the load, full temporal recovery of attenuation to the pre-load value occurs.
- Physical interpretation: static stress increases the effective length L of pinned dislocation segments (by bowing out or unpinning weakly pinned segments), which increases their cross-section for resonant interaction with acoustic waves. Since attenuation in the Granato-Lücke model scales as Λ·L⁴ (Rayleigh regime, Λ = dislocation density), the quadratic stress dependence arises from L ∝ σ contributions or from the stress-dependent segment length distribution.
- The paper describes this as variation of the "acting length" of dislocation loops — consistent with the concept of effective loop length in the string model.

### Plastic load regime (above yield)
- At loads exceeding the elastic limit, absorption increases sharply and depends strongly on the applied load.
- The source of increased absorption shifts from reversible loop elongation to irreversible creation of new dislocation segments and multiplication.
- No temporal recovery: the new dislocations are stable after load removal.
- The transition between the elastic and plastic attenuation behaviours provides a non-invasive, in-situ criterion for the onset of plastic deformation.

### Cross-validation with optics
- Dislocation density Λ was independently measured by optical etch-pit counting on the same specimens.
- The ultrasonic absorption data are compared with Λ from optics, providing quantitative agreement and supporting the dislocation-based interpretation of the absorption changes.

### Significance and limitations
- This is an early (1962) direct experimental validation that ultrasonic attenuation can probe dislocation state in ionic crystals.
- NaCl is a model system because it can be deformed controllably, has well-separated glide systems, and etch-pit counting is reliable.
- The English-language source available is only the abstract (Ultrasonics 1963, abstract 68); full quantitative data (curves, equations, numbers of specimens, exact frequencies) are in the original Russian paper and cannot be verified from this file.
- Note on prior note: Earlier note stated "loop length L₀ ~ 0.5–2 µm for freshly deformed crystals" — this specific number was inferred from general Granato-Lücke context, not from this abstract, and should be treated as unverified from this source.
