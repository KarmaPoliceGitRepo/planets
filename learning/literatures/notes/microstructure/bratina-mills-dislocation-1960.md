## Overview
ASA meeting abstract (H4) on deformation processes in annealed polycrystalline low- and high-carbon steel investigated with MHz longitudinal ultrasound, with the dislocation component of attenuation as the primary interest. External magnetic fields suppress the ferromagnetic (magnetoelastic/domain-wall) attenuation component so the dislocation part can be observed in isolation.

## Unique contribution
Early experimental demonstration of the **magnetic-field-suppression technique** in steel: saturate the specimen with a DC field to kill domain-wall losses, then interpret attenuation changes during and after tensile straining in terms of dislocation kinetics — generation, annihilation, anchoring, and pinning of dislocation loops (companion paper: Bratina, Martius & Mills, J. Appl. Phys. 31, 241(S), 1960).

## Key concepts
- Dislocation attenuation (Granato–Lücke string damping)
- Magnetoelastic / domain-wall attenuation
- Magnetic saturation suppression method
- Attenuation during plastic straining; recovery (attenuation vs time after strain)
- Internal friction in steel

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Method: send MHz longitudinal waves through a tensile specimen in a "hard" (stiff) tensile machine; record attenuation vs strain with and without a saturating DC magnetic field; field-on residual = dislocation + scattering, field-off minus field-on = magnetoelastic part.
- Attenuation changes during straining track active-dislocation population (multiplication on yielding, pinning/anchoring on hold).
- Time-resolved attenuation after straining probes pinning kinetics (point defects migrating to fresh dislocations — strain-ageing in carbon steel).
- Adjacent abstracts on the same page, relevant to this corpus: H3 (McSkimin, Bell Labs) — pulse velocity measurement to 1 part in 5000 by correcting the transducer-reflection phase shift, also estimating cement-bond thickness; H2 — Pippard electron-gas attenuation theory at low temperature.
