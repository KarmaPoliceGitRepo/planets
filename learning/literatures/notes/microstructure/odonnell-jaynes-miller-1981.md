# Kramers-Kronig Relationship Between Ultrasonic Attenuation and Phase Velocity

- **Source:** O'Donnell, Jaynes, Miller JASA 69 696 1981

## Overview
The full journal paper following the 1978 letter, providing a rigorous derivation of Kramers-Kronig relations for linear acoustic systems using linear response theory, and deriving approximate nearly-local forms (Eq. 30a/30b) relating attenuation to local dC/df. Validated on single relaxation, CoSO₄ solution, polyethylene, and normal dog myocardium.

## Unique contribution
Complete theoretical derivation from first principles (linear response theory, causality) of exact and approximate attenuation-dispersion relations for acoustics; provides the mathematical justification for the near-local Bode-like approximations; validated across four physically distinct acoustic systems demonstrating broad applicability.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Derivation: K(ω) = K₁(ω) + iK₂(ω) for acoustic compressibility; causality → Kramers-Kronig integrals (Eqs. 10, 11).
- Weak attenuation limit: C(ω) = [ρ₀K₁(ω)]^{-1/2}; α(ω) = ρ₀C(ω)ωK₂(ω)/2 (Eq. 15).
- Near-local form (Eq. 30): α(ω) ≈ (π/2) × [ω²/C³(ω)] × dC/dω, with integral form for ΔC.
- Validated for single relaxation: near-local form agrees with exact to within ~5% away from resonance.
- CoSO₄ (1 mol/L): measured α(f) with two relaxations; predicted ΔC agrees with measured to ~10%.
- Polyethylene: α nearly linear in f; ΔC nearly logarithmic — confirmed within 5%.
- Dog myocardium: ΔC from 1 to 10 MHz predicted ~2.5 m/s total; validates near-local form in soft tissue.
- Key conclusion: demonstrations that α and C are not independent measurements in linear, causal systems.
