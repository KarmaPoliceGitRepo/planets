# Ultrasonic Attenuation Caused by Scattering in Metals

## Overview
A comprehensive experimental and theoretical study of ultrasonic attenuation due to grain scattering in polycrystalline metals (Ni, 416 stainless steel bcc, 440C stainless steel fcc, brass). Uses the Lifshitz-Parkhomovskii and Merkulov theory with mode conversion included. Extracts grain size distributions from attenuation spectra using matrix inversion and shows the importance of shear mode contribution.

## Unique contribution
Demonstrates for the first time that mode conversion (longitudinal→shear scattering) accounts for ~80% of total Rayleigh attenuation in metals — correcting the oversimplification of including only longitudinal→longitudinal scattering — and validates the complete theory against multiple metal types.

## Key concepts
- Grain scattering with mode conversion
- Lifshitz-Parkhomovskii-Merkulov theory
- Mode conversion coefficient
- Rayleigh attenuation in polycrystals
- Ni, stainless steel, brass measurements
- Grain size distribution inversion
- Papadakis diffraction correction
- W anisotropy factor

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Immersion pulse-echo; 5–100 MHz range; diffraction correction applied using Papadakis near-field formula.
- Theory: α_total = α_LL + α_LT where α_LT (longitudinal→transverse) is ~4× α_LL for typical metals; total ~5× larger than LL alone.
- Ni: measured α at various grain sizes; matrix inversion gives distribution p(D); agrees with metallographic grain counts within ~15%.
- 416 SS (bcc): high W anisotropy; measured α consistent with theory including mode conversion.
- 440C SS (fcc): lower anisotropy; good agreement.
- Brass: intermediate anisotropy; single-crystal elastic constants used to compute W.
- Key formula: α = (8/3)π⁴f⁴D³(W_LL + W_LT)/(v_L⁵); mode-conversion term W_LT ~ 4W_LL for most metals.
- First explicit demonstration of shear mode dominance in Rayleigh attenuation of polycrystals.
