## Overview
A landmark early paper measuring ultrasonic attenuation in aluminum rods and glasses at 2–15 MHz, identifying for the first time the f⁴ (Rayleigh) grain scattering law in polycrystalline metals and distinguishing it from viscous absorption. Presents a theoretical formula for attenuation due to scattering by anisotropic grains of cubic symmetry.

## Unique contribution
First experimental demonstration that the dominant attenuation mechanism in polycrystalline aluminum at MHz frequencies is Rayleigh scattering from grains (not viscosity), and first quantitative comparison with grain scattering theory. Establishes the landmark observation that grain size correlates with attenuation, and that f² scattering occurs for grain sizes > λ/3.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Measurements on two Al rod specimens (grain size 0.23 mm and 0.13 mm) and two glass rods.
- Glass shows f² attenuation (viscous); Al shows f⁴ (Rayleigh scattering).
- Rayleigh scattering formula for cubic polycrystal: α = (ωs⁴/v³)·(4πa³/3)·ρ_g where ωs = scattering strength ∝ W²·f⁴.
- W (anisotropy factor) = 2(S₁₁ − S₁₂ − S₄₄/2) for cubic crystals; key material parameter.
- Grain size ratio between two Al specimens (1.77) agrees with ratio of attenuation coefficients (≈1.77³ ≈ 5.5 at low f).
- At higher frequencies where D > λ/3: transition to f² stochastic behavior observed.
- Mode conversion not yet considered; later work (Papadakis 1965) showed shear mode accounts for ~80% of Rayleigh attenuation.
- Absorption in glass: classical viscous α ∝ f²; no grain scattering contribution.
