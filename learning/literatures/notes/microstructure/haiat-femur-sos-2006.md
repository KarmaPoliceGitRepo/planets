## Overview
An experimental and numerical study investigating how frequency-dependent attenuation and velocity dispersion affect speed of sound (SOS) measurements in intact human femur specimens. Compares SOS values computed using group velocity and first-zero-crossing methods on 38 femurs; introduces an attenuation-compensation method and evaluates correlation with X-ray density.

## Unique contribution
Demonstrates that SOS at the femur depends significantly on which signal processing marker is used (group velocity vs. first zero crossing) due to attenuation-induced pulse distortion; derives a correction method and shows that first-zero-crossing SOS better correlates with bone density because it uses early signal information less affected by multipath interference.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- 38 intact human femur specimens tested by through-transmission at 0.5–2 MHz.
- SOS_group and SOS_ZC differ by up to 80 m/s due to frequency-dependent attenuation distortion.
- Numerical simulation explains: attenuation shifts spectral centroid toward low frequencies, delaying apparent pulse arrival.
- New attenuation-compensation method: estimates frequency-dependent delay shift from BUA; corrects transit time → reduces difference between methods to <20 m/s.
- Correlation with X-ray density: SOS_ZC shows r² = 0.72; SOS_group r² = 0.55; SOS_ZC preferred.
- Conclusion: for proximal femur, first-zero-crossing measurement is recommended as standardized clinical technique.
