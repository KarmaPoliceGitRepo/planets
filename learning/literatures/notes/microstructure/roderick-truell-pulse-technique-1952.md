# The Measurement of Ultrasonic Attenuation in Solids by the Pulse Technique and Some Results in Steel

- **Source:** Roderick and Truell JASA 23 267 1952

## Overview
An early experimental paper measuring longitudinal wave attenuation in steel specimens over 5–50 MHz using the pulse technique, with careful diffraction (beam spreading) corrections. Compares results for furnace-cooled (large grain) and water-quenched (fine grain) specimens to test grain scattering theory predictions.

## Unique contribution
One of the first careful pulse-technique attenuation measurements in steel with beam-spreading correction, demonstrating that furnace-cooled steel shows Rayleigh scattering (α ∝ f⁴) while quenched steel shows stochastic-like (α ∝ f²) behavior due to different grain sizes, with a factor of ~50 difference in da/df between the two heat treatments.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Pulse technique: transducer bonded to end of steel rod; reflected echoes from far end measured as function of frequency.
- Beam spreading correction based on piston diffraction theory; critical for accurate α extraction.
- Furnace-cooled specimen (grain size ~200 µm): α ∝ f⁴ at 5–20 MHz; transitions to f² at higher frequencies.
- Water-quenched specimen (grain size ~10 µm): α ∝ f² throughout 5–50 MHz range.
- da/df ratio between two specimens at same frequency: ~50× difference.
- Water buffer method also tested: transducer separated from specimen by water path, yielding same α values after correction.
- Results consistent with Lifshitz-Parkhomovskii (1951) grain scattering theory.
