## Overview
A doctoral thesis from Georgia Tech on the interaction of ultrasonic waves with periodically structured (grating-like) surfaces. Develops the Rayleigh-Fourier method for computing reflected and diffracted wave amplitudes from periodic surfaces, investigates the effect of surface imperfections on diffraction efficiency, and identifies the backward beam displacement phenomenon.

## Unique contribution
Establishes a rigorous Rayleigh-Fourier numerical framework for periodic surface diffraction in elastics, identifies the backward beam displacement phenomenon (beam center moves opposite to expected direction near grating resonances), and characterizes how random imperfections in the periodicity degrade diffraction orders — relevant to phononic crystal NDE and surface characterization.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Rayleigh-Fourier method: surface profile expanded in Fourier series; boundary conditions for each harmonic yield matrix equation for diffraction amplitudes.
- Validated against FE simulations and experimental measurements on machined aluminum gratings.
- Backward beam displacement: near specific Rayleigh anomaly frequencies, reflected beam center shifts in −x direction despite incident beam at +θ; explained by excitation of pseudo-surface wave.
- Effect of random imperfections: diffraction order amplitudes decrease as σ_surface increases; power redistributed into incoherent scatter.
- Imperfect periodicity model: amplitude degradation ∝ exp(−(2kσ)²); standard deviation σ must be ≪ λ for clean diffraction.
- Phononic crystal section: demonstrates ultrasonic bandgap behavior in periodic composite structures; measurement technique for bandgap center frequency and width.
- Applications to NDE: surface texture characterization; phononic crystal integrity inspection.
