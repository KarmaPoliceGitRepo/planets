## Overview
A study investigating transit-time-based speed of sound (SOS) measurements in bone (calcaneus), focusing on how frequency-dependent attenuation and velocity dispersion cause systematic errors in time-domain SOS estimation. Develops corrections using Gaussian pulse theory and validates on human calcaneus specimens.

## Unique contribution
Demonstrates analytically and experimentally that frequency-dependent attenuation distorts the apparent time of arrival of a pulse in bone, causing SOS to depend on which pulse marker (peak, zero crossing, threshold) is used; derives correction factors and shows that corrected SOS values agree across methods to within ~10 m/s.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Gaussian pulse model: transmitted pulse spectrum G(f) = G₀ exp(−(f−f₀)²/2σ²); attenuation shifts center frequency, causing apparent pulse delay.
- Shift in apparent arrival time: Δt = α'/(2πσ²) where α' = dα/df (slope of attenuation) and σ = spectral bandwidth.
- Correction formula derived for peak and zero-crossing markers; applied to 38 calcaneal measurements.
- Uncorrected SOS range: 1490–1540 m/s; corrected values converge to ~40–50 m/s narrower range.
- BUA and SOS show significant correlation (r ~ 0.85) across specimens.
- Practical recommendation: first zero crossing method most robust to attenuation-induced distortion at proximal femur; see Haiat 2006 for femur.
