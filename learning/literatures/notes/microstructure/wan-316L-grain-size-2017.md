## Overview
Experimental study measuring broadband ultrasonic attenuation (5–50 MHz) in 316L austenitic stainless steel specimens heat-treated to achieve different grain sizes (10–200 µm). Compares measured attenuation with Stanke-Kino unified theory predictions and explores the feasibility of grain size determination from attenuation measurements for quality control applications.

## Unique contribution
Provides a comprehensive dataset for 316L SS covering a wide grain size range, validating the Stanke-Kino theory across Rayleigh and stochastic scattering regimes, and demonstrating that the theory prediction accuracy degrades for very large grains (>100 µm) where multiple scattering and grain boundary reflection become significant.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Specimens annealed at 1050–1200°C for 1–8 hours; grain sizes 10–200 µm by EBSD and metallography.
- Immersion pulse-echo; broadband signals; attenuation extracted from spectral ratio method.
- Rayleigh regime (f < ~15 MHz for large grains): α ∝ f⁴D³ confirmed.
- Stochastic regime (f > 25 MHz for large grains): α ∝ f²D behavior seen.
- Theory fit: elastic anisotropy A = C₄₄ − (C₁₁−C₁₂)/2 used; for 316L A ≈ 3.1.
- Discrepancy for D > 100 µm: theory underestimates by 20–30% — attributed to multiple scattering not included in Born approximation.
- Grain size inversion error: ±10% for D < 50 µm; ±20–30% for larger grains.
