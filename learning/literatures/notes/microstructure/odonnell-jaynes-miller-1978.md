# General Relationships Between Ultrasonic Attenuation and Dispersion

## Overview
A short letter presenting general Kramers-Kronig/causality relations between ultrasonic attenuation α(f) and phase velocity dispersion C(f) for any linear acoustic system. Derives approximate "nearly local" forms of these relations using a Bode analogy, valid when α and C vary slowly with frequency. Validated on hemoglobin solutions.

## Unique contribution
First application of Kramers-Kronig dispersion relations explicitly to ultrasound, with practical near-local approximations suitable for soft tissue and materials measurements; demonstrates that attenuation and dispersion are not independent experimental signatures but are related by fundamental physics (causality), making it invalid to use their independent agreement to validate a specific mechanism.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Exact (non-local) Kramers-Kronig: C(ω) determined from α(ω') at all frequencies (integral relation).
- Nearly local approximation: ΔC(ω) ≈ (2/π) × ∫α(ω')dω' (Eq. 6), valid when α and C vary slowly.
- Table I: if α ∝ ω², ΔC ∝ ω (classical viscous); if α ∝ ω (soft tissue), ΔC ∝ ln(ω).
- Validation on hemoglobin (30 and 60 g/100 cm³): α(f) measured by Carstensen-Schwan; ΔC predicted from Eq. 6; excellent agreement with their measured dispersion.
- Key implication: a model that correctly predicts both α and C independently is not more validated than one that predicts only α; agreement of both merely confirms causality+linearity.
- Follow-on full paper: O'Donnell, Jaynes, Miller JASA 69 (1981) gives complete derivation.
