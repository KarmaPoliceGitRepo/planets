## Overview
A doctoral thesis from the University of Bath developing nonlinear ultrasonic techniques for detecting sub-surface damage in engineering structures. Covers bolted/adhesive joints, kissing bonds, and fatigue/impact damage. Reviews linear and nonlinear ultrasonic theory, then presents experimental implementations including resonant frequency shifts, second harmonic generation, and nonlinear wave modulation spectroscopy (NWMS).

## Unique contribution
Develops an integrated nonlinear US toolkit combining multiple nonlinear signatures (β, higher harmonics, subharmonics, modulation) for detection of contact-type defects (kissing bonds, clamped cracks) that are nearly transparent to linear methods; demonstrates application to both metallic and composite structures.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Theory review: linear propagation; nonlinear elasticity and β parameter; dislocation-induced nonlinearity; contact acoustic nonlinearity (CAN).
- NWMS method: two frequencies (f₁ low, f₂ high) insonified; damage at contact generates sum/difference frequency f₂ ± nf₁; amplitude of sidebands indicates defect severity.
- Kissing bond detection: NWMS gives 10–20 dB higher sideband level vs. undamaged; linear methods show <1 dB difference.
- Fatigue: β increases monotonically with fatigue life fraction; saturation near final fracture.
- Impact damage in CFRP composites: nonlinear imaging with NWMS localizes delaminations; linear C-scan misses partial-closure cracks.
- Bolted joint loosening: resonance frequency shifts and β both sensitive to clamp force reduction.
- Practical limitation: nonlinear measurements require high-amplitude excitation; structural nonlinearity (boundary conditions) can mask material nonlinearity.
