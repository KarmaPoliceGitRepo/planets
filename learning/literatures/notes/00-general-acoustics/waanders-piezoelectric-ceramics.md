## Overview
Philips application guide to PZT (Lead Zirconate Titanate) piezoelectric ceramics. Covers the physical basis of piezoelectricity, PZT material properties, piezoelectric constants (d, g, k), equivalent circuits, ageing/depolarization, and practical applications from sonic buzzers to medical ultrasound transducers and actuators.

## Unique contribution
Practical, application-oriented treatment of PZT by the manufacturer (Philips PXE series). Provides worked numerical examples (stress, voltage, strain calculations) and property tables for specific PZT grades, including hysteresis curves and ageing data. Excellent reference for transducer design with commercial ceramics.

## Key concepts
- Piezoelectric effect and inverse effect (Curie brothers, 1880)
- Ferroelectric materials, perovskite crystal structure (ABO₃)
- Curie temperature, poling treatment, Weiss domains
- Dielectric hysteresis (D-E loop)
- Piezoelectric constants: d (charge/force or strain/field), g (voltage/stress), k (coupling factor)
- Compliance s_ij, permittivity ε_ij
- Electromechanical coupling factor k_p, k_t, k₃₃, k₃₁
- PZT ageing (logarithmic time dependence)
- Electrical and mechanical depolarization
- Resonant and anti-resonant frequencies

## Methods / results / takeaways
The piezoelectric charge constant d₃₃ (field and strain along polarisation axis) is typically 200–600 pC/N for soft PZT grades, while d₃₁ (transverse) is about −1/2 of d₃₃. The coupling factor k measures the fraction of input energy converted: k² = (electrical energy stored)/(total mechanical energy input). Soft PZT (high d, low Q_m) suits sensors and low-power transducers; hard PZT (low d, high Q_m) suits high-power resonant devices. Curie temperatures range ~150–360°C for standard grades; safe operating temperature is roughly half the Curie point. Ageing follows log(t) causing properties to drift ≈1–2% per decade of time.
