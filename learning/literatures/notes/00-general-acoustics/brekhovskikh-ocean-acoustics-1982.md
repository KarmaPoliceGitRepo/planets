## Overview
A comprehensive theoretical treatment of underwater sound propagation covering the ocean as an acoustic medium, ray theory, reflection from sea surface and bottom, shallow-water propagation, the underwater sound channel (USC), range-dependent waveguides, scattering from rough surfaces, and propagation in random media. Balances theory with experimental context, prefacing each theoretical chapter with key observational facts.

## Unique contribution
Offers a rigorous theory of the underwater sound channel using both ray and mode representations, including the canonical SOFAR channel model. The parallel treatment of deterministic and stochastic phenomena (random ocean, rough surface scattering, bubbles) in a single volume provides a complete foundation for sonar modelling. The preface philosophy — that theory changes on decade timescales while observations change yearly — guides the book's structure.

## Methods / results / takeaways
Ray theory (Snell's law + eikonal equation) describes refraction in continuously stratified water; constant-gradient layers yield circular ray paths. Convergence zones appear at horizontal ranges ~60 km intervals in typical deep-ocean profiles. The SOFAR channel axis lies at the sound-velocity minimum (~1000 m depth), trapping energy over ocean-basin ranges. Normal modes quantise through a WKB phase integral: ∫k_z dz = nπ. The adiabatic approximation holds in a slowly varying range-dependent waveguide, conserving mode action; the parabolic equation method handles stronger range dependence. Rough-surface scattering is characterised by the Rayleigh parameter R = 2k σ cosθ (σ = rms roughness); for R ≪ 1 the surface appears smooth. Gas bubbles of radius a resonate near f₀ = (1/2πa)√(3γp₀/ρ) (Minnaert), producing strong scattering and attenuation in bubbly water and biological scattering layers.
