## Overview
Proceedings of the First International Symposium on Ultrasonic Materials Characterization, sponsored by ASNT, IEEE Sonics & Ultrasonics Group, NSF, ONR, and NBS. Marks the emergence of ultrasonic characterisation beyond simple defect detection, addressing grain size, texture, residual stress, mechanical properties of composites, and quantitative flaw sizing. 644 pages; covers five thematic sections with 30+ papers.

## Unique contribution
A landmark document in the transition of ultrasonics from qualitative defect detection to quantitative materials characterisation. First symposium devoted explicitly to using ultrasound to measure material properties (hardness, grain size, bonding, residual stress) alongside flaws. Brings together key contributors from NBS, Stanford (Kino group), Rockwell, Johns Hopkins, and European institutes.

## Key concepts
- Defect characterisation: near-surface crack detection, flaw orientation and sizing, stress intensity factor, adaptive filtering, scattered-wave sizing
- Materials characterisation via backscattering: grain size quantification in steel
- Ultrasonic prediction of mechanical properties: grain size → strength/toughness correlations
- Fatigue assessment by acoustic methods
- Phase analysis of short pulses for time-of-flight accuracy
- Texture and preferred orientation by velocity measurements
- Metallurgical characterisation; elastic constants of composites
- High-temperature elastic constant measurement (20–1100°C)
- Residual stress measurement by ultrasound (acoustoelastic effect)
- Ultrasonic tomography for residual stress mapping
- Attenuation measurement; phase-insensitive transducer methods

## Methods / results / takeaways
The symposium established that grain size in steel can be inferred from the frequency dependence of ultrasonic backscattering: in the Rayleigh regime (ka ≪ 1), scattering ∝ f⁴·(grain volume); in the stochastic regime (ka ~ 1), scattering ∝ f². Klinman et al. demonstrated predictive correlations between grain-size-derived attenuation and tensile strength/toughness in plain carbon steel, with errors < 15%. The acoustoelastic effect (stress-dependent velocity) underpins residual stress measurements; both first-order (birefringence) and higher-order methods are reviewed. High-temperature elastic constant measurements to 1100°C were demonstrated using buffer rods, enabling characterisation of refractory materials.
