## Overview
A comprehensive review chapter developing and applying resonance scattering theory (RST) to acoustic and elastic waves scattered by objects of various shapes. RST bridges quantum-mechanical resonance theory with classical scattering, linking scattering amplitudes to the eigenvibrations of the target through phase-matched circumferential (surface) waves. Covers solid cylinders, spheres, shells, gas bubbles, elastic plates, fluid cavities, and solid inclusions.

## Unique contribution
First systematic review of RST applied to classical acoustic and elastic scattering. Provides the connection between target resonances and Regge poles (circumferential wave poles), giving physical interpretation to peaks in backscatter spectra as surface waves completing circumnavigations constructively. Also treats inverse scattering (identifying targets from resonance patterns) and extends to electromagnetic (radar) scattering.

## Methods / results / takeaways
The scattered pressure from an elastic cylinder is expanded in cylindrical partial waves with coefficients F_n involving determinants of interface conditions. RST separates F_n into a rigid-body (background) term plus resonance poles; at each eigenfrequency a peak appears. Physically, resonances correspond to Rayleigh or Whispering-Gallery type surface waves that circumnavigate the target at integer multiples of 2π phase. The resonance frequency of a gas bubble of radius a is f₀ = (1/2πa)sqrt(3γp₀/ρ) (Minnaert formula). Thin shells show flexural Lamb-type resonances. These signatures can identify target material properties from backscatter spectra (inverse problem).
