## Overview
The foundational English-language monograph on wave propagation in layered (stratified) media, covering both acoustic and electromagnetic waves under a unified mathematical framework. The book develops spectral methods, plane-wave decompositions, and normal-mode theory to analyze reflection, refraction, and transmission at interfaces and through continuous gradients. It became a widely cited reference in underwater acoustics, seismology, optics, and RF engineering.

## Unique contribution
First comprehensive Western-accessible treatment combining acoustic and electromagnetic layered-media theory under one roof. The parallel development reveals shared mathematical structure (transfer matrices, impedance methods) and cross-fertilises methods across disciplines. Presents Brekhovskikh's own research alongside a thorough bibliography of Soviet work in wave propagation.

## Key concepts
- Plane harmonic waves in homogeneous unbounded media
- Spectral (Fourier) representation of arbitrary wave fields
- Reflection and refraction at flat interfaces (Snell's law, Fresnel coefficients)
- Inhomogeneous (evanescent) plane waves
- Total internal reflection, critical angle
- Leontovich approximate boundary conditions for locally reacting surfaces
- Transfer matrix (impedance method) for multilayer systems
- Normal modes in waveguides
- Ray theory and WKB approximation
- Lateral (head) waves
- Surface waves (Rayleigh, Stoneley)
- Beam displacement upon reflection (Goos-Hänchen effect analog)

## Methods / results / takeaways
The foundation of the theory is a Fourier decomposition of any field into plane waves. For each plane-wave component, reflection and transmission at interfaces are governed by the impedance mismatch; the impedance method allows multilayer systems to be handled by successive matrix products. At a fluid-fluid interface there is a critical angle for total reflection; beyond it the transmitted wave is evanescent. Lateral (head) waves arise from the critical-angle incidence and propagate along the interface, providing information used in seismic refraction surveys. For continuously stratified media the WKB approximation replaces exact solutions; turning points require special treatment (Airy functions). Normal modes in a waveguide arise when constructive interference conditions (phase integral = nπ) are met. The impedance/transfer-matrix approach, originally developed in acoustics and RF engineering, also applies directly to multilayer optical coatings.
