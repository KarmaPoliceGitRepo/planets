## Overview
A foundational textbook establishing the formal analogies between electrical network theory and mechanical/acoustic systems. Mason develops a unified framework for analyzing electromechanical transducers (electromagnetic, electrostatic, piezoelectric, magnetostrictive) and acoustic/mechanical wave filters using the same mathematical tools as electrical circuit theory. Originally based on lectures at Stevens Institute, 1940–41.

## Unique contribution
First systematic textbook to apply electrical network theory (image parameters, filter sections, distributed-constant networks) to lumped mechanical systems, acoustic tubes, and piezoelectric crystal devices. Introduces transmission-line models for acoustic waveguides and provides the first comprehensive equivalent-circuit treatment of piezoelectric crystal filters.

## Key concepts
- Electromechanical analogies (force ↔ voltage, velocity ↔ current)
- Image impedance, image parameters of two-port networks
- Mechanical filters (lumped and distributed)
- Acoustic wave equation in tubes; horn theory
- Acoustic filter theory, side branches
- Radiation impedance, directional arrays
- Vibrating membranes and plates
- Piezoelectric driving systems (longitudinal, bimorph)
- Magnetostrictive driving systems
- Crystal equivalent circuit (Butterworth–Van Dyke model precursor)
- Narrow-band and wide-band crystal filters

## Methods / results / takeaways
The acoustic tube is modelled as a transmission line with series inductance ρdx (mass) and shunt compliance dx/(ρc²) per element; the characteristic impedance is Z₀ = ρc/A. Horns (area tapers) are treated by the Webster horn equation. Radiation from a piston in a baffle is analysed via the radiation resistance and reactance of a circular piston. For piezoelectric crystals, the motional arm of the electrical equivalent circuit has inductance L (effective mass), capacitance C_m (compliance), and resistance R_m (loss), in series with the static capacitance C₀. This framework predicts resonant and anti-resonant frequencies and coupling factor k. Crystal filter design uses lattice networks and the image-parameter method to specify bandwidth and attenuation.
