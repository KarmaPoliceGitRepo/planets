## Overview
The second volume of Rayleigh's foundational two-volume treatise on acoustics, covering aerial vibrations, wave propagation, resonators, diffraction, scattering, and audition. One of the most influential works in classical acoustics, uniting mathematical analysis with physical explanation. Volume II covers Chapters XI–XXIV with additions through the 1896 second edition.

## Unique contribution
Establishes many foundational results still in use: Rayleigh's equation for surface waves, Helmholtz resonator theory, radiation from vibrating spheres and plates, Doppler effect, diffraction from apertures, and the reciprocity theorem. The treatment of nonlinear phenomena (second-order pressure, Kundt's tube striations, Rayleigh streaming) predates modern nonlinear acoustics. The chapter on audition provides the 1896 state of knowledge on psychoacoustics.

## Methods / results / takeaways
The velocity-potential φ satisfies the wave equation ∇²φ = (1/c²)∂²φ/∂t². For a Helmholtz resonator the natural frequency is ω₀² = c²·S/(V·l_eff) where S is neck area, V reservoir volume, l_eff effective neck length (including end corrections). Radiation from a small sphere shows that when ka ≪ 1 (subwavelength), very little energy is radiated; the near field is largely reactive. Scattering cross-section of a small hard sphere ∝ k⁴a⁶ (Rayleigh scattering law). In the diffraction chapter, Huygens-Fresnel zones explain why geometric shadows are not sharp; Fresnel diffraction applies near the aperture. The atmospheric refraction analysis shows that when wind is overhead, downward refraction on the downwind side and upward on the upwind side produce marked asymmetry in audibility. Many results remain textbook standards today.
