## Overview
The first volume in Brekhovskikh's two-volume treatment of wave propagation in stratified media. It systematically develops the theory of plane and quasi-plane acoustic and elastic waves in horizontally layered fluid and solid media, with applications to underwater acoustics and geophysics. The 1989 edition is an earlier print of what appears as a 1990 edition in the same folder.

## Unique contribution
Rigorous mathematical treatment of the reflection and transmission of acoustic waves at arbitrary angles in layered fluid and solid media, including the exact derivation of angle-dependent reflection coefficients, head waves, and leaky waves. Provides a foundation for modelling sonar and seismic propagation in realistic ocean and geological layered structures.

## Key concepts
- Plane wave reflection and transmission coefficients
- Snell's law in layered media
- Critical angle, total internal reflection
- Head waves (refracted / diving waves)
- Quasi-plane waves, beam displacement (Goos–Hänchen shift)
- Leaky waves, interface waves
- Transfer matrix (propagator matrix) method
- WKB approximation for smoothly varying media
- Acoustic impedance mismatch at interfaces
- Anisotropic layered media

## Methods / results / takeaways
The plane-wave reflection coefficient at a fluid–fluid interface is derived as R = (Z₂cosθ₁ − Z₁cosθ₂)/(Z₂cosθ₁ + Z₁cosθ₂) where Z = ρc. Beyond the critical angle, R becomes complex with |R| = 1 and a phase shift arises. The transfer matrix method propagates field quantities through a stack of layers by multiplying 2×2 matrices, yielding the overall reflection/transmission response. For a smoothly varying medium, the WKB approximation gives the ray-path integral for travel time and amplitude decay. Head waves arise at critical incidence and travel along the faster layer, appearing in seismograms as first arrivals.
