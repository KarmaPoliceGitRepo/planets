## Overview
A graduate-level textbook on linear acoustics covering wave propagation, sound fields, source radiation, transducer theory, flow acoustics, and numerical methods. Developed from teaching at the Technical University of Denmark, it serves as a rigorous mathematical reference bridging classical theory and modern computational tools.

## Unique contribution
Strong emphasis on sound intensity (both active and reactive), the Kirchhoff–Helmholtz integral, and beamforming. The book includes a dedicated treatment of microphone arrays and beamforming (the cover visualises circular array output), which is unusual for a general acoustics text. Flow acoustics and aeroacoustics are included.

## Key concepts
- Wave equation derivation from fluid mechanics
- Plane waves, spherical waves, cylindrical waves
- Sound intensity: active and reactive
- Radiation from piston, baffled sources
- Kirchhoff–Helmholtz integral equation
- Free-field and half-space Green's functions
- Sound absorption, impedance boundary conditions
- Transducer reciprocity
- Flow acoustics, Lighthill's analogy
- Microphone arrays, beamforming
- Finite element and boundary element methods

## Methods / results / takeaways
The active sound intensity I = ½ Re(p u*) is the physical power flux; reactive intensity is related to near-field energy oscillation. The Kirchhoff–Helmholtz integral allows boundary-element computation of radiation and scattering from arbitrary surfaces. Reciprocity relationships enable calibration of transducers. Flow-induced noise (jet noise, trailing-edge noise) is estimated via Lighthill's tensor representation. Numerical chapters show how FEM solves room-acoustics and duct-propagation problems by discretising the Helmholtz equation.
