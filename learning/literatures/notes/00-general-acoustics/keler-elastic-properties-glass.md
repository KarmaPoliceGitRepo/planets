# Determination of the Elastic Properties of Glass and Fine Ceramics by the Ultrasonic Impulse Method

- **Source:** Determination of the elastic properties of glass and fine ceramics by the ultrasonic impulse method - (1956) Keler.pdf

- **Author/Year:** E.K. Keler, E.I. Kozlovskaya, O.V. Nosikov, 1956 (Institute of Silicate Chemistry, USSR Academy of Sciences; translated journal paper)
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Experimental paper using the Sokolov UZD-12 ultrasonic defectoscope to measure longitudinal and transverse wave velocities in glass and ceramic specimens, from which Young's modulus E, shear modulus G, and Poisson's ratio ν are derived. Demonstrates that the static and ultrasonic methods give comparable accuracy for elastic constants, and explores temperature effects on the elastic properties up to the softening point.

## Unique contribution
One of the early Soviet experimental demonstrations of the ultrasonic impulse method for ceramics and glass, confirming agreement with static measurements. Practical data on technical porcelain, insulator porcelain, radio ceramics, optical glass (K-8), and window glass, including temperature-dependent behaviour up to 800°C for ceramics and 500°C for glass.

## Key concepts
- Ultrasonic impulse method (pulse-echo on a block specimen)
- Longitudinal wave velocity CL = 2l/TL
- Transverse wave velocity CS from oblique incidence with prismatic probe
- Elastic moduli: Young's E = 2G(1+ν), shear G = CS²·ρ·g, bulk K
- Poisson's ratio ν from the ratio CL/CS
- First and second critical angles at an interface
- Temperature dependence of elastic constants; absorption above Tg (glass transition)
- Sokolov defectoscope UZD-12

## Methods / results / takeaways
Longitudinal velocity is measured by the time-of-flight between initial and reflected pulses in the specimen. Transverse velocity requires an oblique incidence (prismatic) probe, comparing with a steel standard (CS steel = 3.13 × 10⁵ cm/s). Results for technical porcelain: G ≈ 3.8 × 10⁵ kg/cm², E ≈ 9.4 × 10⁵ kg/cm², ν ≈ 0.27–0.29; optical glass K-8: G ≈ 3.3 × 10⁵ kg/cm², E ≈ 8.25 × 10⁵ kg/cm², ν ≈ 0.22. The measured values agree well with static testing to within ~2–4% for a single specimen; specimen-to-specimen variation is larger (up to ~15% for E in some ceramics). Above the softening temperature Tg, the amplitude of the reflected pulse decreases, indicating increasing ultrasonic absorption in the viscous material.
