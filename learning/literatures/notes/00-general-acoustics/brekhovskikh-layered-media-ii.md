## Overview
The second volume of Brekhovskikh and Godin's systematic treatment of acoustic wave propagation in inhomogeneous media. Where Volume I (plane and quasi-plane waves) treats infinite planar wavefronts, Volume II addresses the more physically realistic case of point sources and spatially bounded beams. Topics include spherical-wave reflection, beam displacement effects, lateral waves, moving media, normal modes, and range-dependent waveguides.

## Unique contribution
Extends the plane-wave theory of Vol. I to point sources and bounded beams — the settings encountered in practical sonar, ocean acoustics, and NDE. The 2nd edition adds chapters on arbitrarily inhomogeneous moving media and two appendices on range-dependent waveguides (mode and parabolic-equation methods). Computer freeware links via Ocean Acoustics Library supplement the theory.

## Key concepts
- Integral representation of spherical-wave field (Sommerfeld–Weyl integral)
- Reflected and refracted spherical waves
- Beam displacement (Schoch displacement, Goos-Hänchen analog)
- "Nonspecular" effects: lateral and angular deviations
- Lateral (head) wave from point sources
- Weak boundary, impedance boundary conditions
- Wave equation in moving media; reciprocity and flow-reversal theorem
- Exact solutions for linear and quadratic sound-speed profiles
- Discrete spectrum: normal modes in waveguides
- Range-dependent waveguide; adiabatic approximation; ray invariant
- Parabolic equation (PE) method
- Antiwaveguide propagation

## Methods / results / takeaways
A key result is the Sommerfeld integral representation of the reflected spherical wave; saddle-point evaluation gives the geometrical-optics reflected wave, while branch-cut contributions yield the lateral wave. Beam displacement on reflection can be positive (Schoch) or negative near total internal reflection. The flow-reversal theorem shows that reciprocity holds even in a moving layered medium if source and receiver are exchanged simultaneously with flow reversal. Normal modes for a point source are obtained by Fourier-Bessel transform; in a range-dependent waveguide the adiabatic approximation (slowly varying environment) conserves the mode action integral (ray invariant). The parabolic equation method handles range dependence efficiently and is used in modern ocean-acoustic propagation codes. Antiwaveguides (negative velocity gradient near surface) produce quasi-modes that leak energy, with lateral waves responsible for long-range propagation.
