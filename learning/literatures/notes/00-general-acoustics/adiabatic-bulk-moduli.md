## Overview
Short supplemental notes from MIT's 8.03 physics course explaining why the adiabatic bulk modulus (κ = γp) rather than the isothermal bulk modulus (κ = p) governs the speed of sound in gases. Derives the result using the ideal gas law and the first law of thermodynamics.

## Unique contribution
Provides a compact, pedagogically clear derivation of the adiabatic versus isothermal distinction for sound propagation. Clarifies why γ = C_p/C_v appears in the Newton–Laplace formula for sound speed in gases.

## Key concepts
- Bulk modulus κ = −V(dp/dV)
- Isothermal process: κ = p (slow)
- Adiabatic process: κ = γp (fast, relevant for sound)
- Ideal gas law: pV = nRT
- First law of thermodynamics: dU = dQ − pdV
- Heat capacities C_p and C_v; relation C_p = C_v + R
- Ratio γ = C_p/C_v > 1 for ideal gas
- adiabatic relation: pV^γ = constant

## Methods / results / takeaways
Sound waves are adiabatic because the period is too short for significant heat exchange. For an adiabatic process in an ideal gas: V dp = −γp dV, giving κ_adiabatic = γp. Therefore c = sqrt(γp/ρ) = sqrt(γRT/M) where M is molar mass. For air at 20°C, γ ≈ 1.4, giving c ≈ 343 m/s. The isothermal speed c_iso = sqrt(p/ρ) is about 18% lower, a historically important correction Newton failed to make until Laplace.
