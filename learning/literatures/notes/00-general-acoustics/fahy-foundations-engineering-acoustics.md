## Overview
A broad graduate-level text for engineering practitioners covering the physics of sound in fluids, impedance concepts, energy and intensity, source radiation, sound absorption, ducts, enclosures, and vibro-acoustics. Fahy emphasises physical intuition alongside mathematics and covers measurement as well as theory.

## Unique contribution
Particularly strong treatments of sound intensity measurement (two-microphone technique, source power determination) and porous absorber modelling (Delany–Bazley / Miki models, backed cavity configurations, Helmholtz resonators). The impedance chapter is unusually thorough, covering mechanical, specific acoustic, radiation, and modal radiation impedances in one place.

## Key concepts
- Wave equation in three dimensions and various coordinates
- Acoustic impedance (mechanical, specific, radiation, modal)
- Sound intensity: active and reactive components; measurement
- Source power determination via intensity scanning
- Sources: monopole, dipole, quadrupole; Rayleigh integral
- Porous sound absorbers: flow resistivity, porosity, structure factor
- Helmholtz resonators, panel absorbers
- Sound transmission through partitions
- Duct acoustics, mufflers
- Enclosures: diffuse field, reverberation, transmission loss

## Methods / results / takeaways
The two-microphone intensity probe estimates intensity as I = −(p₁+p₂)/2 × ∂u/∂t·Δd, allowing directional power flow mapping around sources. Porous absorber impedance is modelled via a modified plane-wave equation with complex effective density and bulk modulus. Helmholtz resonator absorption peaks at resonance when radiation resistance matches internal losses. Duct cut-off frequencies are f_mn = c/(2a)·sqrt(m²+(na/b)²) for rectangular ducts; below cut-off, only plane waves propagate. The critical frequency for panel transmission loss is f_c = c²/(2π) · sqrt(m/B) where m is mass/area and B is bending stiffness.
