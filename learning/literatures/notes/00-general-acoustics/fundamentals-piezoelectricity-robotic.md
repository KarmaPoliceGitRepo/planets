## Overview
A self-contained appendix chapter introducing piezoelectricity for robotics and sensor applications. Reviews the historical background (Curie brothers, 1880), explains polarisation mechanisms in dielectric and ferroelectric materials, and provides the mathematical framework for the piezoelectric effect using constitutive equations.

## Unique contribution
Concise overview aimed at engineers rather than physicists; introduces PVDF (polyvinylidene fluoride) and PVDF-TrFE polymer piezoelectrics alongside PZT, reflecting the interest in flexible sensors. Useful reference that cites Mason (1942, 1958) and Cady (1946) as primary theory sources.

## Methods / results / takeaways
The piezoelectric constitutive equations relate stress T, strain S, electric field E, and electric displacement D via coupling matrices d, e, g, h. In sensor mode, D = d·T + ε^T·E; in actuator mode, S = s^E·T + d^t·E. PVDF films have much lower piezoelectric coefficients than PZT (d₃₃ ≈ −33 pC/N vs ~300–600 for PZT) but offer flexibility and large area coverage, making them attractive for tactile skin sensors. The 1920s crystal-resonator frequency-control application launched piezoelectronics commercially.
