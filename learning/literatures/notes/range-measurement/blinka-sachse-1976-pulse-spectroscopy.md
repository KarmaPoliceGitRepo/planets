## Overview
Describes an acoustoelastic stress measurement technique using shear wave birefringence in aluminum. A broadband ultrasonic pulse containing both fast and slow shear wave components is transmitted through a stressed specimen; the power spectrum of the received echo has periodic minima at specific frequencies, from which the difference in arrival times — and hence stress — can be determined without resolving individual waveforms.

## Unique contribution
Uses ultrasonic pulse spectroscopy (frequency-domain analysis of birefringent shear echoes) to measure acoustoelastic stress, avoiding the need to time-resolve closely spaced shear wave arrivals in the time domain. Derives the relationship between spectral minima spacing and stress.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Stressed material has two shear wave polarizations (fast and slow) with slightly different velocities → different arrival times ΔT.
- Echo power spectrum has periodic minima at f_min = (2n−1)/(2ΔT), where n = 1, 2, 3, …
- ΔT extracted from spacing of spectral minima.
- Stress resolved from ΔT via acoustoelastic constants.
- Demonstrated on aluminum under uniaxial load.
- Stress resolution: ~36 psi (0.25 MPa) per inch of path in aluminum if spectral minima are resolvable to 10 kHz.
- Avoids need for broadband time-domain resolution of the two shear arrivals when ΔT is very small.
