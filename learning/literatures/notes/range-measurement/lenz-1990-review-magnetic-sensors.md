## Overview
An invited IEEE review covering eleven magnetic sensor technologies, comparing their sensitivity ranges and suitability for three application categories: measuring fields stronger than Earth's field, perturbations in Earth's field, and gradients in generated/induced fields.

## Unique contribution
Comprehensive side-by-side comparison of eleven magnetic sensing technologies across the full sensitivity spectrum from SQUID (most sensitive, ~10⁻¹⁴ T) to Hall-effect and magnetoresistive devices, with practical guidance on frequency response, size, power, and application domain.

## Key concepts

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Eleven technologies plotted on a common sensitivity range chart (Fig. 1 in paper).
- **Search coil**: Faraday induction; detects ≥10⁻¹⁴ T; AC fields only; 1 Hz–1 MHz bandwidth.
- **Flux-gate**: high DC sensitivity; used in navigation and geophysics.
- **SQUID**: most sensitive (~10⁻¹⁴ T); requires cryogenic cooling.
- **Hall-effect**: 10⁻³–10³ G; DC to 1 MHz; wide temperature range (−273°C to +200°C); low power (0.1–0.2 W).
- **Magnetoresistive (Permalloy)**: 10⁻⁷–50 G open-loop, to 10⁻⁹ G closed-loop; DC to ~1 GHz; small, low power (0.1–0.5 mW); MEMS-compatible.
- Applications categorised by field strength relative to Earth's field (~0.5 G): above → Hall/MR; equal → flux-gate/optically pumped; below → SQUID/nuclear precession.
- Magnetic sensors offer rugged, reliable, contactless operation compared to competing technologies.
