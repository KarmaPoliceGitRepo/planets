## Overview
A comprehensive reference chapter (20 pages) from the ASM Handbook on Machining covering all practical aspects of honing and microhoning. It addresses process fundamentals, machine types (vertical, horizontal, manual vs. power stroking, portable), honing stone selection across dozens of materials, grit sizing, cutting fluids, speed/feed parameters, size control methods (air gaging, plug gages, expanding gages), specialized processes (electrochemical honing, hone forming), and microhoning (superfinishing) — including setup comparisons for bearing races, crankshafts, and other automotive parts.

## Unique contribution
The chapter is the most encyclopedic single reference in this folder: it contains a comprehensive stone-selection table (Table 2) covering ~50 materials with recommended abrasive type (A=alumina, B=cBN, C=SiC, D=diamond), grit size, and bond hardness for burr/rough, stock-removal, and fine-finish stages. It also provides detailed coverage of electrochemical honing and hone forming — niche processes not covered by the other documents. Microhoning production rate data for automotive components (Table 11) quantify achievable finishes and throughput.

## Key concepts
- Honing (bore sizing and surface finishing via bonded abrasives, combined rotation + reciprocation)
- Microhoning / superfinishing (short-stroke, high-frequency; workpiece rotates, not tool)
- Crosshatch lay pattern
- Stock removal rules of thumb
- Honing stone composition: alumina, SiC, cBN, diamond; vitrified, resinoid, metal bonds
- Grit size selection (36–600 for honing; 320–1200 for microhoning)
- Manual vs. power stroking
- Size control: air gaging, plug gages, expanding gages, automatic feedback
- Electrochemical honing (ECH)
- Hone forming (simultaneous honing + electrodeposition)
- Surface finish parameters: Ra, Rz, tp (Rmr)
- Bore shape errors (out-of-round, bellmouth, taper, waviness, barrel, etc.)
- Plateau surface texture (crests → wide plateaus via microhoning)
- Length-to-diameter ratio extremes (up to 307:1 for oil-well bores)

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Honing produces Ra 0.80–0.20 µm (32–8 µin.); microhoning achieves 0.10–0.05 µm (4–2 µin.) routinely, and finer with reduced productivity.
- General stock-removal rule: remove ~2× the existing bore error (e.g., 0.05 mm out-of-round → ~0.10 mm removed).
- Soft steel removal rates can reach 32,000 mm³/min; 60 HRC steel drops to ~16,000 mm³/min.
- Stone hardness convention: grade H ≈ extremely hard, L ≈ medium soft, P ≈ very hard, Q–S ≈ medium; harder bonds used for cast iron and non-ferrous; softer for chromium, ceramics, very hard alloys.
- Alumina preferred for carbon and alloy steels; SiC for very soft/tough steels, cast iron, non-ferrous; diamond for carbide/ceramics/glass; cBN for hardened steels and exotic alloys.
- Air gaging enables in-process size control; post-gaging at three bore depths (X and Y axes) provides 100% inspection of size, roundness, and taper.
- Rotation speed is not standardized but increases with: easy-shearing materials, rougher surfaces, fewer/narrower stones, finer finish requirements.
- Electrochemical honing (ECH): electrochemical dissolution + mechanical abrasion; retains crosshatch; uses less corrosive electrolyte than ECM; all equipment must be corrosion resistant.
- Hone forming (HF): reverse of ECH — hones while electrodepositing metal; deposition rate 0.018–0.05 mm/min; used for salvaging/reconditioning out-of-tolerance parts.
- Microhoning roughness improvement ratio typically 4:1 to 8:1 in efficient operation (e.g., 6.3 µm → 1.5 µm, or 0.40 µm → 0.05 µm Ra).
- Microhoning converts rough machined crests into wide plateaus, improving wear and lubrication conditions; at higher work speeds the crosshatch disappears and a mirror finish results.
- Typical automotive microhoning: tappet body to 0.05–0.10 µm Ra at 800 parts/h (12 spindles); crankshaft to 0.125–0.20 µm Ra at 80 parts/h (10 spindles).
