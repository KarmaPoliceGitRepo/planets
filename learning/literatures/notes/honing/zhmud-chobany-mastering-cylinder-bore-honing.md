## Overview
A 7-page SAE conference paper surveying state-of-the-art cylinder bore honing for internal combustion engines. It covers honing history and fundamentals, modern multistep processes, advanced bore materials (thermal spray coatings, Nikasil, hypereutectic Al), tooling and abrasive selection, honing fluid chemistry, and mechanochemical finishing via the Triboconditioning® process. The paper situates honing within broader engine tribology optimization aimed at reducing friction, ring wear, blowby, and oil consumption.

## Unique contribution
The paper is notable for its advocacy and technical exposition of the Triboconditioning® process — a mechanochemical, in-manufacture running-in technique that reduces friction mean effective pressure by 5–15% on a 1.6 L GDI engine and simultaneously grows a low-friction tribofilm and generates compressive subsurface stresses to ~10 µm depth. It also compiles Sunnen's full honing fluid product line with formulation rationale, and provides a table of typical GD&T tolerances for thermally sprayed bore coatings (e.g., cylindricity 10 µm, Rpk 0.25 µm).

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Honing produces characteristic crosshatch through simultaneous rotation and reciprocation; the crosshatch angle and Rvk (target ≥ 0.6 µm) are critical for oil retention and scuffing resistance.
- Multistep processes: rough hone → semi-finish → finish/plateau hone. Only Rpk and the crosshatch angle are set by the finish honing step.
- For LDS coated bores, coating thickness is typically 0.3–0.6 mm; remaining layer after honing is 0.08–0.15 mm. Rough honing and position honing are both used at the first step depending on whether position correction is needed.
- APS coating layers are ~0.2 mm; fine boring can substitute rough honing for NANOSLIDE® blocks.
- The Triboconditioning® process uses tungsten carbide ledges (not abrasives) with a synthetic process fluid (PF-B05), producing a negatively skewed, plateau-like surface and a low-friction tribofilm — without compromising oil retention.
- Helical slide honing achieves extremely smooth plateaus by a different cutting kinematics, but unlike Triboconditioning® does not prime the surface or induce compressive stresses.
- Diamond abrasives preferred for cast iron, carbide, ceramics; cBN for hardened steels and exotic alloys.
- Mirror-like finishes on LDS/TWAS coatings are viable because oil is retained in coating pores rather than grooves.
- Statistical control requirements have tightened: Cpk targets raised from 1.33 → 1.67 → 2.0+.
- Low honing process forces improve finished-part tribology but increase cycle time.
