# Effects of Piston Design Parameters on Piston Secondary Motion and Skirt-Liner Friction

- **Source:** Mansouri & Wong 2005 (Proc. IMechE Part J: J. Engineering Tribology, Vol. 219, pp. 435–449)
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1bdD0L6xj32FskJ4WASg2FxT-V0jjNnSi/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Mansouri, Wong / 2005 (Proc. IMechE Part J 219, pp. 435–449; MIT)
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Parametric study of how piston design variables (pin offset, skirt profile, clearance, surface waviness, oil film thickness) affect piston secondary motion and skirt-liner friction in a 6-cylinder diesel engine (152 mm bore). Uses a mixed-lubrication piston dynamics model coupling Reynolds equation, elastic skirt deformation (compliance matrix), and boundary contact (asperity) model.

## Unique contribution
Derives non-dimensional scaling relationships showing that the ratio of surface waviness to oil film thickness is the dominant parameter controlling skirt-liner boundary contact friction during the power stroke, where boundary contact contributes ~80% of total skirt friction. Shows that oil film thickness has a larger effect than radial clearance on friction and secondary motion.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Engine: 6-cyl diesel, 152 mm bore; baseline piston with pin offset and skirt barrel profile.
- Model: 2D Reynolds equation for hydrodynamic pressure; compliance matrix for elastic skirt deformation; boundary contact model for asperity loads; full Newton iteration each crank step.
- Pin offset: shifts lateral contact from anti-thrust to thrust side near TDC; reduces impact velocity and slap; moderate reduction in friction.
- Skirt barrel profile: affects contact pressure distribution; optimum barrel reduces peak contact pressure and friction.
- Radial clearance: larger clearance → more lateral displacement → higher impact forces; but oil film thickness has greater effect on friction than clearance alone.
- Oil film thickness: thicker film reduces boundary contact fraction; the ratio (surface waviness / oil film thickness) governs boundary contact and friction; lower ratio = lower boundary friction.
- Surface waviness: higher waviness → increased asperity contact → proportionally higher friction in boundary regime; manufacturing control of waviness critical.
- Power stroke: boundary contact accounts for ~80% of total skirt friction; hydrodynamic component dominates at mid-stroke only.
- Non-dimensional scaling: friction force scales with (waviness/film thickness) ratio; allows comparison across different piston sizes and operating conditions.
- Practical implication: reducing skirt surface waviness and maintaining adequate oil supply are the most effective lever for reducing skirt friction, more so than tightening radial clearance.
