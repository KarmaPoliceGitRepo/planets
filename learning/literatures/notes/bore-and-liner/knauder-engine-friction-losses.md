# Investigations of the Friction Losses of Different Engine Concepts. Part 1

- **Source:** Knauder 2019 friction losses (Lubricants 2019, 7, 39)
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1eOucNa7SI01wpnXY2i0tFOO0jcl11Hgr/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Knauder, Allmaier, Sander, Sams / 2019 (Lubricants 7(5), 39; MDPI)
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Combined experimental and simulation approach to perform subassembly-resolved friction loss analysis on a modern four-cylinder passenger-car diesel engine. Friction is measured using the indication method (motored FMEP from pressure data) combined with strip-test campaign across 120 engine operating points. Strip tests progressively remove subassemblies (pistons, valve train, timing drive, seals, bearings) to isolate friction contributions. Journal bearing losses are verified against hydrodynamic (Reynolds + Greenwood-Tripp) MBS simulations using AVL EXCITE.

## Unique contribution
Applies a combined indication + strip-test methodology that requires only a single standard strip-test stage while additional stages enable independent verification of journal bearing simulation. Quantifies friction reduction potential when increasing engine media temperature from 70 °C to 110 °C across the full engine speed/load map.

## Key concepts
- Friction Mean Effective Pressure (FMEP); indication method
- Strip-test subassembly-resolved friction analysis
- Piston group friction; valve train friction; crankshaft bearing friction
- Engine media supply temperature effect on viscosity and friction
- Reynolds equation; Greenwood-Tripp mixed lubrication model
- Journal bearing simulation (MBS + EHD); AVL EXCITE
- SAE 5W30 lubricant properties; HTHS viscosity
- TEHD (Thermo-Elasto-Hydro-Dynamic) journal bearing model

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Test engine: 4-cylinder passenger-car diesel. 120 operating points at 70 °C, 90 °C, 110 °C supply temperature.
- FMEP range: 0.46–0.96 bar. Peak at 3500 rpm, 120 bar Pcyl, 70 °C.
- Increasing supply temperature 70→110 °C reduces FMEP by up to 21% at n > 1500 rpm part load; only ~8% at low speed high load (onset of mixed lubrication at piston group and valve train).
- Valve train + upper timing drive friction torque: 1.6–1.75 Nm at n > 1500 rpm; slight increase at low speed as temperature rises (mixed lubrication onset).
- Lower timing drive: ~1.1–1.3 Nm, nearly speed-independent.
- Crankshaft main bearings: 0.4 Nm (1000 rpm, 110 °C) to 3.8 Nm (4000 rpm, 70 °C); strong speed and temperature dependence.
- Journal bearing simulation (AVL EXCITE, SAE 5W30, ρ=853 kg/m³, η₄₀°C=59.88 mPas, η₁₀₀°C=9.98 mPas) agrees well with strip-test measurements.
- Indicates lubricant viscosity reduction is beneficial for friction at mid-to-high speeds but risks mixed lubrication at low speed high load.
