# Effects of Lubricant Shear Thinning on Mixed Lubrication of Piston Skirt-Liner System

- **Source:** 2016 - Effects of lubricant shear thinning on mixed lubrication of piston skirt-liner system
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1_2OWNAEeKXeX5JDPTQFvbUoyoKvmP6sT/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Ning, Meng, Xie / 2012 (published Proc IMechE Part C, 227(7))
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Theoretical study of piston skirt–liner mixed lubrication incorporating lubricant shear-thinning for multigrade oils. The model couples piston secondary-motion dynamics (6-DOF → 2-DOF lateral + tilt) with average Reynolds equation and Greenwood–Tripp asperity contact, adding the Cross model for shear-rate-dependent viscosity and Vogel equation for temperature dependence. Liner temperature measured experimentally on a 1.339 L gasoline engine.

## Unique contribution
Explicitly integrates shear-thinning (Cross viscosity model) and measured liner temperature distribution into a skirt-liner mixed lubrication model and quantifies the effect on minimum oil film thickness, eccentricity, contact pressure, and FMEP for mono- and multigrade oils. Shows 25% FMEP reduction for SAE 10W30 vs SAE 30.

## Key concepts
- Piston secondary motion (lateral and tilt)
- Average Reynolds equation (Patir–Cheng flow factors)
- Mixed lubrication (Greenwood–Tripp asperity contact)
- Shear thinning (Cross model)
- Vogel viscosity equation
- Friction mean effective pressure (FMEP)
- Thrust side / anti-thrust side
- Oil film temperature distribution
- Multigrade lubricants (SAE 10W30, 15W40)

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Shear rates at skirt reach 10⁵–10⁷ s⁻¹; multigrade viscosity reduction is significant.
- Shear thinning reduces MOFT compared to temperature-only model; peak oil film pressure changes substantially at power stroke TDC.
- For SAE 10W30 vs SAE 30: lower viscosity reduces viscous friction but increases boundary contact pressure and wear risk at expansion TDC.
- Multigrade 10W30 gives ~30% thinner film than 15W40 but FMEP only marginally lower, while contact pressure increases noticeably.
- Piston secondary motion amplitude is insensitive to multigrade vs monograde differences (the dominant driver is clearance and side force).
- Model validated against motoring FMEP measurements over 1200–6000 rpm with good agreement.
