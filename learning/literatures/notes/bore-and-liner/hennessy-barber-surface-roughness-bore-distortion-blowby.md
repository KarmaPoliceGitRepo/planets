# The Effects of Cylinder Wall Surface Roughness and Bore Distortion on Blow-by in Automotive Engines

- **Source:** Tribology Transactions 38(4), pp. 966–972 (1995), DOI: 10.1080/10402009508983494
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1rlMeH-H3JUk1rC-d5lkcfgvFqERUB2Hj/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Hennessy, M. (General Electric Corporation); Barber, G.C. (Oakland University) / 1995 (Tribol Trans 38(4))
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Experimental motorised test device study investigating the combined effects of cylinder wall surface roughness (Ra 0.22–0.89 µm) and initial bore distortion (1.8–10.7 µm) on blow-by in a 86 mm bore 4-cylinder engine. Removable cast iron liners are honed to different roughness levels; distorted liners created by bolt-pressure distortion during honing. After 12 hours running at 1500 rpm, blow-by and final geometry measured. Distorted bores show ~10–13% higher blow-by than non-distorted; rougher surfaces also show higher blow-by (contradicting some prior work). All liners develop oval shape (major axis along thrust/anti-thrust) after running.

## Unique contribution
Demonstrates experimentally that bore distortion and surface roughness interact and cannot be treated independently for blow-by prediction; contradicts earlier findings that higher roughness promotes ring conformance — instead finds that rougher bore valleys act as micro-passageways that increase blow-by, and that initial distortion produces higher blow-by by preventing ring conformance.

## Key concepts
- Motorised test device: 4-cylinder block, one test cylinder, 1500 rpm, pressurised air supply
- Bore distortion: non-distorted 1.8–2.8 µm vs distorted 9.1–10.7 µm (bolt-pressure method during honing)
- Surface roughness: Ra 0.22–0.89 µm; silicon carbide two-step honing (C100L + C240L)
- Blow-by as conformance metric; peak pressure ~2.1 MPa; 12 h run duration
- Distorted bores: ~10–13% higher blow-by than non-distorted
- Rougher surface → higher blow-by (contrary to Daskavich, Williams/Daniel); valleys = micro-passageways
- All cylinders develop oval shape with major axis along thrust/anti-thrust after running (wristpin 90° to oval axis)
- Scuffing event on one distorted liner (2D) dramatically increases final distortion to 16.5 µm

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Test setup: engine-block derived test device, one cylinder with removable liner; motor-driven at constant 1500 rpm; air pressurised to 2.1 MPa; blow-by measured by flow meter + vacuum pump.
- Distortion creation: jig with circumferential bolts at 5 N-m torque during honing; released after honing → elastic spring-back creates initial multi-lobe distortion; non-distorted = no bolt jig.
- Geometry measured: 11 axial levels × 24 circumferential points; distortion = mean |deviation from best-fit cylinder|.
- Results: non-distorted bores: initial 1.8–2.8 µm → after test 5.1–8.5 µm (worn-in oval); distorted bores: initial 9.1–10.7 µm → final 7.5–12.8 µm (except scuffed 2D: 16.5 µm).
- Blow-by (Fig. 12): non-distorted cylinders show increasing blow-by with initial Ra; distorted cylinders similarly but generally higher absolute values.
- Distorted vs non-distorted: ~10–13% higher blow-by for distorted, attributed to ring non-conformance to initial distortion providing passageways.
- Surface roughness effect: higher Ra → higher blow-by in both groups; larger valleys interpreted as micro-passageways (contradiction with conformance-wear hypothesis of prior work may be due to different operating conditions — fired vs motorised).
- Post-test geometry: all cylinders oval with major axis thrust/anti-thrust (piston wristpin axis perpendicular); consistent with piston side thrust loading.
- Bore-liner relevance: quantifies blow-by penalty of initial bore distortion (~10–13%) and challenges assumption that rougher surfaces always improve ring conformance; highlights need for combined distortion + surface roughness optimisation.
