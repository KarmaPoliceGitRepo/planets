# Analysis of the Piston Assembly, Bore Distortion and Future Developments

- **Source:** Engine Tribology (Elsevier Tribology Series 26), Chapter 4, ed. C.M. Taylor, 1993, pp. 127–176

- **Type:** book-chapter
- **Author/Year:** Chittenden, R.J.; Priest, M. / 1993 (in Engine Tribology, Elsevier Tribology Series 26, ed. C.M. Taylor)
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Comprehensive review chapter covering piston secondary motion, piston skirt lubrication, bore distortion characterisation and ring conformability, and future research directions for piston-assembly tribology. Derives equations of motion for piston lateral translation and rotation with five contact condition cases; presents Reynolds equation solution for piston skirt oil film; gives parametric studies of gudgeon pin offset, clearance, and viscosity on piston slap force and friction. Bore distortion section covers causes (manufacturing, assembly, thermal, pressure), Fourier representation, Incometer measurement, and the Hill & Newman ring conformability criterion applied to individual Fourier orders.

## Unique contribution
Provides the Hill & Newman analytical formula for ring conformability as a function of Fourier order — quantifying the minimum free-shape deviation required per order for a ring to conform to a distorted bore — and applies it to show that 3rd and 4th order distortions of only a few microns cause non-conformity with multi-point gas leakage gaps of 10–20 µm; also one of the earliest treatments integrating piston secondary motion dynamics with bore distortion effects in a single chapter.

## Key concepts
- Piston secondary motion: lateral translation + rotation; 5 contact conditions (piston skirt to liner)
- Reynolds equation for piston skirt; cavitation; piston slap force
- Gudgeon pin offset, radial clearance, lubricant viscosity parametric effects
- Bore distortion causes: manufacturing, assembly (bolt preload), thermal, gas pressure
- Fourier representation of bore distortion; circumferential orders; Incometer measurement
- Ring conformability: Hill & Newman criterion; Ui = 3Fr·d·(d−a)³ / [4Eha³(i²−1)²]
- 2nd order: ring conforms fully (inherently); 3rd order: 3 non-conformity regions ~10 µm; 4th order: 4 regions ~20 µm
- Future developments: EHD, non-Newtonian, boundary films, wear, surface texture, blow-by/oil consumption

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Equations of motion: piston mass and inertia with 5 force/moment balance cases depending on which surface (skirt or crown land) contacts liner and on which side; closed-form Newton-Euler framework.
- Skirt lubrication: Reynolds equation in piston-fixed frame; integration gives load capacity and friction; cavitation treated by requiring non-negative pressure.
- Parametric study: gudgeon pin offset towards thrust side reduces lateral velocity and impact force at TDC slap event; increased clearance raises slap force; lower viscosity reduces film support.
- Bore distortion Fourier decomposition: radial deviation = Σ[Ai·cos(iθ) + Bi·sin(iθ)]; amplitude Ri = √(Ai²+Bi²); typically orders 2–4 carry largest amplitude.
- Ring conformability formula: Ui (ring free-shape amplitude needed to conform to bore distortion amplitude of order i) = 3Fr·d·(d−a)³ / [4Eha³(i²−1)²] where Fr = radial ring force, d = bore diameter, a = ring mean radius, E = Young's modulus, h = ring radial thickness; for same bore distortion amplitude, conformability degrades sharply with order.
- Example: 2nd order 5 µm distortion — ring conforms; 3rd order 5 µm — ring does not conform, leaves 3 gaps ~10 µm; 4th order 5 µm — 4 gaps ~20 µm; higher orders increasingly detrimental for same amplitude.
- Future needs identified: full EHD treatment, non-Newtonian and shear-thinning oils, boundary film modelling, temperature-viscosity coupling, component elastic deformation, surface roughness and texture, wear prediction, transient effects, blow-by and oil consumption linkage.
- Bore-liner relevance: foundational reference for ring conformability assessment against Fourier bore distortion orders; provides analytical framework still cited in modern ring/bore literature; quantifies why higher-order distortions are disproportionately harmful to sealing.
