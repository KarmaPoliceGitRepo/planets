# Global Energy Consumption Due to Friction in Passenger Cars

- **Source:** Holmberg 2012 global friction energy (Tribology International 47, 2012)

- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Global quantitative assessment of fuel energy consumed to overcome friction in passenger cars (engine, transmission, tyres, brakes). Estimates friction coefficients for each tribocontact type (boundary, mixed, HD, EHD, rolling) across four car scenarios: Car 2000 (typical), Car 2010 (best commercial), Lab 2010 (best research), Car 2020 (projected). Calculates global fuel and CO₂ savings potentials.

## Unique contribution
First comprehensive bottom-up quantification of global friction energy in passenger cars with regional breakdown and four technology scenarios. Shows that a 3× improvement in overall fuel economy results from reducing friction because exhaust/cooling losses reduce proportionally.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- One-third of fuel energy consumed to overcome friction; direct frictional losses (excl. braking) = 28% of fuel energy; 21.5% actually moves the car.
- 2009: 208,000 million litres used globally to overcome friction; one average car uses 340 l/year for friction (~13,000 km/a).
- Piston ring contact friction coefficients: boundary 0.14 (Car 2000) → 0.005 (Car 2020); mixed 0.10 → 0.005; EHD sliding 0.08 → 0.005.
- Short term (5–10 years): 18% friction reduction, saving 117,000 million l/a, 290 million tonnes CO₂/a, 174,000 million €/a.
- Long term (15–25 years): 61% reduction, saving 385,000 million l/a, 960 million tonnes CO₂/a, 576,000 million €/a.
- Electric cars: friction-related energy losses ~half those of ICE cars.
- Main friction loss sources: tyre–road 35%, HD lubrication 16.8%, braking 15%, EHD sliding+rolling 8.2%, mixed lubrication 7.6%.
- Key technologies: DLC coatings (>30% friction reduction in lubricated contacts), surface texturing, low-viscosity lubricants, nano-lubricants.
- Note: paper is context/motivation for engine tribology work; not specific to bore-liner but frequently cited for quantifying piston ring friction loss share.
