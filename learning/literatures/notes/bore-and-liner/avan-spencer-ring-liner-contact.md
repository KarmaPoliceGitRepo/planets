# Experimental and Numerical Investigations of a Simulated Piston Ring-Liner Contact

- **Source:** Experimental and Numerical Investigations of a Simulated Piston Ring-Liner Contact

- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Avan, Spencer, Dwyer-Joyce, Almqvist, Larsson / ~2012
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Joint Sheffield–Luleå experimental and numerical study of piston ring–cylinder liner tribology. A Plint TE-77 reciprocating rig was fitted with sections of heavy-duty diesel ring and liner; five 10 MHz piezo-crystal sensors (1.3 × 2.5 mm) were bonded to the back of the liner to measure oil film thickness ultrasonically at five locations along the stroke. An all-regime numerical model (mass-conserving LCP Reynolds + asperity contact mechanics) was run in parallel to predict film thickness and friction.

## Unique contribution
Provides a well-documented bench-scale validation dataset: simultaneous multi-point ultrasonic OFT at five stroke locations combined with friction force measurement, compared against a mass-conserving LCP Reynolds solver with Patir–Cheng flow factors and Boussinesq contact mechanics. The asymmetric barrel-shaped ring profile and cavitation effects on upstroke measurement reliability are explicitly characterised.

## Key concepts
- Ultrasonic film thickness measurement (quasi-static spring model)
- Plint TE-77 reciprocating tribometer
- Reflection coefficient; acoustic impedance
- Piston ring lubrication regimes (boundary / mixed / hydrodynamic)
- Cavitation in ring-liner conjunction
- Mass-conserving LCP cavitation algorithm (Giacopini et al.)
- Patir–Cheng flow factors
- Minimum oil film thickness (MOFT)
- Stick-slip at TDC
- Barrel-shaped ring profile

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Sensor placement: five sensors 1.42–13.28 mm from TDC; cast-iron specimens from 130 mm bore heavy-duty diesel; pure base oil at 22 °C (viscosity ~85.7 cSt).
- Cavitation on the upstroke corrupts ultrasonic data (air-oil mixture gives anomalously high reflected coefficient); downstroke data are reliable.
- MOFT increases towards mid-stroke as hydrodynamic lift grows with piston speed; sensor 3 (highest speed) shows largest film.
- Friction comparison: simulation overestimates MOFT slightly—attributed to uncertain effective viscosity (shear heating); stick-slip at TDC causes experiment to show earlier friction drop (~15° CA) vs simulation (~35° CA).
- Numerical model matches downstroke film and mid-stroke friction well; BDC agreement better than TDC because the asymmetric ring retains a larger squeeze film on the downstroke.
