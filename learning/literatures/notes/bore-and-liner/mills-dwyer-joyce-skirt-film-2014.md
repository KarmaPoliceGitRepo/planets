# Ultrasound for the Non-Invasive Measurement of IC Engine Piston Skirt Lubricant Films

## Overview
Experimental measurement of piston skirt oil film thickness on both thrust and anti-thrust surfaces of a Honda CRF450R single-cylinder engine (449 cc, 96 mm bore) under motored and fired conditions at 4000 rpm. PZT strip sensors externally bonded to the cylinder below the water jacket; films measured without cylinder penetration. Results show piston clearance under motoring and interference under fired conditions, with piston secondary motion inferred from multi-sensor film profiles.

## Unique contribution
First paper to map skirt film simultaneously on both thrust and anti-thrust surfaces in a fired engine using ultrasound. Demonstrates that motored and fired conditions produce fundamentally different piston dynamic regimes (clearance vs. interference), and explicitly links the axial film profile to piston rotation and 'piston slap' during the expansion stroke.

- Trapped oil in roughness peaks
- Piston interference / clearance

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Second reflection (R²) used due to acquisition system timing; error ~4% from pressure effect on bulk modulus; temperature compensated via oil-exit thermocouple.
- Motored (4000 rpm): anti-thrust film ~6 µm; clearance on thrust side (piston rattles in bore); larger cycle-to-cycle variation.
- Fired (4000 rpm, 90% load): simultaneous thin films on both surfaces; minimum ~2.5 µm at upper skirt (~1/3 from top) during expansion; load-dependent reduction.
- 'Double minimum' on anti-thrust during compression/exhaust attributed to piston rotation as it translates.
- Piston roughness (~10 µm peak-to-peak) contributes an equivalent ~3.2 µm 'trapped oil' film layer; correcting for this, true minimum approaches zero—consistent with observed polishing patterns.
- During motored, piston runs in clearance; during fired, thermal expansion causes interference with simultaneous bilateral contact.
