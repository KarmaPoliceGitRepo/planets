# SAE J2904: Power Cylinder Friction: Mechanisms

- **Source:** SAE Surface Vehicle Information Report J2904™ MAY2018 (Issued 2010-01, Reaffirmed 2018-05), 48 pp.
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1NseTwbtONCMbTVbSqDuly5Bi4jvVkO4I/view
- **Type:** standard
- **Author/Year:** SAE Piston and Rings Standards Committee / 2018 (SAE J2904; originally issued 2010)
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
SAE information report cataloguing all mechanisms by which power cylinder components affect engine friction, rating each factor as Minor / Medium / Major Effect. Covers piston (mass, cooling, ring grooves, ring lands, skirt, pin bore), ring pack (top, second, oil rings), cylinder bore (surface finish, honing angle, bore distortion, material, coating), connecting rod, oil properties, oil jets, and piston pin. Includes a friction contribution hierarchy: ring pack (mainly oil ring 50–75%, top ring 13–40%) and piston each contribute roughly 25–50% of power-cylinder friction. Bore distortion is rated a Medium Effect.

## Unique contribution
Authoritative industry consensus document providing a structured, practitioner-oriented taxonomy of all power cylinder friction mechanisms with effect ratings; the bore distortion section explicitly states that higher-order circumferential distortions (4th order and above) are harder for rings to conform to, that Fourier analysis is the correct tool, and that bore distortion magnitude requirements tighten with increasing harmonic order — codifying industry design guidance in a normative reference.

## Key concepts
- Power cylinder friction split: rings 28–45%, piston 25–47%, rods 18–33%
- Top ring friction mainly from gas pressure (expansion stroke); oil ring from high tangential load
- Bore distortion (Medium Effect): circumferential + axial; Fourier harmonic decomposition
- Higher-order distortion (4th+) harder to seal; acceptable distortion amplitude decreases with order
- Axial distortion: rings may not expand/contract fast enough to follow contour → excessive force
- Bore dynamic distortion: changes during operation due to thermal and combustion loading
- Bore surface finish: peak honed (automotive, smooth) vs plateau honed (diesel, Ra + valleys); laser texturing
- Piston skirt guidance (Major Effect): secondary motion, skirt size, flexibility, coating
- Ring face profile (barrel, taper), tension, axial width, material/coating — Medium Effects
- Oil viscosity (Major): dominant friction lever; oil additives, temperature
- Assembly quality: O-ring distortion, head bolt torque procedure → liner distortion

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Friction contribution figure: oil ring 50–75% of ring-pack friction; top ring 13–40%; second ring 10–22%. Rod bushing friction major contributor within connecting rod.
- Bore distortion mechanism: if ring cannot conform to circumferential bore distortion → regions of high contact pressure → higher friction. Rings less able to conform to higher-order than oval (2nd order).
- Axial bore distortion: rings must expand/contract during stroke; too fast variation → force concentrations.
- Dynamic bore distortion: noted that distortion changes with combustion/thermal cycle; continuous permanent deformation possible.
- Bore surface: plateau hone valleys provide oil reservoir; plateau roughness key for friction; texture (laser dimpling) can reduce friction at top-ring reversal.
- Skirt: secondary motion (piston slap) dominant friction contributor from piston; coatings major effect.
- Oil ring tension: major friction driver; bore distortion forces higher tension requirement → indirect major contribution from distortion.
- Quality issues section: head bolt torque errors → excessive cylinder distortion → ring non-conformity; O-ring swelling → liner distortion.
- Bore-liner relevance: normative industry reference confirming Fourier analysis as standard bore distortion assessment tool; codifies bore distortion as Medium Effect on friction with explicit guidance on order-dependent conformability limits.
