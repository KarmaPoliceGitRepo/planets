# The Determination of Transducer Coupling Coefficients by Time Domain Analyses

## Overview
Introduces a time-domain method for characterising thin piezoelectric transducers (CdS thin films) bonded to a delay line, using voltage step response instead of frequency-domain insertion loss. FFT techniques are used to transform between domains. Compares the coupling coefficient k determined by the two methods; they agree to within 9%.

## Unique contribution
First demonstration that the time-domain voltage step response of a transducer provides both amplitude and phase information of the transfer function, unlike insertion loss which gives only amplitude. Shows that amplitude and phase data are complementary rather than redundant, and that time-domain data permit a more complete and reliable transducer characterisation than insertion loss alone.

## Key concepts
- Time-domain transducer characterisation
- Voltage step response
- Fast Fourier transform (FFT) for transducer analysis
- Insertion loss method vs time-domain method
- Electromechanical coupling coefficient k
- CdS thin film transducer
- Diffraction and absorption corrections
- Equivalent circuit of ultrasonic delay line

## Methods / results / takeaways
- CdS transducer on 2.54 cm x-cut quartz delay line; impedance ratio z_d/z_0 = 0.71
- Insertion loss analysis: k = 0.106, f0 = 264 MHz (reduced from 0.155 single-crystal due to c-axis misalignment in polycrystalline film)
- Time-domain method: k = 0.116, f0 = 235 MHz — 9% agreement in k; difference in f0 attributed to inadequacy of isotropic diffraction model for anisotropic x-cut quartz
- Key insight: minimum-phase functions relate amplitude and phase uniquely, but in general the two provide supplementary information; a good amplitude fit does not guarantee a good phase fit or time-domain waveform
- Conclusion: time-domain method is faster and provides richer information, but requires known phase corrections for diffraction and dispersion in the delay medium
