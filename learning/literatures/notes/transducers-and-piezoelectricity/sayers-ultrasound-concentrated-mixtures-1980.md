# On the Propagation of Ultrasound in Highly Concentrated Mixtures and Suspensions

- **Source:** Sayers 1980 ultrasound concentrated mixtures
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/15TZVlfsJsM6sHdLvUQ-HYL3DWeTFhknI/view
- **Type:** paper (Journal of Physics D: Applied Physics 13, 1980)
- **Author/Year:** C. M. Sayers / 1980
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Applies multiple scattering theory (Lloyd-Berry method, derived from electronic structure of liquids/disordered alloys) to ultrasound propagation in highly concentrated suspensions and mixtures. Extends the Waterman-Truell (1961) dilute-suspension result by including self-consistency of the effective medium at high volume fractions. Derives that frequency-dependent velocity dispersion in a binary isotropic mixture depends sensitively on particle radius — enabling particle sizing from velocity measurements.

## Unique contribution
Shows that the Waterman-Truell approach (standard multiple scattering, non-self-consistent) gives incorrect sign of the dispersion term at high concentrations. The self-consistent Lloyd-Berry approach gives opposite sign: longitudinal wave velocity decreases with increasing frequency in a symmetric binary mixture. Demonstrates that multiple scattering corrections are more important than previously assumed at high particle concentrations. Provides explicit formula for velocity dispersion as function of kₗa showing particle-radius sensitivity.

## Key concepts
- Multiple scattering theory: Waterman-Truell vs Lloyd-Berry (self-consistent) approaches
- Self-consistent effective medium: required when volume fraction is not small; surrounding medium ≠ pure matrix
- Scattering cross section: Qs ∝ k₁⁴a⁶ in Rayleigh limit; criterion for validity of Waterman-Truell: n₀Qs/k₁ < 1
- Velocity dispersion: v(ω)/v(0) − 1 depends on kₗa = ωa/v₁; sensitive to particle radius a
- Binary mixture: two components same density/shear modulus, different longitudinal velocities
- Kramers-Kronig: velocity dispersion and absorption absorption are related via dispersion relations

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Single scatterer T-matrix from Ying-Truell (1956) boundary conditions
- At low concentration: Waterman-Truell and Lloyd-Berry agree
- At high concentration: Lloyd-Berry gives additional terms (same order n₀²) changing sign of multiple scattering correction
- Self-consistent solution for symmetric two-component mixture: M = (1/2)M₁ + (1/2)M₂ + frequency-dependent term
- Velocity decreases with increasing frequency in binary mixture (negative dispersion)
- Particle sizing: measurement of velocity dispersion vs frequency gives particle radius sensitivity
- Application context: flow measurement in concentrated coal slurries, process monitoring
