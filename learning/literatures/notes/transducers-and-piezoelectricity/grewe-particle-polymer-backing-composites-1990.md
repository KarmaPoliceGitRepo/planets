# Acoustic Properties of Particle/Polymer Composites for Ultrasonic Transducer Backing Applications

- **Source:** Grewe 1990 particle polymer backing composites
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1af0P5HsFh7E2weiAbjCv4Uek8n0dcKls/view
- **Type:** paper (IEEE Transactions on Ultrasonics, Ferroelectrics, and Frequency Control, 37(6), 1990)
- **Author/Year:** M. G. Grewe, T. R. Gururaja, T. R. Shrout, R. E. Newnham / 1990
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Systematic study of acoustic impedance and attenuation in particle/polymer composites for transducer backing applications. Investigates tungsten, PZT, and alumina particles in various polymer matrices (epoxy, polyurethane, rubber, thermoplastics) and four fabrication routes (hand-mixed 0-3, hot uniaxial press HUP, BURPS burnout process, sintered). Establishes quantitative trends relating particle type, size, volume fraction, polymer crosslinkage, and connectivity to impedance and attenuation. Recommends optimal compositions.

## Unique contribution
First systematic experimental matrix isolating individual contributions of polymer matrix, filler type, particle size, volume fraction, and polymer-filler adhesion to composite backing attenuation. Identifies that weak particle-matrix adhesion (poor wettability) causes higher attenuation via interfacial friction. Shows that processing route controls connectivity (0-3 vs 3-3) and dramatically changes attenuation: BURPS tungsten composites achieve >217 dB/cm but only 8.3 MRayl. Validates Reuss bulk modulus model for impedance prediction below 40 vol% filler.

## Key concepts
- Transducer backing function: absorb energy from rear face → shorter pulse, wider bandwidth; impedance-sensitivity trade-off
- Backing impedance range: 2–30+ MRayl depending on design
- 0-3 connectivity: isolated filler particles in continuous polymer matrix
- Impedance model: Z = √(ρ·K_Reuss); Reuss bulk modulus model accurate up to ~40 vol% filler
- Attenuation mechanisms: (1) scattering (Rayleigh/stochastic), (2) absorption (thermoelastic, inelastic, relaxation), (3) interfacial friction
- Crystallinity/crosslinkage: higher crystallinity or crosslinkage → lower attenuation
- Particle size effect: larger particles → higher attenuation (for W, PZT); opposite for alumina (impedance mismatch dominant)

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Pure polymer impedance: 1.1–2.5 MRayl; attenuation: 7–139 dB/cm (Eccogel 1365-80 highest: 139 dB/cm)
- Tungsten/Spurr epoxy (20 vol%, 5 μm): Z = 19 MRayl, α = 69 dB/cm @ 5 MHz
- HUP tungsten (75 vol%): Z > 50 MRayl, α < 24 dB/cm — lower attenuation due to connectivity change (0-3 → 1-3/3-3)
- BURPS tungsten: Z = 8.3 MRayl, α > 217 dB/cm — highest attenuation but low impedance (microcracks from burnout)
- Recommendation for high-attenuation backing: large (50 μm) W particles at low volume % in soft, lightly crosslinked polymer (e.g., Eccogel 1365-80)
- Reuss model breaks down for >40 vol% filler (underestimates impedance)
- Impedance frequency-independent; attenuation increases with frequency (slope greater for filled composites)
