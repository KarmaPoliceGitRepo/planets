## Overview
Characterises ultra-low acoustic impedance materials — silica aerogel and microporous PMMA (porous acrylic) — as candidates for quarter-wavelength matching layers in air-coupled ultrasonic transducers. Measures acoustic velocity, impedance, and attenuation for a range of porosity values.

## Unique contribution
Provides quantitative acoustic characterisation of silica aerogel (ZA ≈ 0.508–0.512 MRayl) and porous PMMA (ZA = 0.28–2.02 MRayl) as matching layer materials. Silica aerogel has 5–15× lower attenuation than porous PMMA alternatives at equivalent impedance, making it the preferred material for low-loss air-coupled matching.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Silica aerogel: ZA = 0.508–0.512 MRayl; acoustic velocity = 318–320 m/s; density ~100–150 kg/m³
- Porous PMMA (various porosities): ZA = 0.28–2.02 MRayl depending on pore fraction; velocity 180–900 m/s
- Silica aerogel attenuation: very low (5–15× lower than porous PMMA at equivalent ZA)
- For quarter-wavelength matching from PZT (30 MRayl) to air (0.00043 MRayl): ideal ZA = √(30 × 0.00043) ≈ 0.11 MRayl — aerogel is closest available material
- PVDF (ZA ≈ 4 MRayl) to air matching: ideal matching layer ZA = √(4 × 0.00043) ≈ 0.04 MRayl — even aerogel is ~5× too high; multi-layer approaches required
- Both materials fragile and humidity-sensitive; aerogel especially so (hygroscopic, structurally brittle)
- Practical use: aerogel discs bonded to transducer face; PMMA grades offer some mechanical robustness advantage
- Results establish aerogel as best single-layer matching material for air-coupled applications at frequencies below ~1 MHz
