## Overview
Comprehensive review of broadband ultrasonic absorption and velocity measurement techniques in liquids, covering CW and pulsed methods from kilohertz to gigahertz. Reviews resonator and travelling-wave methods, discusses sonic cell designs and electronic instrumentation, and presents examples of broad-band absorption spectra in relaxation spectrometry applications.

## Unique contribution
Provides a unified treatment of multiple complementary ultrasonic spectrometry methods needed to span the full frequency range of liquid characterisation. Discusses advantages and limitations of CW resonator vs pulse-modulated techniques, and relates measurements to thermodynamic parameters (reaction volumes ΔV, reaction enthalpies ΔH, shear viscosity, thermal conductivity) accessible via acoustic probes.

## Key concepts
- CW resonator techniques vs pulse-modulated travelling wave methods for liquids
- Frequency range: kHz to GHz; different methods required at different ranges
- Ultrasonic absorption and velocity both measurable; access to relaxation spectra
- Thermodynamic quantities accessible: ΔV, ΔH, shear viscosity (via acoustic waves without invasive labelling)
- Applications: physics, physical chemistry, material science, biology, medicine
- Sonic cells, high-frequency transducers, electronic signal processing for liquid spectrometry

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Review covers: variable-path cells, resonator cells, pulse-echo, sing-around, and laser methods
- Relaxation spectrometry: identifies molecular processes with time constants <1 μs
- Broadband requirement arises from diversity of liquid wavelength and attenuation values
- Each technique covers a limited sub-range; multiple overlapping methods needed for full spectrum
- Examples: absorption spectra of solutions using different CW/pulse methods showing consistency
