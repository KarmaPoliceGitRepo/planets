## Overview
PhD thesis on the design of sonar transducers for 100 kHz – 1 MHz. Uses one-dimensional transmission line analysis to predict frequency responses and identifies intermodal coupling between planar and thickness modes as the primary source of discrepancy between prediction and measurement. Investigates the piezoelectric-tunable transducer (two ceramics — one driven, one with passive electrical load) as an alternative to quarter-wave matching for achieving wide/variable bandwidth. Also covers material property measurement techniques using solid buffer rods.

## Unique contribution
Studies the piezoelectric-tunable transducer in which the resonant frequency can be swept over more than one octave by varying the passive electrical load, though instantaneous bandwidth remains ≈10% of centre frequency. Extends this to active control using two voltage sources with variable relative amplitude and phase. Provides practical buffer-rod measurement techniques for acoustic velocity and attenuation that avoid water-tank limitations.

## Key concepts
- 1D transmission line analysis for sonar transducer design (100 kHz – 1 MHz)
- Quarter-wave matching layers for wideband response
- Piezoelectric-tunable transducer (dual-ceramic: driven + passive load)
- Resonant frequency control over >1 octave by passive electrical load
- Active transducer control (two voltage sources, variable phase/amplitude)
- Intermodal coupling between planar and thickness vibration modes
- Buffer rod measurement techniques for velocity and attenuation
- Sonar transducer design for underwater acoustics

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Discrepancy between 1D model predictions and measurements attributed to intermodal planar-thickness coupling (not captured in 1D model)
- Piezoelectric-tunable design: ≈10% instantaneous fractional bandwidth at any setting; centre frequency tunable over >1 octave by passive load variation
- Active control: same results achievable with two driven ceramics at specific amplitude/phase ratios
- Buffer rod measurements: practical alternative to water-tank testing for material characterisation
