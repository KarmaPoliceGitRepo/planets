## Overview
Provides a comprehensive phenomenological treatment of three independent loss mechanisms in piezoelectrics: dielectric (tan δ'), elastic (tan φ'), and piezoelectric (tan θ'). Shows how "intensive" and "extensive" losses are related through the electromechanical coupling factor k. Demonstrates experimentally that heat generation at off-resonance is dominated by dielectric loss, while resonance heat is dominated by mechanical loss (intensive).

## Unique contribution
Establishes the complete relationship between "intensive" (externally controllable) and "extensive" (material-intrinsic) loss tangents through the coupling factor k. Shows that the Q_m at antiresonance is larger than at resonance, and proposes that antiresonance mode is superior for high-power applications (ultrasonic motors). First rigorous measurement scheme for separating all three loss components from different hysteresis curves.

## Key concepts
- Dielectric loss tangent tan δ' (P-E hysteresis)
- Elastic loss tangent tan φ' (z-X hysteresis)
- Piezoelectric loss tangent tan θ' (D-X or z-E coupling)
- Intensive vs extensive losses
- Mechanical quality factor Qm
- Resonance vs antiresonance mode
- Electromechanical coupling factor k
- Heat generation in piezoelectrics

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Measurement scheme: tan δ' from D-E hysteresis; tan φ' from z-X hysteresis; tan θ' from z-E or D-X measurement (subtracting other contributions numerically)
- Loss interrelations: tan δ (extensive) = (1/(1-k²)) × [tan δ' + k² (tan φ' - 2tan θ')]
- Off-resonance heat: dominated by intensive dielectric loss tan δ' (P-E hysteresis) — increases strongly with applied field
- Resonance heat: dominated by intensive mechanical loss tan φ' — activates at high vibration velocity
- Qm (antiresonance) > Qm (resonance) in first-order approximation — antiresonance is better for high-power devices
- Significant decrease in Qm above critical vibration velocity (~0.1 m/s) due to increase in extensive dielectric loss Rd, not Rm
- Piezoelectric losses tan θ' are NOT negligible — comparable to dielectric and elastic losses in soft PZTs
