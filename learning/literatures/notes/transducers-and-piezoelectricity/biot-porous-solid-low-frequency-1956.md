# Theory of Propagation of Elastic Waves in a Fluid-Saturated Porous Solid. I. Low-Frequency Range

- **Source:** Biot porous solid low frequency 1956

- **Author/Year:** M. A. Biot / 1956
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Foundational theory (Part I) for elastic wave propagation in a fluid-saturated porous solid in the low-frequency range (Poiseuille flow assumption valid). Derives the stress-strain relations for a porous aggregate with four elastic constants (A, N, Q, R corresponding to Lamé moduli plus fluid-solid coupling), and dynamic equations with three mass density parameters (p₁₁, p₂₂, p₁₂). Identifies three body waves: one rotational and two dilatational (first and second kind). Plots phase velocity and attenuation vs frequency ratio f/fc for six representative cases.

## Unique contribution
First complete theoretical framework for acoustic wave propagation in fluid-saturated porous materials, derived from variational principles and Lagrangian dynamics. Establishes that there must be two dilatational waves (first kind: fast, fluid-solid nearly in phase; second kind: slow, high attenuation, diffusion-like). Introduces characteristic frequency fc = b/(2πp₂) and nondimensional parameter f/fc. Provides explicit formulas for phase velocity and attenuation of all three wave types. Defines "dynamic compatibility" condition under which the slow wave nearly vanishes.

## Key concepts
- Four elastic constants (isotropic porous solid): N (shear), A (dilation coupling), Q (fluid-solid coupling), R (fluid bulk modulus contribution)
- Three mass density parameters: p₁₁ (solid effective mass), p₂₂ (fluid effective mass), p₁₂ (coupling, negative)
- Characteristic frequency fc = b/(2πp₂): transition from viscosity-dominated to inertia-dominated regime
- Two dilatational waves: first kind (fast, in-phase, low attenuation ∝ f²); second kind (slow, anti-phase, diffusion-like)
- One rotational wave: phase velocity increases slightly with frequency; attenuation ∝ f²
- Poiseuille flow assumption: valid below fc (pore-size dependent); Part II extends to higher frequencies
- Dynamic compatibility condition: specific ratio of elastic/dynamic constants → slow wave attenuation → ∞

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Lagrangian formulation: kinetic and potential energy quadratic forms → stress-strain and dynamic equations
- Seven independent elastic constants in fully anisotropic case; reduces to 4 for isotropic material
- Nondimensional parameters σ (elastic) and γ (dynamic) characterise material; Table I: 6 representative cases
- Phase velocity of rotational wave: increases from V₀ = (N/p)^½ toward higher value at high f/fc
- Fast dilatational wave (first kind): small dispersion, attenuation ∝ (f/fc)²
- Slow wave (second kind): attenuation ∝ √(f/fc) near f=0; behaves like heat conduction/diffusion
- Application: water-saturated rock, soil mechanics, porous ceramics NDE
