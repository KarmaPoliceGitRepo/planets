## Overview
Presents macroscopic mechanical models for acoustic attenuation in solids, focusing on explaining the experimentally observed frequency-independent Q (quality factor) in rock-forming materials. Considers Coulomb friction, fluid viscosity, and solid friction models, ultimately deriving a model with Q independent of frequency using a gradient solid-friction mechanism.

## Unique contribution
Provides a physically motivated solid-friction model that reproduces frequency-independent Q observed in geophysical materials, as opposed to viscous friction (which gives Q ∝ 1/f) or Coulomb friction (Q ∝ f²). Relevant to transducer backing materials and composite acoustic modelling where frequency-independent loss is required.

## Key concepts
- Acoustic quality factor Q
- Solid friction vs Coulomb friction vs viscous damping
- Frequency-independent attenuation in solids
- Rayleigh dissipation function
- Thermoelastic damping (negligible below 10^8 Hz)
- Grain boundary scattering
- Anelasticity model

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Q independent of frequency observed in non-ferromagnetic inorganic solids over 10^-2 to 10^6 Hz
- Thermoelastic damping: negligible for f < 10^8 Hz in silicates
- Scattering: 1/Q ∝ L³f⁴/V⁴ (Rayleigh scattering); important when wavelength ~ grain size
- Fluid viscosity: 1/Q ∝ f — inconsistent with observed frequency independence
- Coulomb friction: 1/Q ∝ f² — also inconsistent; also predicts cessation of oscillation
- Solid friction with distance-dependent roughness: gives Q independent of frequency — consistent with observations
- The solid-friction model corresponds physically to grain-boundary sliding with increasing resistance at larger displacements
- Model is phenomenological; not extrapolable into new frequency or temperature regimes without additional data
