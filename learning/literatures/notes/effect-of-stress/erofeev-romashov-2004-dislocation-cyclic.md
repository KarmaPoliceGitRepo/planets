# Dislocation Model for Acoustic Wave Attenuation Under Cyclic Loading

- **Source:** Erofeev 2004 cyclic loading dislocations
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1aKO54TeQsqEc-EBKm-9ugualhqE-OyTl/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** V. I. Erofeev & A. V. Romashov, 2004
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Proposes an extended dislocation model that accounts for dislocation-dislocation interactions (beyond the classical Granato-Lucke string model) to predict the evolution of ultrasonic attenuation and velocity during cyclic fatigue loading. The model predicts an attenuation maximum at approximately 20% of the total fatigue lifetime, which is consistent with experimental observations on aluminium and copper.

## Unique contribution
Extends the Granato-Lucke dislocation string model by adding dislocation-dislocation interaction terms, which creates a non-monotonic attenuation-vs-cycle behaviour (peak then decrease). Shows that both attenuation peak position (~20% lifetime) and velocity behaviour (initial increase 0–10–15%, then decrease) are captured by the model, unlike the original Granato-Lucke which predicts monotonic increase.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Classical Granato-Lucke model: dislocation loops behave as strings pinned at ends; attenuation ∝ loop length squared; predicts attenuation monotonically increases with damage → contradicts experiments
- New model: adds interaction energy between neighboring dislocation loops; this introduces a restoring mechanism that reverses attenuation growth after a peak
- Predicted attenuation maximum: occurs at ~20% of total fatigue lifetime (N/N_f ≈ 0.2); this matches experimental data for Al and Cu
- Velocity behaviour:
  - Elastic region (early cycles, 0–10–15%): velocity increases due to work hardening and dislocation multiplication
  - Plastic/accumulation region: velocity decreases as defect density increases and loops grow
- Both trends match experimental measurements on Al and Cu polycrystals
- Model parameters: average loop length L₀, dislocation density ρ_d, interaction parameter α; extracted from fit to velocity-cycle data
- Practical implication: ultrasonic velocity can be used to estimate remaining fatigue life; the velocity peak corresponds to the transition point and the attenuation peak serves as an early warning (~20% life consumed)
