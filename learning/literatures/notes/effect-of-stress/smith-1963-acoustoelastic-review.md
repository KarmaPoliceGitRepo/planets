# Stress-Induced Anisotropy in Solids — The Acousto-Elastic Effect

- **Source:** RT Smith 2013 stress-induced anisotropy
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1Jn14fZPux3fqSsCCnR1YnE0fqbXwHUoo/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** R. T. Smith, 1963
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
A comprehensive review article from 1963 covering the theory, experimental methods, and applications of the acoustoelastic effect in stressed solids. Smith reviews the Hughes-Kelly and Toupin-Bernstein theories, reconciles different notations for TOE constants (Murnaghan, Toupin-Bernstein, Gol'dberg), describes all major measurement techniques for shear wave birefringence, and critically discusses the complications from preferred grain orientation in polycrystals.

## Unique contribution
Unifies the Murnaghan (l,m,n), Toupin-Bernstein (ν₁,ν₂,ν₃), and Gol'dberg (A,B,C) third-order constant systems into a single conversion table. Provides detailed descriptions of the 45° single-transducer technique, the two-transducer parallel connection technique, and a novel two-transducer common-path method (Smith's own modification). Introduces the concept of "sing-around" technique for high-precision TOF measurements. Gives the most complete discussion of the practical problem of separating stress-induced birefringence from texture-induced birefringence available at that time.

## Key concepts
- Acoustoelastic effect / acousto-elastic birefringence
- Shear wave birefringence
- Third-order elastic constants: Murnaghan (l,m,n), Toupin-Bernstein (ν₁,ν₂,ν₃), Gol'dberg (A,B,C)
- Hyperelasticity / strain-energy function
- Sing-around technique
- 45° transducer technique
- Preferred grain orientation / crystallographic texture
- Truesdell identity (validity of second-order theory)
- Principal waves (nine types)

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Relationship between notation systems: l = ½ν₁+ν₂, m = ν₂+2ν₃, n = 4ν₃ (Murnaghan ↔ Toupin-Bernstein); n = A, m−½n = B, l−m+½n = C (Murnaghan ↔ Gol'dberg)
- Fractional shear wave velocity difference: ΔV/V₀ = T/(2ρ₀V₀²) · (4μ+n) where T = uniaxial tension
- Sing-around: pulse re-triggers transmitter; frequency directly tracks transit time; accurate to ~1 in 10⁶
- 45° transducer method (Rollins): single transducer at 45° to stress axis, birefringence creates beat modulation in echo envelope, Δν/ν₀ = c/(2fx) where x is spacing of minima
- Two-transducer common-path method (Smith): both transducers on same face, polarized ⊥ and ∥ to stress, electrical delay varied to null successive echoes; plot of electrical phase vs. echo number gives ΔV/V₀ from slope
- Summary table of TOE constants (1963): polystyrene, Pyrex, Armco iron, REX 535 nickel-steel, C1018 steel, 6061-T6 Al, 1100-F Al, fused silica
- Critical warning: "simple determination of ΔV/V₀ will not give unambiguous estimates of stress if nothing further is known about the structure of the material" — preferred grain orientation can swamp stress birefringence
- Preferred orientation can persist after stress relief; residual birefringence ≠ residual stress
- Applied loads of >20 tons needed to match zero-load birefringence in nickel-steel specimen (due to texture)
- Truesdell identity: (ε₁−ε₂)(U²₁₁−4U₁₂+6U₁₀) = 0 — provides test for existence of strain-energy function in second-order theory
