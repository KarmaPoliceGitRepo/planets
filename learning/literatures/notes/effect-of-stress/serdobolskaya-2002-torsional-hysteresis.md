# Acoustic Velocity Hysteresis Under Cyclic Torsional Loading

- **Source:** Serdobolskaya 2002 torsional hysteresis
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1SBY-B41CwuTyqIdlr59Ogf23lIEkPwe1/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** O. Yu. Serdobolskaya, 2002
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Investigates the hysteresis of acoustic wave velocity in duralumin and brass specimens subjected to cyclic torsional loading. Shows that the velocity-stress curve forms a "butterfly" hysteresis loop and that velocity decreases monotonically with cycle count in the plastic region. Applies the Preisach-Mayergoytz (P-M) statistical hysteron model to describe the acoustic hysteresis.

## Unique contribution
Applies the P-M (Preisach-Mayergoytz) model — originally developed for magnetic hysteresis — to acoustic velocity hysteresis under mechanical loading. Demonstrates that the "butterfly" shape of the acoustic hysteresis loop is characteristic and reproducible, and that velocity decreases monotonically (accumulates) with cycle count in the plastic regime, providing a potential fatigue indicator.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Materials: duralumin (D16T, Al-Cu-Mg alloy) and brass (L63, Cu-Zn)
- Torsional loading: sinusoidal torque cycles at controlled amplitude; acoustic pulses transmitted axially through specimen
- Velocity vs. torsional stress: characteristic "butterfly" hysteresis loop (velocity changes sign of slope at stress extremes)
- In plastic region (stress > yield): velocity baseline shifts downward with each cycle (irreversible component)
- Velocity decrease is approximately linear with cycle count N in the early cycles
- P-M model: hysteresis described by distribution of "hysteron" units on Preisach triangle; each hysteron switches between two states at defined up/down thresholds
- The P-M distribution for acoustic hysteresis has a form consistent with dislocation unpinning/repinning statistics
- Practical implication: monotonic velocity decrease could be used as a damage accumulation indicator for fatigue monitoring
- Limitation: velocity change is very small (~10⁻⁴ per cycle) requiring high-precision measurement (similar to sing-around or pulse superposition methods)
