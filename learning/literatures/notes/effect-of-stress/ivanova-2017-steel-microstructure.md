# Effect of Microstructure on Rayleigh Wave Acoustoelastic Response in Steels

- **Source:** Ivanova 2017 steel deformation Rayleigh
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1mcNkWtj_t3zx2iw5PDEdeS_ItLSV8cLN/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Ivanova et al., 2017
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Investigates the influence of microstructural state (grain size, phase) on the acoustoelastic response of Rayleigh (surface) waves in steel specimens subjected to uniaxial tension. Five microstructural groups are compared: initial (as-received), recrystallized, heated to 950°C, heated to 1100°C, and quenched (martensite). Examines how plastic strain reduces Rayleigh wave velocity and how the acoustoelastic constant K changes with microstructure.

## Unique contribution
Systematically separates the effects of grain size and phase (ferrite/pearlite vs. martensite) on the acoustoelastic response of surface waves. Shows that the acoustoelastic constant K decreases with grain coarsening (lower sensitivity), and that the martensitic group exhibits the largest variation in velocity under stress, making it both the most sensitive and most variable microstructural state.

## Key concepts
- Rayleigh / surface waves
- Microstructure (grain size, phase)
- Acoustoelastic constant K
- Plastic strain effects on wave velocity
- Martensite vs. ferrite-pearlite microstructures
- Heat treatment effects

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Five microstructural groups prepared by different heat treatments from the same steel base
- Rayleigh wave velocity V₀ ≈ 2900 m/s for mild steel in initial state
- Velocity decreases progressively with increasing plastic strain for all groups
- Acoustoelastic constant K (ΔV/V₀ per unit stress): decreases as grain size increases due to coarsening at 1100°C
- Martensite (quenched) group: largest spread of K values; highest sensitivity to stress but also highest variability between specimens
- Recrystallized group: most uniform K; recommended for stress measurement applications requiring repeatability
- Grain coarsening (950°C → 1100°C heating) reduces K by ~15–20%
- Implication: microstructural state must be known for quantitative acoustoelastic stress measurement; coarse-grained or mixed-phase steels introduce significant uncertainty
