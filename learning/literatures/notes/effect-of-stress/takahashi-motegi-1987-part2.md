# Stress Dependency on Ultrasonic Wave Propagation Velocity — Part 2: TOE Constants of Carbon Steels

- **Source:** Takahashi 1987 Part 2 TOE constants
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1veOjo7L3WwK_eQILSdhFVzIIVKKVB4Vg/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** S. Takahashi & R. Motegi, 1987
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Experimental companion to Part 1. Measures the Murnaghan third-order elastic (TOE) constants for four carbon steels (S20C, S30C, S40C, S50C) using uniaxial tensile loading and ultrasonic velocity measurements. Applies both the Eulerian (Part 1) and Lagrangian (Hughes-Kelly) formulations to extract l, m, n and l', m', n' from velocity-stress data.

## Unique contribution
First systematic measurement of Murnaghan TOE constants for a series of plain carbon steels of different carbon content. Shows that TOE constants are roughly 10× larger in magnitude than SOE constants, and that the constant n varies systematically with carbon content while l and m are less sensitive.

## Key concepts
- Murnaghan TOE constants: l, m, n (Eulerian) and l', m', n' (Lagrangian)
- Carbon steel microstructure (S20C–S50C)
- Ultrasonic velocity vs. uniaxial stress measurement
- Acoustoelastic coefficients
- Lamé constants (SOE): λ, μ

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Specimens: S20C, S30C, S40C, S50C carbon steels (0.2–0.5 wt% C)
- Pulse-echo technique at ~5 MHz; longitudinal and shear waves measured as function of applied uniaxial stress up to ~200 MPa
- Measured Eulerian TOE constants (×10¹¹ Pa): l ≈ −8 to −9, m ≈ −5 to −6, n varies −3 to −7 depending on carbon content
- TOE constants are approximately 10× larger than SOE constants (λ ≈ 1.1×10¹¹, μ ≈ 0.8×10¹¹ Pa for steels)
- n shows the strongest carbon-content dependence; increases in magnitude with carbon content
- Conversion: l = 2(λ+2μ) + ½(l'+2m'), m = −4λ−12μ−2m', n = 12μ+n' (from Part 1)
- Lagrangian constants l', m', n' also tabulated for comparison with Hearmon and other prior work
- Practical implication: knowledge of TOE constants for specific steel grades enables quantitative stress measurement using Hughes-Kelly equations
