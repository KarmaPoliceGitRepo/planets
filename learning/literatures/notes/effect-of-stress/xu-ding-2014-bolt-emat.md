# Bolt Axial Stress Measurement Using EMAT and Mode-Converted Ultrasound

- **Source:** Xu Ding 2014 bolt EMAT
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1yc0lGkPa4Mrh5N8ryvjnrPXBgl5SUnqd/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Xu & Ding, 2014
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Presents a non-contact method for measuring bolt axial stress using an electromagnetic acoustic transducer (EMAT) that generates a mode-converted shear-to-longitudinal wave in the bolt head. The ratio k of the mode-converted to direct-path signal amplitudes is shown to correlate linearly with bolt axial tension, eliminating the need to know the exact bolt length.

## Unique contribution
EMAT-based mode conversion eliminates couplant and avoids the classical problem of bolt-length uncertainty in TOF methods. The k = T₁₃/T₁₂ amplitude ratio proves linearly proportional to bolt axial force F over the practical clamping range, with high R² and low measurement error (~5%).

## Key concepts
- EMAT (electromagnetic acoustic transducer)
- Mode-converted ultrasound (shear → longitudinal)
- Bolt axial stress / clamping force
- Time-of-flight (TOF) vs. amplitude ratio method
- Non-contact ultrasonic measurement
- Acoustoelastic effect in fasteners

## Methods / results / takeaways
- EMAT mounted on bolt head generates shear (T₁₂) wave and detects both direct shear echo (T₁₂) and mode-converted longitudinal echo (T₁₃) reflected from the bolt tip
- Ratio k = T₁₃/T₁₂ is used as the measurement quantity; avoids length-dependent TOF measurement
- Linear calibration: k = 5.8076×10⁻⁶F + 0.77658 (R² = 0.9799) where F is axial force in N
- Measurement range: 0 to ~60 kN axial force on M12 steel bolt
- No couplant needed; sensor can be applied to already-tightened bolt (in situ measurement)
- Temperature effects not fully characterized but noted as a source of uncertainty
- Compared to torque-based tightening: ultrasonic method gives direct axial force, avoiding friction uncertainty
- Maximum error: ~5% relative to reference load cell measurement
- Bolt steel: standard structural bolt (grade unknown but likely 8.8 or 10.9)
