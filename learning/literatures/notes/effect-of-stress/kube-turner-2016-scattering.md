# Stress-Dependent Ultrasonic Scattering in Polycrystalline Materials

- **Source:** Kube & Turner 2016 stress-dependent scattering
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/13BRgDczkKywLswJ7SUe5Hm8TtdSdwRgh/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Christopher M. Kube & Joseph A. Turner, 2016
- **Coverage:** partial (large file, truncated extraction)

## Overview
Develops a statistical model for the scattering of ultrasonic waves from randomly oriented grains in a stressed polycrystal. Uses stress-dependent elastic moduli (from acoustoelasticity theory) to define nine differential scattering cross-sections covering all combinations of incident and scattered longitudinal/shear wave modes. Analyzes aluminum under uniaxial stress to show that scattering amplitudes are far more sensitive to applied stress than phase velocities.

## Unique contribution
First comprehensive theory linking acoustoelastic (stress-dependent velocity) effects with stress-dependent grain scattering in polycrystals. Shows that amplitude-based scattering measurements can detect stress changes that would be invisible to phase-velocity measurements. Identifies optimal scattering configurations (propagation/polarization directions) for maximum stress sensitivity. Provides the theoretical basis for a new stress measurement technique using scattered wave amplitudes.

## Key concepts
- Stress-dependent scattering coefficients
- Differential scattering cross-section
- Acoustoelastic effect in polycrystals
- Grain boundary scattering
- Ultrasonic backscatter
- Nine scattering mode combinations (L→L, L→T1, L→T2, T1→L, T1→T1, etc.)
- Uniaxial stress sensitivity
- Texture vs. stress effects

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Model incorporates stress-dependent second-order grain statistics (from Kube & Turner 2015) into differential scattering cross-section via Born approximation for randomly textured polycrystals
- Nine scattering coefficients defined for L→L, L→T, T→L, T→T mode combinations
- Evaluated for polycrystalline aluminum under uniaxial stress at various propagation directions
- Key finding: scattering coefficients exhibit stress sensitivity 10–100× larger than phase velocity changes for the same stress level
- Sensitivity depends strongly on propagation/polarization direction of incident and scattered waves
- Frequency and grain size effects: sensitivity scales with ka (wavenumber × grain radius) in stochastic regime
- Dislocation effects are not included; model treats grain scattering only (explicitly decoupled from dislocation absorption)
- Future direction: experimental correlation of backscatter amplitude with applied/residual stress (less sensitive to dislocation behavior than attenuation or phase velocity)
