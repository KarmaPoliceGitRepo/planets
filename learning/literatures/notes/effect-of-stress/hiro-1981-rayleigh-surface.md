# Acoustoelastic Effect of Rayleigh Surface Waves Under Stress

- **Source:** Hiro 1981 Rayleigh surface wave
- **Drive link:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1CGItupF0PCEtGDkpkSGEYtwr8fqPBfxq/view
- **Type:** paper
- **Author/Year:** Hiro et al., 1981
- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Derives and experimentally validates expressions for the acoustoelastic effect on Rayleigh (surface) wave velocity under plane stress. Shows that surface waves respond to the in-plane biaxial stress state and become dispersive under non-uniform stress (e.g., bending). Provides the acoustic birefringence analogue for surface waves.

## Unique contribution
Extends acoustoelastic theory to surface waves, providing explicit dependence on both principal in-plane strains (e₁₁ and e₂₂). Demonstrates that Rayleigh waves become dispersive under depth-varying stress (e.g., bending), with the velocity shift depending on the product kH (wavenumber × depth scale). Identifies the depth-averaging effect that limits surface wave stress sensitivity for shallow stress gradients.

## Key concepts
- Rayleigh / surface waves
- Surface wave acoustoelastic effect
- Acoustic birefringence (surface wave analogue)
- Non-uniform / bending stress
- Depth-averaging of stress
- Dispersion under non-uniform stress
- kH dependence

## Methods / results / takeaways
- For uniform in-plane biaxial stress: ΔV/V₀ = A·e₁₁ + B·e₂₂ where e₁₁, e₂₂ are principal strains parallel and perpendicular to propagation direction
- For mild steel: V₀ = 3.00×10³ m/s; coefficients A = −0.31, B = −0.69
- Under non-uniform stress (bending), Rayleigh wave velocity depends on kH:
  ΔV/V₀ = (β₀ + β₁/kH)·e₁₁ with β₀ = −0.99, β₁ = 3.55 (where H = depth of neutral axis)
- The kH term represents the depth-averaging: at high kH (short wavelength), surface wave probes only near-surface stress; at low kH (long wavelength), probes deeper average
- For small kH (long wave): surface wave velocity approaches that of bulk wave, losing sensitivity to surface stress gradient
- Birefringence: two Rayleigh waves polarized ∥ and ⊥ to stress direction have different velocities, analogous to shear wave birefringence in bulk
- Practical implication for bending: two measurement frequencies needed to separate β₀ and β₁ terms and fully characterize bending-stress profile
