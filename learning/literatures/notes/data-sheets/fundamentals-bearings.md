## Overview
Chapter 10 of Slocum's FUNdaMENTALS of Design course notes covering all major bearing types used in precision machines: sliding contact, rolling element, flexural, externally pressurized fluid film, hydrodynamic, and magnetic bearings. Topics include preload, bearing mounting, load-lube-life calculations, dynamic seals, and error motions.

## Unique contribution
Slocum's treatment integrates fundamental physics with design principles (especially the reciprocity principle: "invert your design and compare alternatives"). The chapter maps each bearing type to its niche application domain and provides quantitative design guidelines. It is particularly strong on flexure bearings and precision machine design considerations.

## Key concepts
- Contact bearings (sliding, rolling, flexural)
- Non-contact bearings (hydrostatic, hydrodynamic, aerostatic, aerodynamic, magnetic)
- Preload and stiffness
- Hertzian contact stress
- L10 bearing life and dynamic load rating
- Angular contact bearings vs. deep groove bearings
- Back-to-back and face-to-face bearing mounting
- Contact angle
- Bearing lubrication (grease, oil, air)
- Error motions (axial, radial, tilt)
- Dynamic seals
- Principle of reciprocity

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Contact bearings offer high normal stiffness but are subject to wear or fatigue; non-contact bearings can offer near-infinite life but require external power.
- Preloading rolling element bearings eliminates dead-band (backlash) and increases stiffness, at the cost of increased heat generation and reduced life.
- For precision applications, angular contact and tapered roller bearings are preferred because they can be preloaded in a controlled way.
- Hydrodynamic bearings require a minimum speed to establish the lubricant film; hydrostatic bearings provide full film at all speeds including zero.
- Error motion budgeting is essential for precision machines: each bearing source (spindle, guide, etc.) contributes independently to total error.
