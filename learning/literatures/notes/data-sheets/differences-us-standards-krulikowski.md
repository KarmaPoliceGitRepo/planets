# Differences Between US Standards and Other Standards

- **Coverage:** full

## Overview
Chapter 6 of a GD&T textbook comparing ASME Y14.5M-1994 with ISO dimensioning standards and other national/government/corporate standards. Provides detailed comparison tables covering general rules, form, datums, orientation, tolerance of position, symmetry, concentricity, and profile tolerances, along with a discussion of the future of dimensioning standards.

## Unique contribution
Side-by-side comparison tables (Tables 6-6 through 6-13) covering over 30 specific areas where ASME and ISO differ in interpretation, symbolism, or approach. Also covers US government (MIL-STD-100G), national, and corporate supplemental standards. Predicts convergence of ASME and ISO into a single global standard driven by the global marketplace.

## Key concepts
- ASME Y14.5M-1994 vs. ISO standards
- Independency principle (ISO default) vs. envelope/Taylor principle (ASME Rule #1)
- GPS Masterplan (ISO TR 14638)
- First-angle vs. third-angle projection
- Feature control frame vs. tolerance frame
- Composite positional tolerancing
- Projected tolerance zone
- Simultaneous gaging requirement
- MIL-STD-100G (US Department of Defense)
- Corporate dimensioning standards and addenda

## Methods / results / takeaways
- ASME and ISO are estimated 60–70% common as of ~2002; predicted to reach 80–90% within 5 years.
- Key interpretive difference: ISO allows the Envelope Principle only when explicitly invoked (with encircled E symbol); ASME applies it by default (Rule #1).
- ISO uses a "general tolerances" note (ISO-2768) for unspecified dimensions; ASME has no equivalent default.
- ISO tolerance frame and datum specification allow omitting datum letters if the frame is directly connected by leader line.
- MIL-STD-100G references ASME Y14.5M-1994 for GD&T; other DoD drawing practices are self-contained.
- The authors advocate for a single global GD&T standard to enable international CAD/CMM interoperability.
- ASME's single-document approach is easier to specify and has consistent revision dates; ISO's multi-document structure allows faster topic-specific updates but creates consistency risks.
