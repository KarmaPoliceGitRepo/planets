## Overview
A licentiate thesis from KTH investigating the requirements for reliably predicting capability indices (Cp, Cpk) of machining systems through simulation. Defines a machining system as five modules (machine tool, tool, cutting process, workpiece, fixture) and explores what knowledge in statistics, machining accuracy, measurement, and modelling is needed to produce a reliable simulated capability value.

## Unique contribution
Frames the capability prediction problem as requiring four distinct knowledge areas that are rarely addressed together: (1) statistical capability methods, (2) machining accuracy mechanisms (thermal, geometric, static stiffness, dynamic stiffness), (3) measurement tools, and (4) modelling and simulation. Shows that current models cannot yet be integrated into a reliable end-to-end simulation because they lack compatible data structures and assumptions.

## Key concepts
- Capability indices (Cp, Cpk)
- Machining system modules (machine tool, tool, cutting process, workpiece, fixture)
- Machining accuracy categories: thermal influence, geometry/kinematics, static stiffness, dynamic stiffness
- Process simulation
- Statistical methods for capability studies
- DBB (Double Ball Bar) measurements

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Natural variation in machining is unavoidable; tolerances and capability indices manage it rather than eliminate it.
- Reliable capability prediction requires thorough planning, execution, and evaluation of capability studies — not just applying formulas.
- Temperature influence, geometric errors, static and dynamic stiffness each independently affect machining accuracy and must all be modeled.
- Current state: no model exists that integrates all four accuracy categories into a single reliable capability prediction.
- Collaboration with SCANIA CV AB provided industrial validation context.
- Thesis covers the state of knowledge up to 2002; detailed chapter content not fully extracted due to file size.
