## Overview
Chapter D4 of the Kalsi Seals Handbook covering the effects of shaft lateral displacement (dynamic runout, deflection, vibration, and axial motion) on rotary seal performance. Provides engineering guidance for minimizing these effects to maximize Kalsi-brand rotary seal life in demanding applications such as oilfield drilling tools.

## Unique contribution
Provides detailed, tested guidance on acceptable runout limits (routine lab tests use ≤0.002"/0.05 mm TIR), the mechanisms by which runout damages seals (extrusion, compression-relaxation fatigue, groove-wall wear), and multiple patented floating backup ring and seal carrier arrangements that isolate seals from shaft motion. Includes specific test data: a 950-hour test at 5,000 psi with 0.010" runout at 252 ft/min.

## Key concepts
- Dynamic runout (TIR/FIM)
- Total Indicator Reading (T.I.R.)
- Shaft deflection due to side load
- Static lateral misalignment
- Extrusion gap clearance
- Compression set
- Floating seal carrier arrangements
- Hydraulically force-balanced backup rings
- Stick-slip vibration in drilling
- Preloaded rolling element bearings
- Journal bearings for stiffness

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Runout out-of-round is more damaging than eccentric runout because it causes two compression-relaxation cycles per revolution.
- Sleeve running surfaces add to runout (slip fit eccentricity) and interrupt heat transfer; avoiding sleeves improves seal temperature and life.
- Less runout is always better; no bright-line limit is given because acceptable runout depends on speed, pressure, temperature, and seal type.
- Shaft deflection can be minimized by spacing bearings adequately, positioning radial loads near bearings, and maximizing shaft stiffness.
- Preloaded angular contact and tapered roller bearings minimize internal clearance and therefore runout.
- Axial motion causes abrasive ingestion; thrust bearing preloading and wide-lip seals help mitigate this.
- Rotating-housing applications are less favorable than rotating-shaft configurations due to once-per-revolution gland dimension cycling.
