## Overview
A technical data sheet for Precision Acoustics' PVDF student kit: three pieces of uniaxially poled PVDF film (110, 52, and 28 µm thick) with gold-on-chrome electrodes. Covers basic material properties, usage guidelines, electrode attachment methods, and handling precautions.

## Unique contribution
Concise practical reference for PVDF as a transducer material, with emphasis on laboratory handling: how to cut without creating shorting filaments, why soldering is prohibited, and how to make reliable electrical contacts using conductive adhesives or mechanical clamping rather than solder.

## Key concepts
- PVDF (polyvinylidene fluoride) piezoelectric polymer
- Uniaxial poling
- Piezoelectric charge coefficients (d31, d32, d33)
- Curie temperature and maximum usable temperature
- Coercive and breakdown field strengths
- Conductive adhesive bonding (Henkel Loctite Abelstik 56C/64C)
- Cyanoacrylate adhesive compatibility
- Mechanical contact electrode attachment
- Quarter/half-wave thickness resonance

## Methods / results / takeaways
- Longitudinal wave speed: 2260 m/s; shear wave speed: 1410 m/s; density: 1780 kg/m³.
- Melting temperature: 175–180°C; Curie temperature: 205°C; maximum usable: 70–80°C.
- d31 = 22 pC/N, d32 = 3 pC/N, d33 = −30 pC/N; relative dielectric constant: 10–12.
- Coercive field: 50–80 MV/m; breakdown field: 200 MV/m.
- Cutting with a blunt edge creates shorting PVDF filaments — always use a sharp scalpel.
- Soldering is prohibited: heat above 80°C causes irreversible degradation of piezoelectric properties.
- Low-viscosity epoxy or nitrile contact adhesive recommended for bonding to substrates.
