## Overview
A technical whitepaper from Master Bond explaining the glass transition temperature (Tg) concept for epoxy adhesives: what it is, how it is measured by three different methods (DSC, TMA, DMA), and how it affects the selection of adhesives for temperature-critical applications.

## Unique contribution
Provides a practitioner-oriented comparison of DSC, TMA, and DMA measurement methods for Tg, noting that results can differ by 5–30°C depending on method. Highlights the exceptional EP36 series B-staged epoxies, which combine a low Tg (35–40°C) with a service temperature up to 500°F (260°C) — an exception to the usual rule that higher Tg means better heat resistance.

## Methods / results / takeaways
- As temperature rises through Tg, CTE increases 3–5× compared to the value below Tg.
- DSC is cheapest but least sensitive; TMA is preferred for filled systems; DMA is most accurate but expensive and complex.
- TMA is recommended as the best balance of accuracy, practicality, and cost.
- For silicones, Tg is not a useful indicator of service temperature — these have very low Tg (–40 to –100°C) yet serve up to 250°C; resistance is gauged by elongation retention.
- Brief excursions above Tg are generally recoverable once the material returns to ambient temperature.
- High Tg epoxies tend to be rigid; flexible (lower Tg) epoxies may be preferable where thermal cycling is rapid.
- When reporting Tg, the measurement method must be specified to allow meaningful comparison.
