# Cast Iron and Steel Microstructure
**Aliases:** gray cast iron; compacted graphite iron; CGI; spheroidal graphite iron; SGI; ductile iron; nodularity; pearlite/ferrite ratio; bainite; martensite; austenite; pearlite; phase transformation; prior austenite grain size; PAGS; microstructure; heat treatment; quenching; tempering; annealing; Compacted graphite iron; grey iron; white iron

**Definition:** Cast irons are iron-carbon alloys (>2 wt% C) whose properties are dominated by graphite morphology: flake (gray iron, brittle, high damping and thermal conductivity), compacted/vermicular (CGI, intermediate), and spheroidal (SGI/ductile iron, high toughness). Nodularity (% spheroidal graphite) and the pearlite/ferrite matrix ratio together control mechanical properties and CTE. In steels, austenite transforms on cooling to pearlite (slow), bainite (intermediate), or martensite (fast quench). Prior austenite grain size (PAGS) controls hardenability and final microstructure length scales; martensite packet size is ~1/10 of PAGS. Microstructure affects ultrasonic velocity and attenuation through changes in elastic constants, grain size, and graphite content.
