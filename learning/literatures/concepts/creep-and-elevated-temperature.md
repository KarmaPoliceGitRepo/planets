# Creep and Elevated-Temperature Behaviour
**Aliases:** creep; power-law creep; Norton creep law; tertiary creep; stress relaxation; dynamic strain aging; Portevin-Le Chatelier effect; DSA; elevated temperature properties; high-temperature strength; hot tensile test; Gleeble tester; Ramberg-Osgood; proof strength at temperature

**Definition:** Creep is time-dependent plastic deformation under sustained stress, significant above approximately 0.4 Tm (homologous temperature). Primary creep decelerates (transient strain hardening); secondary creep is steady-state (power-law creep: dε/dt = Aσ^n); tertiary creep accelerates to fracture. Stress relaxation is the time-dependent stress drop under constant strain. Dynamic strain aging (DSA, Portevin-Le Chatelier effect) occurs when diffusing solute atoms pin dislocations at intermediate temperatures, causing serrations in the stress-strain curve and a non-monotonic ductility minimum. At elevated temperatures, proof strength f₀.₂ and Young's modulus both decline; Eurocode 9 tabulates reduction factors for aluminium alloys in fire design.
