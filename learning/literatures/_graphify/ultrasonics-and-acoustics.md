# Graphify input — Ultrasonics & Acoustics (literature corpus)

## Overview
This corpus covers the theory and application of ultrasonic and acoustic wave methods for non-destructive evaluation, tribological measurement, and materials characterisation. It spans wave propagation fundamentals (bulk, surface, guided, and nonlinear waves), transducer technologies (piezoelectric, EMAT, laser, air-coupled), modelling frameworks (transfer-matrix, Biot, CFD/CAA), and specific measurement domains: wear, film thickness, contact stiffness, range/velocity, and stress. The literature runs from foundational 1950s–1970s theory to 2017 experimental studies, with strong coverage of contact-mechanics applications originating from the Sheffield/Tribosonics research cluster.

## Entities & Relationships

- **Spring interface model (Baik-Thompson 1984)** —underpins→ **contact stiffness / film-thickness measurement** : quasi-static long-wavelength approximation treating a thin layer or partial contact as a distributed spring K; reflection coefficient R = (iωZ/2K)/(1+iωZ/2K); foundational equation used across the entire contact/lubrication cluster.
- **Reflection coefficient R** —encodes→ **interfacial stiffness K** : R depends on frequency, acoustic impedance Z, and K; measuring R(f) at multiple frequencies yields frequency-independent K, validating the spring model (Drinkwater 1996 broadband 4–17 MHz validation).
- **Interfacial stiffness K** —linear in→ **contact pressure P** : K = K₀ + b·P confirmed across steel contacts at 0–1000 MPa (Krolikowski 1989, 1991); BGT rough-surface contact model is closest match but still underpredicts by 10–40%.
- **Ultrasonic film-thickness measurement** —uses three regimes→ **ToF / resonance / spring model** : ToF method applies for h > 40 µm; resonance method for 10–40 µm; spring model for ~2 nm to ~25 µm (at 60 MHz); method selection depends on film thickness vs. wavelength (Dwyer-Joyce 2003).
- **EHD lubricant speed of sound** —rises dramatically with pressure→ **bulk modulus / Jacobson-Vinet model** : speed rises from ~1450 m/s at ambient to ~3550 m/s at 0.8 GPa; must be corrected via Jacobson-Vinet equation of state for accurate film-thickness calculation.
- **Thermal expansion / acoustic velocity temperature dependence** —causes→ **systematic TOF error in wear measurement** : temperature change of 1°C ≈ 1 MPa worth of acoustoelastic delay shift; Brunskill (2015) reference-notch method and FFT resonant-dip method both eliminate this error without explicit temperature measurement.
- **Rayleigh wave** —penetration depth ∝→ **wavelength** : displacement amplitude decays to ~10% of surface value at one wavelength depth; longitudinal component penetrates ~0.18λ, transverse component ~0.43λ (Soczkiewicz 1997 tabulated for all Poisson ratios).
- **Lamb wave attenuation (A₀/S₀)** —separately sensitive to→ **normal stiffness KN / shear stiffness KT** : A₀ at 300 kHz tracks KN (out-of-plane contact); S₀ at 120 kHz tracks KT (in-plane contact); only combination of both modes gives both stiffness components (Drinkwater 2003).
- **Rayleigh wave at wedge tip** —undergoes→ **mode conversion to Lamb A₀/S₀** : as frequency-thickness product (f·d) decreases toward tip, Rayleigh wave progressively converts to A₀ then S₀; out-of-plane enhancement can exceed 40× for shallow-angle defects (Edwards 2011).
- **Transfer matrix (Thomson-Haskell) method** —unstable at→ **high frequency-thickness products** : exponentially growing/decaying partial-wave terms mix; Global Matrix method avoids instability by assembling all layer equations simultaneously (Lowe 1995, canonical review).
- **Laser ultrasonics (thermoelastic regime)** —generates→ **dipole source directivity** : principal stresses parallel to free surface; shear and surface waves strong; compressional lobe at ~50–60° to surface normal; dipole model fails at θ = 0° (Krylov); ablation regime produces broad normal compressional lobe.
- **Picosecond ultrasonics (pump-probe)** —enables→ **nanometre thin-film velocity measurement** : GHz–THz acoustic pulses via femtosecond lasers; superlattice stacking + Rytov dispersion relation extracts individual layer velocities down to 2–5 nm (Pu & Li 2006).
- **EMAT** —generates Rayleigh waves via→ **Lorentz force on eddy currents** : meander-line coil pitch set to half Rayleigh wavelength for constructive interference; non-contact but efficiency drops with lift-off; purely analytical simulation possible via Dodd-Deeds + large-radius extrapolation (Xie et al. 2016).
- **Air-coupled transducer** —requires→ **quarter-wavelength impedance matching layer** : PZT impedance ~35 MRayl vs. air ~400 Rayl; optimal single-layer impedance ~0.11 MRayl (geometric mean); attenuation per wavelength γ < 0.14 Np required; polyethersulfone membranes best practical choice (Gómez 2004).
- **Acoustoelastic effect** —enables→ **non-contact stress / pressure measurement** : wave velocity changes with stress; combining Rayleigh + LCR waves on same volume cancels temperature effect; validated on steel pressure vessels ±0.07 MPa (Ling 2009).
- **Acoustic nonlinearity parameter β** —correlates with→ **surface micro-damage / plastic strain** : second harmonic amplitude A₂ ∝ β·A₁²·propagation distance; β'/β'₀ increases monotonically with tensile plastic strain in Al 6061-T6; enables early-stage damage detection (Jhang 2012).
- **Cross-correlation TOF** —lowest random error of→ **TOF estimation methods** : outperforms phase-slope and overlap methods, especially for attenuating or dispersive materials (Hull 1985); couplant layer must exceed ~80–90 µm to avoid interference-induced distortion (Janssen 1995).
- **Diffraction (near-field) effects** —cause→ **systematic TOF bias in precision measurements** : ignored diffraction corrections introduce systematic velocity overestimate; Papadakis (1967) analytic corrections reduce standard deviation by ~30%; for USMs, flow calibration can compensate volumetric flow errors but NOT velocity-of-sound errors (Lunde 2003).
- **STAMINA continuous-wave method** —amplifies sensitivity by→ **number of reflections n** : each standing-wave peak shifts by n·Δf; at 10 MHz on 100 mm bolt n ≈ 339, giving ~34 kHz apparent shift per 100 Hz Δf — micron elongation resolution impossible with single-pulse methods.
- **Biot theory** —predicts→ **fast and slow compressional waves in porous media** : fast wave (frame and fluid in phase, less attenuated); slow wave (out of phase, highly attenuated at low frequency and rarely detected experimentally); global transmission coefficient sums all fast/slow conversions via matrix recurrence (Nassar 2013).
- **Near-field distance N = D²f/(4c) = D²/4λ** —separates→ **Fresnel and Fraunhofer zones** : actual beam regularity transition occurs at ~0.75 N; maximum focal length for transducer focusing = 0.8 N; at least 5 mesh points per wavelength required for accurate near-field numerical simulation.
- **Magnetoacoustic effect (Alers-Fleury 1963)** —follows→ **H² dependence for most metals** : ΔC/C ∝ H²sin²θ (longitudinal) / H²cos²θ (shear); breaks down to linear H dependence in high-purity Cu at 4.2 K along open-orbit Fermi surface directions.

## Glossary

- **acoustic impedance (Z = ρc)** — product of material density and sound speed; governs reflection/transmission at interfaces; determines baseline R from a free surface (R = +1 rigid, −1 stress-free).
- **interfacial stiffness (K)** — stiffness per unit area of a partially-contacting or thin-layer interface; extracted from ultrasonic reflection coefficient at long wavelengths via the spring model; units N/m³.
- **reflection coefficient (R)** — ratio of reflected to incident ultrasonic amplitude; R = 0 for transparent interface, R = 1 for perfect reflector; used to infer K, film thickness, or contact area.
- **time-of-flight (TOF, transit time)** — elapsed time for an ultrasonic pulse to travel between two points; basis for thickness (d = vt/2) and velocity measurement; corrupted by diffraction, couplant, and temperature.
- **Rayleigh wave (SAW, surface acoustic wave)** — elastic wave propagating along a free surface with retrograde elliptical particle motion and amplitude decaying exponentially with depth; non-dispersive on a uniform half-space; velocity cR ≈ 0.92c_shear.
- **Lamb wave (guided plate wave)** — guided elastic wave in a traction-free plate with symmetric (Sₙ) and antisymmetric (Aₙ) mode families; all modes dispersive, governed by the frequency-thickness product (f·d).
- **frequency-thickness product (f·d)** — single parameter governing Lamb wave dispersion; modes and phase velocities are functions of f·d only, enabling scaling between frequency and plate thickness.
- **spring model (Tattersall / Baik-Thompson model)** — long-wavelength approximation treating a thin interface (contact, oil film, adhesive) as a distributed spring of stiffness K; valid when acoustic wavelength >> interface layer thickness (h < λ/10 rule of thumb).
- **acoustoelastic effect** — dependence of ultrasonic velocity on applied or residual mechanical stress; basis for non-invasive stress measurement via wave transit-time changes.
- **thermoelastic regime (laser ultrasonics)** — laser intensity below ablation threshold; thermal expansion launches acoustic waves without surface damage; generates dipole (horizontal force pair) source directivity with oblique compressional lobe and strong shear/surface waves.
- **EHD / elastohydrodynamic lubrication** — lubrication regime in highly-loaded non-conformal contacts where elastic deformation and pressure-dependent viscosity both influence film thickness; characterised by Dowson-Higginson film thickness equation.
- **transfer matrix method (Thomson-Haskell propagator)** — technique representing each layer by a matrix relating boundary quantities at its two surfaces; matrices are multiplied across all layers; numerically unstable at high f·d; superseded by Global Matrix method.
- **near-field distance (N = D²/4λ)** — characteristic distance separating complex near field from regularly diverging far field; actual beam regularity transition at ~0.75 N; maximum usable focal length for transducer focusing is 0.8 N.
- **phase-comparison technique (ultrasonic interferometry)** — CW frequency is adjusted to maintain fixed phase with returning echo; theoretical wear resolution 0.015 µm at 10 MHz; practical accuracy limited to ~0.75 µm by temperature and vibration (Birring-Kwun 1989).
- **STAMINA method** — continuous-wave frequency-swept standing-wave technique; amplitude and frequency of standing-wave peaks encode interface stiffness, film thickness, viscosity, or elongation; sensitivity amplified by number of reflections n.
- **EMAT (electromagnetic acoustic transducer)** — non-contact transducer using Lorentz force or magnetostriction on eddy currents to generate/detect ultrasound; requires electrically conducting target; efficiency drops with lift-off.
- **picosecond ultrasonics** — pump-probe optical technique using femtosecond laser pulses to generate and detect GHz–THz acoustic pulses; enables velocity measurement in films as thin as 2–5 nm.
- **acoustic nonlinearity parameter (β = B/A)** — material property quantifying quadratic nonlinearity; second harmonic amplitude A₂ ∝ β·k²·A₁²·distance; sensitive to dislocation density, micro-damage, and plastic deformation.
- **Biot fast/slow waves** — two compressional modes in fluid-saturated porous media; fast wave has frame and fluid in phase (less attenuated); slow wave has them out of phase (highly attenuated at low frequency, rarely detected directly).

## Key findings

- Thermal effects dwarf wear signal in many ultrasonic wear tests: a 30-minute tribometer test can produce 12 ns TOF shift from temperature alone, comparable to or larger than the wear signal; reference-notch or resonant-dip methods eliminate this without thermometry.
- The spring model R = (iωZ/2K)/(1+iωZ/2K) is the workhorse for contact and film-thickness measurement but fails when film thickness approaches λ/10; continuum transfer-matrix model must be used for thicker films and near resonance.
- Ultrasonic film-thickness measurement spans >6 decades of range (2 nm to >40 µm) by combining three methods: spring model (<25 µm), resonance/FFT (10–40 µm), ToF (>40 µm).
- In EHD contacts the lubricant speed of sound can increase 2–4× (from ~1450 m/s at ambient to ~3550 m/s at 0.8 GPa); ignoring this gives large film-thickness errors.
- Rayleigh wave amplitude decays to ~10% of surface value within one wavelength depth; penetration depth scales with wavelength, so frequency selection directly controls the probed depth in surface characterisation and coating inspection.
- Near-field (Fresnel) effects cause systematic velocity overestimate in precision pulse-echo measurements; analytic diffraction corrections (Papadakis 1967) reduce standard deviation by ~30% and are critical for flow meter accuracy.
- Cross-correlation is the most robust TOF estimator: it outperforms phase-slope and peak-overlap methods for attenuating materials (composites, polymer melts); couplant layer must exceed ~80–90 µm critical thickness to avoid interference artefacts.
- Temperature compensation is non-negotiable for guided-wave SHM: even ±1°C fluctuation makes simple subtraction impractical; optimal baseline selection from a temperature-indexed library provides ~30 dB suppression of residual signals (Croxford 2007).
- The Global Matrix method is numerically stable for multilayer dispersion computation at all frequency-thickness products; Transfer Matrix (Thomson-Haskell) becomes unstable and should be avoided at high f·d.
- Laser ultrasonics achieves 0.02% precision for elastic constant measurement using cross-correlation of echoes plus diffraction correction; accuracy comparable to contact methods and resonance, but applicable to hot, rough, or remote surfaces.
- Air-coupled transducers require matching layer attenuation γ < 0.14 Np per wavelength; polyethersulfone filtration membranes are the best practical single-layer solution covering 0.3–2.5 MHz; silica aerogel has ideal properties but cannot be formed into thin planar layers above ~1 MHz.
- A₀ and S₀ Lamb wave attenuation give independent access to normal (KN) and shear (KT) interfacial stiffness — the only practical route to measuring both stiffnesses of a solid–solid contact (Drinkwater 2003).
- For EMAT simulation, the Dodd-Deeds analytical solution adapted via large-radius extrapolation allows fully analytical meander-coil field computation, avoiding FEM discretisation errors that become significant at >1 MHz.
- Acoustic nonlinearity (second harmonic parameter β') increases monotonically with accumulated plastic strain and correlates with dislocation density, enabling early-stage surface fatigue detection before macroscopic damage is visible.
- Ultrasonic contact stiffness K is proportional to contact pressure P (K = K₀ + b·P, R² > 0.99); the BGT rough-surface model best matches measurements but still underestimates K by 10–40%; GW model is a poor predictor for ground surfaces.

## Gotchas

- **Parallel-face requirement for TOF wear measurement**: if the wear face develops a wedge angle (non-parallel to transducer), TOF gives incorrect average thickness; the method fails for contacts with non-uniform wear (e.g., off-axis sliding).
- **Temperature compensation is not optional**: both acoustic velocity and thermal expansion change with temperature; 1°C ≈ 1 MPa worth of transit-time shift in acoustoelastic pressure measurement; every precision TOF system needs either active temperature control or a differential reference path.
- **Real contact area vs. stiffness ambiguity**: ultrasonic stiffness K depends on the linear dimension of contacts, not their area (Kendall-Tabor 1971); the same total contact area arranged as fewer large contacts vs. many small ones gives different K — stiffness and true contact area cannot be separated without knowing the spatial contact distribution.
- **Spring model breaks down near resonance**: R(f) from a thin layer is well-described by the spring model only for f < ½ f_resonance; for thicker films or higher frequencies, the full continuum transfer-matrix model must be used or large errors result.
- **EHD lubricant bulk modulus must be corrected for pressure**: ambient-condition acoustic properties cannot be used for film-thickness calculation in high-pressure EHD contacts; the Jacobson-Vinet equation of state must supply the in-contact bulk modulus.
- **Coupling layer thickness control is critical**: couplant layer below ~80–90 µm causes multiple internal reflections that distort waveforms and produce large non-monotonic TOF errors; precision measurements require controlled couplant thickness above this threshold.
- **Diffraction corrections differ for laser vs. piston sources**: diffraction velocity bias for a laser thermoelastic source uses a dipolar tangential-stress formula, not the standard piston-source formula; applying the wrong correction systematically biases elastic constant results.
- **Air has no measurable effect on contact acoustics at NDT pressures**: evacuating air from a contact (0.1 kPa) produces no change in R — confirming that air gap effects are negligible and that the spring model's assumption of zero air contribution is valid.
- **sol-gel sprayed HTUT transducers risk barrel microstructure damage**: high-temperature oven processing required for BIT/PZT film deposition may alter the nitrided surface of extruder barrels; buffer-rod sensors are less invasive for production equipment.
- **Dipole source model for laser ultrasonics fails at normal direction**: at θ = 0° the directly radiated and surface-reflected contributions nearly cancel; the residual is kT-dependent and sensitive to surface modifications — this is a limitation for calibration but an opportunity for surface characterisation.
- **Bessel (limited-diffraction) beams have higher sidelobes than focused beams**: the lateral resolution–depth-of-field trade-off is inescapable; Bessel beams offer superior depth of field but first sidelobe is only ~8 dB below main lobe vs. ~18 dB for a focused beam.
- **Transfer Matrix instability at high f·d**: numerical overflow/underflow occurs when exponentially growing and decaying partial-wave terms are explicitly multiplied; the Global Matrix method must be used for thick multilayer structures or high-frequency analyses.
